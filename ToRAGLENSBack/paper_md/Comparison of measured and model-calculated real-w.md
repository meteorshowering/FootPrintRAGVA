# Abstract

The quality of an emission calculation model based on emission factors measured on roller test stands and statistical traffic data was evaluated using source strengths and emission factors calculated from real-world exhaust gas concentration differences measured upwind and downwind of a motorway in southwest Germany. Gaseous and particulate emissions were taken into account. Detailed traffic census data were taken during the measurements. The results were compared with findings of similar studies.

The main conclusion is the underestimation of CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  source strengths by the model. On the average, it amounts to  $23\%$  in case of CO and  $17\%$  for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ . The latter underestimation results from an undervaluation by  $22\%$  of  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  emission factors of heavy-duty vehicles (HDVs). There are significant differences between source strengths on working days and weekends because of the different traffic split between light-duty vehicles (LDVs) and HDVs. The mean emission factors of all vehicles from measurements are  $1.08\mathrm{g}\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  and  $2.62\mathrm{g}\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  for CO. The model calculations give  $0.92\mathrm{g}\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  and  $2.14\mathrm{g}\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  for CO.

The source strengths of 21 non-methane hydrocarbon (NMHC) compounds quantified are underestimated by the model. The ratio between the measured and model-calculated emissions ranges from 1.3 to 2.1 for BTX and up to 21 for 16 other NMHCs. The reason for the differences is the insufficient knowledge of NMHC emissions of road traffic.

Particulate matter emissions are dominated by ultra-fine particles in the  $10 - 40\mathrm{nm}$  range. As far as aerosols larger than  $29\mathrm{nm}$  are concerned,  $1.80\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  are determined for all vehicles,  $1.22\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  and an aerosol volume of  $0.03\mathrm{cm}^3\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  are measured for LDVs, and for HDVs

$7.79\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  and  $0.41\mathrm{cm}^3\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  are calculated. Traffic-induced turbulence has been identified to have a decisive influence on exhaust gas dispersion near the source. © 2005 Published by Elsevier Ltd.

# 1. Introduction

# 1.1. Scope of the paper

To ensure the efficiency of strategies to reduce air pollutants caused by road traffic, it is essential to evaluate the emission data to be used in emission calculation models (Kuhlwein and Friedrich, 2000). Under the BAB II project (BundesAutoBahn—federal highway), this was done by simultaneous measurements of concentrations of gaseous and particulate emissions in ambient air on the upwind and downwind side perpendicular to a motorway under real-world conditions. From the differences of the measured fluxes, the emissions caused by traffic on the motorway can be calculated. Measurements of high horizontal and vertical resolution of CO, NO,  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , 21 non-methane hydrocarbon (NMHC) compounds, and particulates were made on both sides of the motorway.

The scope of this final paper within the special issue on the results of the BAB II project is (i) to summarise the main results, (ii) to compare the measured and model-calculated source strengths of emissions, released by traffic on the motorway section under investigation and finally to evaluate the quality of the emission calculation model updated by the results of the traffic census made simultaneously. Although emission factors for a "mean vehicle" depending on the fleet composition passing the measurement site are given, the main objective is the improvement of the emission model by reliable traffic data and not by updated emission factors. These can not be estimated by the method used in BAB II, because the model needs emission factors of individual vehicle types (motors) as input data rather than emission factors valid for a fleet of vehicles passing the site randomly distributed. The remaining differences between measured and model-calculated source strengths is therefore mainly attributed to imprecise or even missing emission factors for gases, NMHC compounds and particulates from roller test stands.

# 1.2. State of the art

Former emission measurements of gaseous and particulate compounds and subsequent emission factor calculations were often restricted to tunnel studies

(Pierson et al., 1996; Weingartner et al., 1997; John et al., 1999; Schmid et al., 2001; Gomes et al., 2004). The advantage of the tunnel studies is that they are dealing with well-defined volumes of air. However, a major problem of all one-way tunnels is the airflow of up to  $30\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$  in the tunnel tube. Consequently, the driving resistance of the passenger cars changes and, thus, the emission behaviour no longer corresponds to that of vehicles operated in free air.

Zhang et al. (1995) used a remote sensing technique to determine the relative CO and VOC emissions of about 1 million cars at 22 different locations all over the world. The study shows that a small percentage of cars caused  $50\%$  of the total emissions of CO ("high emitters"). De Vlieger (1997) measured the tailpipe emissions of 7 cars under different driving cycles using an on-board measuring system. Major findings were the importance of the cold-start emissions and differences between emissions measured in chassis dynamometer tests and real traffic situations. The advantage of on-board measurements is that errors due to the necessary flux measurements in the tunnel and in free air studies are avoided. An alternative to the on-board measurements are emission measurements made on dynamic roller test stands. Although such emission factors estimated for typical driving cycles are valuable emission model input data, they are available for a very limited number of motors and driving cycles only.

The efficiency of a controlled 3-way catalytic converter in a passenger car and cold-start emissions with respect to benzene, toluene and xylene (BTX) emissions in particular was investigated by Heeb et al. (2000, 2003). Free air measurements were made by Leisen et al. (1992) on a motorway in Germany and by Kittelson et al. (2004) in the US. In the former BAB 656 study by Vogel et al. (2000), model-calculated emissions using real-world concentration differences between the lee and the windward side of the motorway were evaluated. The deficiencies of BAB 656 consisted in the estimation of the CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  plume height using  $\mathrm{O}_3$  as indicator and in comparing measured and calculated emissions for three 1-h intervals only. As there are indications of traffic-induced turbulence being important to the dispersion of emissions close to the source (Eskridge and Rao, 1983; Rao et al., 2002), additionally horizontal and vertical profiles of turbulence were measured in BAB II.

# 1.3. Shortcomings of emission models

Emission calculation models are frequently used to determine input data for numerical modelling of summer smog events or climate change scenarios. Model-calculated emission estimates are necessary for environmental risk assessments and for settling many questions related to public health. Although significant progress was achieved in emission modelling during the last decade, there are still some serious shortcomings of the emission models, which constrict the usage of the models or limit the results of the emission calculations. This means that quantitative information about the quality of model-calculated emissions based on general traffic databases and roller test stand investigations of selected types of motors is rather poor. Especially the transferability of emission factors investigated in the laboratory to real-world traffic conditions seems to be problematic in many cases. The same applies to the model assumptions regarding the composition of the vehicle fleet, the driving pattern of individual cars, and the daily and weekly cycles of traffic load on different types of roads. Information about gaseous emissions is lacking and knowledge of particulate emissions is even poorer. Size distributions and masses of particulate matter (PM) emitted by vehicles are unknown both near the source as well as further downstream. Several attempts using different concepts have been made in the past to overcome the shortcomings mentioned. Within BAB II, a mass balance concept under real-world traffic and atmospheric conditions was investigated to evaluate the results of an emission model. Evaluated emission models are useful tools for sophisticated emission calculations and forecasts. Utilizing such tools, the efficiency of future emission reduction strategies can be determined, consequences of new traffic routes for the residents can be calculated, and reliable ozone predictions can be made, among other applications.

# 1.4. The BAB II concept

This concept is based on the measurement of concentrations on the windward and the lee side of a motorway and on the assumption of homogeneous conditions for all parameters along the respective road section (Vogel et al., 2000; Corsmeier et al., 2005). In this case, source strengths of gaseous and particulate vehicle emissions can be determined by measuring the vertical profiles of the concentrations of the substances and the horizontal wind speed over the height of the plume of the specific compound on the lee side of the motorway, as well as by the respective windward profiles.

The BAB II concept includes symmetric, continuous profile measurements of NO,  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , CO,  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , and

numerous NMHC compounds as well as of PM on both sides of the motorway and measurements of meteorological parameters. A detailed traffic census, taking into account the different layers of vehicles, was carried out. Using an emission calculation model, statistical traffic data, and traffic census results, the calculated emissions are finally evaluated against the emissions measured. Additionally, the traffic census was used to check whether the predictions made by the Umweltbundesamt, UBA (German Environmental Protection Agency) in 1997 with respect to the degree of exhaust gas reduction technologies used in the vehicle fleet had come true (UBA, 1997).

As this paper summarises the BAB II results and compares measured and model calculated emissions, for detailed information about the determination of source strengths, traffic census data and model development look up the specific contributions within this special issue. For the overall project description see Corsmeier et al. (2005). The source strengths for CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  are given by Kohler et al. (2005), those for NMHC compounds by Petrea et al. (2005). PM emissions are investigated by Rosenbohm et al. (2005) and Imhof et al. (2005) and finally the emission model calculations and the traffic measurements are presented by Kuhlwein and Friedrich (2005).

# 2. BAB II partial results

In cases of wind directions perpendicular to the motorway, significant differences in exhaust gas concentrations and particle concentrations can be measured between the windward side and the lee side of the motorway. When making an appropriate traffic census and a stringent quality control for the instruments, the method for detecting real-world vehicle emissions by calculating concentration differences works quite well, if essential prerequisites are fulfilled, as listed below:

- homogeneous flow perpendicular to the motorway;  
- flat motorway and flat surrounding terrain, no flow obstacles;  
- high and constant traffic density on the motorway;  
- no secondary emission sources nearby;  
- field measurements of sufficient time length;  
- a high-level emission calculation model, including detailed traffic data.

The measured emissions and the emissions calculated by the model are compared in this paper. Irrespective of the results of this comparison, however, the BAB II study is much more elaborate in comparison to tunnel studies. The circumstances under which the emissions are calculated are more realistic than in a tunnel. On the

other hand, the uncertainties of the measurements may be greater in case of the open motorway, if the prerequisites for using the method are not fulfilled.

# 2.1. Quality assurance procedure

The project was accompanied by a series of quality assurance (QA) measures, because the calculated upwind-downwind differences are vulnerable to measurement errors. Consequently, simultaneous ambient air measurements of NO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , CO,  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , NMHCs, elemental carbon (EC), and PM (mass and number concentration)—were made by all analysers. The NO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , CO, and NMHC analysers were additionally exposed to certified calibration gases. The quality assurance programme had two main objectives: on the one hand, the harmonisation of the different analysers by application of individual correction equations or factors and, on the other hand, the calculation of the remaining measurement uncertainty (Vogt et al., 2005).

By the QA measures, deviations between the individual analysers could be reduced significantly and the remaining uncertainties were determined. For the components of NO,  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , CO,  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , EC, and the PM fractions  $\mathrm{PM}_1$ , and  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ , measurement uncertainty at high concentrations is low, i.e. between  $3\%$  and  $10\%$ . The upwind-downwind differences measured for the respective species are much higher than the measurement uncertainty. Hence, the emissions calculated from measurements are definitely those of the motorway. Fig. 1 shows an example of the QA results: The harmonisation of  $8\mathrm{NO}_x$  monitors leads to a significant

reduction of the variance of the individual measurement results under comparison conditions. The NMHC uncertainties are on a higher level, between  $10\%$  and  $30\%$ . Some NMHC components with high measurement uncertainties will have to be used carefully in further evaluations, especially when upwind-downwind differences are small (Vogt et al., 2005).

All particle-counting devices exhibit differences in measurement accuracy depending on the particle size investigated. The scanning mobility particle sizer (SMPS) as well as the optical particle counter (OPC) devices show higher deviations in larger particle size ranges. Measurement uncertainties were generally higher for the particle-counting devices than for the gas monitors. According to Rosenbohm et al. (2005) and Imhof et al. (2005), the upwind and downwind particle number concentration differences in the BAB II field experiment exceed the standard deviation by factors of 2-3 in general and consequently can be used for budget calculations.

# 2.4. Particulate matter

Horizontal and vertical aerosol distributions were measured on both sides of the motorway to characterise the particulate traffic emissions. The instruments worked according to the BAB quality assurance guidelines. The background particle size distributions and particle mass concentrations were highly variable according to the weather conditions. By simultaneous upwind and downwind measurements, the PM originating from the road and the vehicles could clearly be estimated.

For 14 May 2001 the average upwind-downwind  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  difference was found to be about  $5\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$  and the average downwind concentration was  $27\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$ . Consequently, only  $23\%$  of the  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  mass concentration was influenced by traffic from the motorway. Finally, vehicle contribution to the  $\mathrm{PM}_{1}$  concentration was estimated to range between 0.6 and  $1.3\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$ .

For the first time, vertical profiles with high temporal and spatial resolution of particulate air pollutants could be recorded by tower elevators (Imhof et al., 2005). Upwind the motorway, no height-dependent difference in the particle number and surface area concentration was observed. On the downwind side, substantially higher concentrations were measured within the plume from near the ground up to  $20\mathrm{m}$  height than above the plume (Fig. 5). This height dependency was found for particles with diameter  $< 300\mathrm{nm}$ , which are soot particles and nucleation mode particles formed by condensation of exhaust gases in most cases and for coarse particles (diameter  $>1\mu \mathrm{m}$ ), originating from tyre and brake abrasion and resuspension of soil dust. Between 300 and  $700\mathrm{nm}$  particle size, no height dependence was found, because motor vehicles emit only a few particles in this size range.

After adjusting the instruments for their different time lags, the vertical distribution of the particle number concentrations showed a similar behaviour as the  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  concentrations: No height dependence on the upwind side and a continuous decrease with increasing height from 5 to  $30\mathrm{m}$  above the earth's surface on the downwind side was found. Consequently, the results in this study confirmed the correlation between particles and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  values that had also been observed in former case studies. A linear relation between the particle surface area (SA) and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  with correlation coefficients of  $R^2 = 0.96$  and 0.89 for 2 downwind cases was found. At around  $30\mathrm{m}$  above ground, the background value of SA was attained. By subtracting the background value from the concentrations measured in the near-surface air layer, direct emissions of the road were obtained, even if contemporaneous upwind and downwind measurements were missing. Together with the parallel wind measurements, mean absolute emission factors (EF) were determined. The PM emissions in the range between  $30\mathrm{nm}$  and  $10\mu \mathrm{m}$  from all vehicles passing the site were found to be  $1.80\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$ . Separation into the categories of LDV led to  $1.22\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  and  $0.03\mathrm{cm}^3\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  aerosol volume, while the values of HDV were  $7.79\times 10^{14}$  particles  $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  and  $0.41\mathrm{cm}^3\mathrm{km}^{-1}$  veh $^{-1}$  particle volume.

In addition to the calculations of PM emissions from measurements on tower elevators during the special observation periods (SOPs) using data of the electrical low-pressure impactor (ELPI), emissions were calculated from continuous ground-based SMPS measurements in the range of  $10 - 400\mathrm{nm}$ . # 2.5. Traffic census, emission calculation

The combination of detailed traffic measurements on an individual road section and corresponding emission modelling has proved to yield high-quality emission data of high temporal resolution. A sound traffic database was generated by (i) manual traffic counts together with an optical classification of vehicle categories, (ii) data coming from an automatic permanent counting station,

(iii) partly automatic acquisition of vehicle speeds, (iv) differentiation of the vehicle fleet into vehicle layers by registration plate number evaluations of 18700 vehicles, and (v) by the determination of the road gradient. Using these data the accuracy of emission calculations is improved considerably compared to calculations with data from literature and general databases (Kuhlwein and Friedrich, 2005).

The traffic measurements allowed for a classification of the individual passenger cars according to the exhaust gas purification standard they fulfilled. It was found that in 2001,  $7.5\%$  more vehicles with low-level catalytic converters (level EURO 1) were on the road than had been expected by the UBA. Moreover, the number of cars with high-level catalytic converter systems (level EURO 3) was only  $17\%$  in reality, although the UBA had predicted a proportion of  $29\%$  (UBA, 1999). This finding was confirmed by comparison between model-calculated emissions in different model stages. All the emissions calculated by the model  $(\mathrm{CO}, \mathrm{NO}_x, \mathrm{NMHC}$ , and  $\mathrm{PM}_{10})$  were higher in model stage 3 (including all traffic measurements) than in model stage 0 when taking traffic data from literature only (Fig. 6). The increase of emissions from model stage 0-3 was most pronounced for CO emissions on the weekend, the time when the vehicle fleet was dominated clearly by LDV ( $98.0\%$  of

43500 vehicles on Saturdays and  $98.9\%$  of 36400 vehicles on Sundays) contributing most to the overall CO emissions  $(80\%)$ .

In case of flowing traffic the mean speed of LDV traffic in direction Heidelberg was  $111.5\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$  with a standard deviation of  $16.0\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$  with no significant dependency from traffic flow up to 3000 vehicles  $\mathrm{h}^{-1}$  in one direction, while the mean speed of HDV traffic was  $85.8\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$  with standard deviation of  $7.5\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ . But the recognition of traffic congestion situations by suitable monitoring methods is very important to the modelling of high-quality emission data with high temporal resolution for individual road sections. Stop-and-go traffic led to a strong increase, especially in CO  $(35\%)$  and NMHC emissions  $(160\%)$  compared to emission rates during free traffic.

The variation coefficients of model-calculated emission rates caused by imprecise activity data were reduced by the new model approach to a maximum of  $5\%$  for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , NMHC, and  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  emissions and to  $11\%$  for CO. A further reduction of the uncertainties did not seem to be reasonable, as the additional effort to achieve better traffic data would increase exponentially and the uncertainties of the emission factors would gain importance with reduced uncertainties of the traffic activity data. # 3. Comparison of measurements and model calculations

# 3.1. Gaseous compounds: CO and  $NO_{x}$

Vehicle emissions calculated from exhaust gas concentration measurements in ambient air along the motorway A656 are compared to emissions determined by an emission calculation model using emission factors (UBA, 1999), statistical data on car fleet and traffic flow from common databases, and detailed and sophisticated traffic census data as described by Kuhlwein and Friedrich (2005). When using all BAB II quality-checked data, the measured emissions exceed the model-calculated CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  source intensities on all days (Figs. 2 and 7). The CO emissions are underestimated by the model by  $33\%$  on weekends and by  $20\%$  on working

days. The corresponding underestimations for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  emissions are  $19\%$  and  $17\%$ , respectively. Apart from the uncertainties in the measurements and analysis of the traffic census, measured emissions are higher than those calculated by the emission model. This may only be due to the underestimation of the emission factors used. Underestimation may be caused by an insufficient estimation of the emission factor at roller test stands (non-realistic driving pattern, low number of motor types in test, low number of test series with motors that have been used for a long time, too less dynamic dynamometer tests) or by loss of effect with aging of the catalytic converters of the cars.

By means of multiple regression calculation, it is possible to estimate separate emission factors for LDV and HDV. For  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  emissions of LDV, a good compliance between modelled and measured emission factors is found (Table 2). The mean emission factor of the light-duty traffic determined from measurements is  $0.68\pm 0.12\mathrm{gkm}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$ . It is about  $15\%$  higher than the emission factor of  $0.59\pm 0.02\mathrm{gkm}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1}$  calculated by the model. When looking at the 10 times higher emissions of HDVs, the emission factor measured for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  still is  $22\%$  higher  $(6.86\pm 1.57\mathrm{gkm}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1})$  than the calculated one  $(5.61\pm 0.31\mathrm{gkm}^{-1}\mathrm{veh}^{-1})$ . It becomes clear that the emission factors given by UBA especially for heavy-duty traffic are smaller than under 'real-world' conditions, which might be due to non-realistic driving patterns used during the roller test stand measurements

# 4. BAB II final results

The basic objective of BAB II was the evaluation of an emission model under real-world conditions, including the evaluation of emission factors and statistical traffic data used in the model. Therefore, a concept of measurements up- and downwind of a motorway was developed and tested in 1997 (Vogel et al., 2000) and implemented in 2001. The mass balancing method chosen requires accurate measurements upwind and downwind of the motorway. Hence, a rigorous quality assurance programme has to be applied before and after the field phase. Furthermore, it is essential to monitor the traffic on the motorway for the determination of source strengths from measurements and for model

calculations with given emission factors. During the BAB II field phase, a detailed traffic census was carried out, providing information of the number of vehicles passing by on the motorway, the speed of the vehicles, the composition of the car fleet, and the engine and exhaust gas reduction techniques. A period of two days when the motorway was closed due to construction work was used to demonstrate that with wind perpendicular to the motorway, plumes downwind of the motorway clearly originate from the traffic passing by Kohler et al., (2005).

Summarising, there are disadvantages of the method chosen, e.g. (i) dependency on meteorological conditions, (ii) experimental complexity and sensitivity due to difference calculations, and (iii) errors caused by few unidentified vehicles with extreme emissions ("high emitters"). But the advantages which are (i) source strengths calculations of real-world traffic, (ii) detection of the real fleet composition, (iii) availability of 2 independent methods for model evaluation (traffic census, emission measurements), (iv) update of the results of an automatic counting station by traffic census results and (v) determination of hints on missing or not accurate emission factors are undoubtedly in favour of the method.

The calculations of source strengths and the evaluation of emission factors were carried out for major components of traffic emissions, i.e. CO,  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ , and 21 NMHC compounds. An important outcome of BAB II is that, in general, the measured emissions of both gaseous compounds CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  are larger than the model-calculated emissions by  $23\%$  and  $17\%$ , respectively. Using multiple regression, it can be shown that the major discrepancy between  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  simulations and measurements is caused by HDV. This indicates that the emission factors given for  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  are still too low, especially for HDV  $(22\%)$ . For CO, the HDV and LDV fractions could not be separated, but it may be assumed that the CO emission factor is influenced mainly by LDV and that the given emission factor also is too low  $(23\%)$ .

For NMHC compounds, quite extreme differences were found between measurements and model calculations. The measurements exceed the model-calculated emissions by factors of 1.3-21. This discrepancy for NMHCs may be attributed to the still insufficient knowledge of traffic emissions of NMHC under real-world conditions. As far as emission factors for NMHCs (others than BTX) are concerned, further test stand investigations including the variety of cars, their age and driving pattern are necessary.

Another important result of BAB II concerns the PM emissions. The pronounced double peak structure in the particle size distributions measured near the motorway gives reason to discuss the problems associated with the incorporation of very small particles by human beings

living and/or working near highly frequented roads. It was found that in the vicinity of the motorway, ultrafine particles were dominant. These particles mainly consist of soot from diesel engines and are considered harmful to human health and, hence, deserve careful attention. The HDV emission factor for PM is about 10 times as high as for LDV. This underlines the importance of particle filters for HDVs. Particle number concentrations determined by different instruments and different calculation methods match well with values found in literature.

A critical point was the determination of mass concentrations for PM. As the particle densities and particle volumes were not available from measurements, assumptions would have been necessary, which would have led to a large uncertainty of the results. PM measurements during BAB II were limited to number concentrations. Considering the influence of PM on the human health, further investigations should focus on the accurate determination of mass concentrations of PM.

Atmospheric turbulence induced by the vehicles (Kalthoff et al., 2005) is an important factor when simulating the exhaust gas and particle concentration near the motorway at less than  $100\mathrm{m}$  lateral distance (Bäumer et al., 2005). Within this range the vehicle induced turbulence is leading to reduced concentrations in the surface layer and higher concentrations in the upper part of the plume. This finding cannot be neglected for modelling efforts and should be considered carefully in future model calculations of exhaust gas dispersion and emission load of the atmosphere near the source.

# ABSTRACT

Vehicle emission is an important contributor to urban air pollution with the increasing number of motor vehicles. Ten typical vehicles were selected in Wuhan to study the emissions of fine particular matters  $\mathrm{(PM_{2.5})}$  and associated chemical components by on-road tests through a Portable Emission Monitoring System (PEMS). The emission factors of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and the compositions of it from different types of vehicle were obtained. Results showed that the average emission factors of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  from gasoline and diesel vehicles were 1.266 and  $16.589\mathrm{mg}/$  km. As the emission standard of vehicles increased from China III to China V,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emission factor gradually decreased from 17.385 to  $1.520~\mathrm{mg / km}$ . Emission rate of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  was  $0.0384~\mathrm{mg / s}$  under low speed, and it increased to 0.0775 and  $0.0964~\mathrm{mg / s}$  under the medium and high speeds. The ratio of organic carbon versus elemental carbon (OC/EC) in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  from gasoline vehicles was 6.89, which was greater than that of diesel vehicles as 3.12. Because gasoline was made of small molecules and the compression ratio of gasoline engine was relatively low, some OC remained in the area where the ignition failed in the cylinder. The top four water-soluble ions with high emission factors were  $\mathrm{Cl}^-$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_4^{2 - }$ ,  $\mathrm{Ca}^{2 + }$ , and  $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ , while K, Na, Ca and Mg had a larger emission factors in the 21 tested inorganic elements. These water-soluble ions and inorganic elements mainly came from the oil burning, fuel additives and engines wear. Results of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emission characteristics would help to improve the air quality in Wuhan.

# 1. Introduction

The atmospheric fine particulate matter  $(\mathrm{PM}_{2.5})$  is a great contributor to air pollution, which not only affects the visibility of atmosphere, but also causes respiratory diseases (Martins and Graça, 2018; Shou et al., 2019; Kelp et al., 2020). The source analysis and control method of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  are research hotspots around the world (Mukherjee and Agrawal, 2018; Jiang et al., 2019; Lv et al., 2019). Studies have already shown that flue gas emitted from industrial coal-fired boilers (Li et al., 2017; Yao et al., 2019), soil dust of construction site (Chen et al., 2017) and vehicle emissions (Liu et al., 2019; Saraga et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019a, b) are the mainly  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  sources. There is a close relationship between  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution and vehicle emissions in many urban areas of large and medium cities (Hajmohammadi et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2019). The main sources of particulate matter in various regions of China were identified by Zhu et al. (2018) through source apportionment studies

published in 1987-2017. This study found that transportation was one of the main source categories, and generally dust and industrial contributions decreased and transportation contributions increased after 2000. A review of receptor-based source apportionment research of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  in China suggested that secondary sources and traffic emission had higher contribution in South China while coal combustion, dust and biomass burning were higher in North China (Zhang et al., 2017). A review about the source apportionment of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  in South Korea showed that motor vehicle and soil dust were identified as the main factors in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution (Ryou et al., 2018). EPA PMF 5.0 was used to make assessment on  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  sources in Patras, and it was found that vehicle emissions contributed to nearly  $34\%$  (Manousakas et al., 2017). Li et al. (2019) found that the vehicle emissions contributed to  $22.7\%$  of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution in Beijing in 2016. Since the dust of construction site can be controlled effectively, the proportion of vehicle emissions in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution in urban areas will increase gradually with the rising number

These methods mainly include remote sensing detection, vehicle bench test, tunnel test and portable vehicle emission measurement (Giechaskiel et al., 2018; Mahesh et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2020a, b). Remote sensing is an effective and economic tool to monitor vehicle emissions, while it is accurate in identifying gasoline vehicles but not diesel ones (Huang et al., 2018). And the chemical composition of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  can not be obtained by remote sensing (Smit et al., 2019). Myung et al. (2009) carried out bench tests on light motor vehicles and found that  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emissions of using liquefied petroleum gas and biodiesel were much lower than those of gasoline and diesel. Carbonaceous components in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from vehicles could be studied through a bench test, and it found that the driving speed directly affected the EC emissions (Fraser et al., 2002). However, the fluence of actual driving conditions on vehicle emissions could not be reflected (Pouresmaeili et al., 2018). Sánchez et al. (2019) studied the  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emission factors of both light and heavy vehicles through tunnel experiments, and the emission factors of heavy vehicles tended to be much higher than those of light vehicles. Tunnel experiments were also conducted by Hao et al. (2019) to study their characteristics of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emissions from motor vehicles in Xi'an, China, and the results showed that OC accounted for  $34.10\%$  of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ . Since tunnels in different locations have different speed limits, and it is difficult to cover both high-speed and real-world driving conditions in some tunnels (Huang et al., 2017; Smit et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020a, b). Huang et al. (2013) used PEMS to detect the concentrations of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emissions from buses, and cars under actual driving conditions, and they found that concentrations of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  increased with the driving speed. PEMS was also used to analyze the chemical composition of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from heavy diesel trucks, and it was found that EC and OC were the major components of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  (Zhang et al., 2015). Based on the results of previous researches, the conclusions drawn from various cities were obviously different due to their geographical locations, climates, populations, etc. Wuhan is located in the central of China, and it has a large population and a large number of motor vehicles with high humidity climate (Yang et al., 2019). The effect of humidity on some gaseous pollutants (such as CO and NOx) emitted from vehicles has been studied (Dolly et al., 2020), while few research were carried out on the  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and its chemical components. Therefore, it was necessary to carry out the research on vehicle emissions of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  in Wuhan, China.

# 2.2.  $p.m_{2.5}$  sampling and chemical components analysis

$\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from the tested vehicles were collected by the SEMTECH-PFS sub-module. SEMTECH-PFS sub-module had three channels for membrane sampling, where three sampling membranes with a diameter of  $47\mathrm{mm}$  were located. These three channels could not be used to collect  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  at the same time. When one channel was ventilated and sampling, the other two channels were closed. Computer was used to decide the channel of the airflow. Before sampling started, a quartz filter (MK360, Munktel, Sweden) was placed on Channel 1, a Teflon filter (R2PJ047, PALL, USA) was placed on Channel 2 and another quartz filter was placed on Chanel 3. The quartz filter membrane on Chanel 1 was mainly used for analyzing organic carbon, elemental carbon and water-soluble ions. The Teflon filter membrane on Chanel 2 was used for analyzing elements. The quartz filter membrane on Chanel 3 was used as the blank sample.

Carbonaceous aerosols consisted of organic carbon (OC), elemental carbon (EC), and a small amount of carbonate. Since carbonates were very small part in atmospheric particles and the proportion of carbonates was less than  $5\%$  of total carbon (TC), they would not be discussed in this study. The DRI thermal carbon analyzer (IMPROVE-A, DRI, USA) was used to detect the concentration of OC and EC, when  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  sample was collected without the pretreatment after equilibrium weighing. Several water-soluble ions in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from the tested vehicles were analyzed, which included  $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ ,  $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ ,  $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ ,  $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ ,  $\mathrm{NH}_4^+$ ,  $\mathrm{Cl}^-$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_4^{2-}$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_3^-$  and  $\mathrm{F}^-$ . Ion chromatograph (IC) was used to analyze the water-soluble ions that collected on the filter. 21 crustal elements including Na, Mg, K, Ca, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Ga, As, Rb, Sr, Ag, Cd, Cs, Ba and Pb were detected by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). Microwave digester was used as the pretreatment of collected filter samples. In order to find out the content of crustal elements in the

$\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  samples, it is necessary to pretreat the collected filter membranes. The process of both pretreating and subsequent storage should avoid mixing the crustal elements to be tested.

# 2.3. Evaluation of  $PM_{2.5}$  emissions

The mass difference of the sampled membrane could be calculated by weighing the masses before and after  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  sampling. The real-time flow rate of vehicle emissions could be obtained by PEMS, and the driving conditions of tested vehicles were recorded by GPS. Emission factor of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  under real driving conditions could be calculated by Equation (1).

$$
E F = \frac {\Delta m \cdot V \cdot R}{V _ {1} S} \tag {1}
$$

Where, EF  $(\mathrm{mg / km})$  was the emission factor of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  under real driving conditions.  $\Delta \mathrm{m}$  (mg) was the mass difference before and after the sampling membrane.  $\mathrm{V(m^3)}$  was the total exhaust volume of the vehicle emission.  $\mathrm{V}_1$ $(\mathrm{m}^{3})$  was the total ventilation volume of the sampling membrane. R was the dilution ratio of SEMTECH-MPS. S (km) was the travel distance of the motor vehicle during  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  sampling.

# 3.2. Emission characteristics of carbonaceous components in  $PM_{2.5}$

In Table 2, the average emission factors of TC for China III, IV and V gasoline vehicles were 1086.15, 1054.06 and  $397.94~\mu \mathrm{g / km}$ , respectively. When compared to China III vehicles, the TC emission factors of China IV and V vehicles decreased by  $2.95\%$  and  $63.36\%$ . Meanwhile, it could be found that the OC emission factors of the tested gasoline vehicles were 8.68 times larger than those of EC except Vehicle 1#. TC emission factor of diesel vehicles was 9.15 times higher than that of gasoline vehicles. To find out the TC emission characteristics of diesel vehicles with different emission standards, the average emission factors of TC for China III, IV and V vehicles were 25,973.93, 11,745.64 and  $1735.88~\mu \mathrm{g / km}$ , respectively. When compared to China III diesel vehicles, the TC emission factors of China IV and V vehicles decreased by  $54.78\%$  and  $93.32\%$ . OC emission factors of diesel vehicles were also 1.06 times larger than EC emission factors, which was similar to the OC and EC emission characteristics of gasoline vehicles.

The ratio of OC/EC was an important parameter in the carbonaceous components of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ , which was often used to determine the source of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ . The OC/EC ratios of carbonaceous compositions from the 10 tested vehicles were shown in Fig. 4, and the average OC/EC ratios of the vehicles with different emission standards were figured out as well. The OC/EC ratio of gasoline vehicles was higher than 3.0 except for Vehicle 1#. It meant there were many organic components in the carbonaceous component, which was consistent with the results of some recent studies (James et al., 2002). The OC/EC ratio of Vehicle 1# was only 0.3, which deviated from the normal value of gasoline vehicle emissions. Because Vehicle 1# was a taxi with both gasoline and natural gas functions, and the engine used a mixture of gasoline and natural gas for a long time. It used gasoline fuel during the test, and insufficient combustion of gasoline in the engine caused an increase in the number of black carbon particles, which led to a small ratio of OC/EC. The average OC/EC ratio of China IV Vehicles was 10.2, and it decreased to 5.3 in China V gasoline vehicles. This indicated the improvement of emission standards could reduce the percentage of OC in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from gasoline vehicles. For diesel vehicles, the average OC/EC ratios of China III, IV and V vehicles were 1.5, 3.0, and 4.9, respectively. The OC/EC ratio of diesel vehicles turned to decrease as the emission standard rose up. It could also be seen from Fig. 4 that the OC/EC ratio of gasoline vehicles was generally greater than that of diesel vehicles, which was mainly because diesel was more likely to form unburned soot particles during the combustion process (Shen et al., 2021). There were two main differences between gasoline vehicles and diesel vehicles, fuel composition and engine structure. Gasoline was made of small molecules and was easy to mix evenly with air. After the spark plug discharged ignition, the fuel burned rapidly. The compression ratio of the gasoline engine was relatively low, and a large amount of OC remained in the area where the

ignition failed in the cylinder. This caused the overall OC/EC of gasoline vehicles to be much higher than that of diesel vehicles.

# 3.5.  $p.m_{2,5}$  composition spectrum of vehicle emission in wuhan

The components of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from 6 gasoline vehicles and 4 diesel vehicles in this experiment were figured out, and the percentage of each component in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  was calculated.  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  Composition Spectrum of both gasoline and diesel vehicles were shown in Table 3.

The data of Table 3 showed that OC and EC were the two types of substances that accounted for the largest proportion of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  in vehicle emissions. The proportion of OC in gasoline and diesel vehicles were  $53.926\%$  and  $51.151\%$ , respectively. The proportion of EC in diesel vehicles (25.169%) was nearly twice as much as gasoline vehicles (13.068%), which indicated more EC components were emitted from diesel vehicles than gasoline vehicles.  $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}, \mathrm{SO}_{4}^{2-}, \mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$  and  $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$  were the water-soluble ions with the top five proportions in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from the tested vehicles. The proportion of  $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$  in gasoline vehicles was more than twice of diesel vehicles, while the proportion of  $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$  in diesel vehicles was more than twice of gasoline vehicles. These might be because the additives in gasoline and diesel vehicles were different. The additives in gasoline vehicles might contain more Ca, while additives in diesel vehicles might contain more Mg. K, Na, Ca, Mg, Sr, Fe, Zn and Pb were the inorganic elements with higher proportions in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from the tested vehicles. K, Na, Ca, and Mg were mainly derived from fuel additives, while Sr, Fe, Zn and Pb were mainly derived from the wear and tear of vehicles engines.

The components in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  could be divided into five categories: OC, EC, water-soluble ions, inorganic elements and other components. Each percentages of these five categories in the particulate matter were calculated, and the results were shown in Fig. 7. The proportions of OC, EC, water-soluble ions and inorganic elements in gasoline vehicles were  $53.93\%$ $13.07\%$ $13.12\%$  and  $0.30\%$  respectively. The proportions of OC, EC, water-soluble ions and inorganic elements in diesel vehicles were  $51.15\%$ $25.17\%$ $14.53\%$  and  $0.44\%$  respectively. Thus, the largest difference in the proportion of various components in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emitted from gasoline and diesel vehicles was EC.

# 4. Conclusion

Ten typical vehicles were selected to study on-road emissions of fine particles in Wuhan, China. Characteristics of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentrations were obtained through the calculations of the data that recorded by the PEMS. The emission factors of vehicles decreased as the standards became stricter, and the diesel vehicles were much higher than gasoline ones. The average  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emission factors of gasoline vehicles certified according to China III, IV and V standards were 1.675, 1.452 and 0.783

$\mathrm{mg / km}$ . While for the diesel vehicles, they rose up to 33.095, 15.134 and  $2.994~\mathrm{mg / km}$ , which was mainly because the molecules of diesel were usually larger than gasoline, and the high humidity increased the difficulty for their full combustion.

Laboratory analysis on collected filter membranes was carried out to study the characteristics of carbonaceous components, water-soluble ions and inorganic elements. Emission factor of carbonaceous components emitted from diesel vehicles was 9.15 times higher than that of gasoline vehicles. However, the OC/EC ratio of gasoline vehicles was over twice of diesel vehicles. The compression ratio of gasoline engine was relatively low, and some OC remained in the area where the ignition failed in the cylinder.

The water-soluble ions with the top four emission factors was  $\mathrm{Cl}^-$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_4^{2-}$ ,  $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$  and  $\mathrm{Na}^+$ . Their percentages in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  increased slightly with high humidity and the order of top water-soluble ions kept the same. Their emission factors of diesel vehicles were all larger than gasoline vehicles. The highest emission factors of inorganic elements in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  were K, Ca, Na and Mg, while Fe, Zn, Sr, Cr, Mn, Pb and other inorganic elements had lower emission factors.  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  composition spectrum of vehicle emission in Wuhan was obtained and the largest proportion was OC. It occupied  $53.93\%$  and  $51.15\%$  in gasoline and diesel vehicles, respectively. Secondly, EC occupied  $13.07\%$  in gasoline vehicles while  $25.17\%$  in diesel vehicles.

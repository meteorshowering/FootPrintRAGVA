# ABSTRACT

Solvent use and paint consumption are significant source sectors of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) emissions in China. The occupational painters have high risk of health effect due to exposure to high VOCs concentration. However, the toxic components in coating environment have not been carefully identified, and the health risks of VOCs exposure have not been sufficiently assessed. This study collected air samples from nine workshops of three major coating sectors in the Yangtze River Delta of China, namely cargo container coating, ship equipment coating, and furniture coating, to evaluate the noncancer and cancer risk of toxic VOCs exposure to occupational painters under a normal working condition. The results show that the container coating had highest cancer risk  $(2.29 \times 10^{-6} - 5.53 \times 10^{-6})$  exceeding the safe limit of  $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ , while non-cancer risk of all workshops was lower than acceptable level of 1. Ethylbenzene and 1,2-dichloropropane should be targeted for priority removal during the container coating process in attempt to reduce adverse health effect on the occupational painters. This study helps better understand the health risk of VOCs exposure in coating workshops in China and provides information for policy-makers to formulate possible control of specific toxic compounds during coating process.

# 1. Introduction

The volatile organic compounds (VOCs) found in polluted atmospheres comprise a broad spectrum of hydrocarbons, halocarbon, and oxygenated hydrocarbons (Atkinson, 2000). Some of the VOC species, such as benzene, 1,3-butadiene, and formaldehyde, are toxic and pose adverse effect on human health (WHO, 2000). Accidental or short-term exposure to high concentration of VOCs may cause irritation of eyes, nose, throat, and lungs as well as damage to the liver, kidney and central nervous system. Long term exposure to even low level of concentration could results in asthma, reduced pulmonary function, cardiovascular disease and

serious cancers (BC CDC, 2018; Soni et al., 2018; US EPA, 2020).

Inhalation is the main exposure pathway to the VOCs. Quantitative estimate of carcinogenic risk and non-carcinogenic risk from inhalation exposure to VOCs are developed by different agencies, such as World Health Organization (WHO), US Environmental Protection Agency (USEPA), Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA) in California, and Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) (WHO, 2000; IRIS, 2014; 2017; ATSDR, 2017; OEHHA, 2017). The potency values of the reference concentrations (RfC) for non-cancer risk assessments and inhalation cancer unit risk (UR) for cancer-risk assessments are summarized, which provide a data foundation of quantitative assessment of health risk of VOCs exposures.

With rapid urbanization and expanding industrial activities in China, tremendous amounts of VOCs are emitted into the atmosphere from various sources such as vehicular emission, solvent use, and petrochemical industry (Zhang et al., 2009; Bo et al., 2008; Mo et al., 2016). Ambient concentrations of VOCs are observed to be

high in the urban metropolitan cities and vicinity of industrial complex (Liu et al., 2008; Mo et al., 2017). However, the VOCs concentrations may range from one per million (ppm) to one per billion (ppb) in the complex mixture of air. Hence, the health risk is significantly different in a wide array of the household microenvironment, densely built environment, and highly industrialized areas (Weng et al., 2009; Feng et al., 2010; Ramirez et al., 2012; Boor et al., 2017). In different indoor and outdoor environment, understanding of the risk of inhalation exposure to the hazardous VOCs is apparently needed for the government regulatory and policy making to control the priority VOCs species from a health risk-based perspective.

Heath risk assessment of exposure to VOCs have been extensively studied in different environment in China (Du et al., 2014; Huy et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2011; Xiang et al., 2017). Indoor microenvironment received most widely attention from the public because people spend most of the lifetime indoors (Sundell, 2004). Guo et al. (2004) compared the inhalation cancer risk in the home, office, restaurant, and classroom in Hong Kong, and found the smoking home had the highest cancer risk while classroom the lowest. Benzene accounted for over  $40\%$  of the cancer risks in these indoor environments. Zhou et al. (2011) evaluated the personal exposure to VOCs in residential indoor/outdoor, workplace, and vehicle, and estimated the associated cancer risk in Tianjin. It was found that the cancer risk of benzene, chloroform, carbon tetrachloride, and 1,3-butadiene exceeded the recommended acceptable limit of 1 per million (US EPA, 2005). Dai et al. (2017) assessed the cancer and non-cancer risk in newly renovated homes Shanghai, China, reporting that halocarbon species such as 1,2-dichloroethane, 1,4-dichlorobenzene, methylenechloride presented the highest cancer risk. In addition to household microenvironment, heath effect assessment of VOCs exposure were studied in industrial workshops. Emission profiles and health risk of VOCs were examined during e-waste dismantling processes and plastic solid waste recycling processes (An et al., 2014; He et al., 2015). Cancer risk and non-cancer risk were then quantified in these manufacturing workshops, with aromatic and halogenated hydrocarbons as the most toxic compounds. Due to a closer proximity with emission sources, VOCs exposure poses a higher threat to the workers in the occupational environment, such as landfill environment (Drumusoglu et al., 2010), printing workshop (Alabdulhadi et al., 2019), wood furniture coating workshop (Tong et al., 2019), petrochemical complex (Mo et al., 2015), coking wastewater treatment plant (Zhang et al., 2019), and asphalt pavement areas (Cui et al., 2020).

Solvent use and paint consumption are significant source sectors of VOCs emissions. A diversity of raw and auxiliary organic materials are consumed during spraying, cleaning, and gluing in a wide array of sub-sectors, such as automobile coating, furniture coating, ship coating, and printing. Many studies have measured the chemical profiles of VOCs emitted into the working atmosphere. Benzene, toluene, xylene, and m/p-xylene (BTEX) were found to be the prevalent species, accounting for over  $50 - 90\%$  of total VOCs emissions (Yuan et al., 2010; Zheng et al., 2013). However, for the coating sectors, the toxic components have not been identified in detail, and the health risks of VOCs exposure have not sufficiently assessed. In particular, the occupational painters are directly exposed to high concentration of VOCs during their working hours. The risk of potential carcinogenic and noncarcinogenic effect is substantially higher over a long-term period of their career life. Recently, Wang et al. (2018) assessed the health risk of VOCs exposure to working painters using laboratory-scale experiments, which, nevertheless, may not sufficiently reflect the complicated environment of the coating process. Tong et al. (2019) collected numerous samples from wood furniture manufacturing

workshop and non-production areas. The VOCs emission characteristics and health risk were evaluated. However, only 17 VOCs species were detected probably due to limitations of their sampling and chemical analysis technique. More field survey and on-site analysis of the health risk of VOC exposure are definitely needed, which could provide scientific support for the regulation of VOC control and health protection of occupational workers.

The main purpose of this study is to evaluate the health risk of VOCs exposure to occupational painters under a normal working condition. Field samples were collected in different workshops in three major coating sectors in the Yangtze River Delta of China, namely cargo container coating, ship equipment coating, and furniture coating sectors. The spectrum of VOC components and their potency values of toxic effect were examined. Non-cancer and cancer risk of toxic species were estimated for the occupational painters in different indoor workshops.

# 2. Methodology

# 2.1. Sampling sites and procedures

The description of the source samples is tabulated in Table 1. Three coating sectors in China were investigated in this study, namely cargo container coating, ship coating, and furniture coating. The coating processes were divided into primary coating, secondary coating, and tertiary coating, depending on the manufacturing flows in each industrial sector. In general, there was one worker in each coating workshop under normal working conditions. The workshops were  $60 - 150\mathrm{m}^2$  for different coating processes in these industrial sectors. The central ventilation system was installed in the workplaces and the exhaust gas was discharged from the chimney and stack. The activated carbon-based masks were used by the workers. Whole-air samples were collected in the coating workshops that were being operated under normal conditions. Fig. 1(a) shows an example of the working condition of furniture coating.

Indoor air was collected using 3.2-L fused silica stainless steel canisters that had been precleaned with high-purity nitrogen and evacuated with an automated canister cleaner. Each sample was taken for  $30\mathrm{min}$  using an inlet with pre-calibrated flow regulator (CS1200 sampler; Entech Instrument, Inc.) operating at  $38~\mathrm{mL~min}^{-1}$  (Entech Instruments Inc., California, USA), as shown in Fig. 1(b). The sampling equipment was held by the sampling staff at about  $1.5\mathrm{-m}$  high, next to the occupational painter and less than  $1\mathrm{m}$  away from the spraying source to represent a similar distance with the painter in real situations. Three samples were collected at similar position in each workshop to ensure the representative of the field samples. Canister vacuums were checked before and after sampling. The field samples and two blank samples were sent to the Peking University (PKU) VOCs laboratory for analysis immediately after sampling within one week of shipping time. The blank

# 2.2.VOCs analysis

The chemical analysis procedures followed U.S. EPA methods TO-14 and TO-15. A 500-mL aliquot of air sample from each canister was concentrated using a three-stage cryofocusing preconcentration system (Entech 7100; Entech Instruments, Simi Valley, CA, USA).  $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}$  and  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  were removed in the first two cryotraps using glass beads and Tenax-TA adsorbents, respectively. VOCs were then trapped at  $-180^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  in the final stage and the trap was rapidly heated, after which VOCs were transferred into the gas chromatography-mass spectrometry/flame ionization detection (GC-MS/FID) system for analysis (GC, HP-7890A; MSD, HP-5975C; Hewlett-Packard, Palo Alto, CA, USA). This system used a Dean SwitchTM (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) to introduce the effluent into a DB-624 column ( $60\mathrm{m} \times 0.25\mathrm{mm} \times 1.8\mu\mathrm{m}$ ; J&W Scientific, Folsom, CA, USA) with a MSD to separate and analyze  $C_4 - C_{12}$  hydrocarbons and halocarbons and a PLOT (Al/KCl) column ( $30\mathrm{m} \times 0.25\mathrm{mm} \times 3.0\mu\mathrm{m}$ ; J&W Scientific) with a FID to measure the  $C_2 - C_4$  hydrocarbons. The GC oven temperature was programmed initially at  $30^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  for  $7\mathrm{min}$ , increasing to  $120^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  at  $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}/\mathrm{min}$  and holding for  $5\mathrm{min}$ , and then increasing to  $180^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  and holding for  $7\mathrm{min}$ . The entire process took  $\sim 47\mathrm{min}$ . The carrier gas was pure helium (purity N 99.999%). During the analysis process, strict protocol for quality assurance and quality control (QA/QC) was used. The standard gases including Photochemical Assessment Monitoring Stations (PAMS) standard mixture and TO-15 standard mixture from Spectra Gases Inc., NJ, USA were used to calibrate the chemical compounds. Three VOCs with known concentrations, including bromochloromethane, 1,4-difluorobenzene, and 1-bromo-3-fluorobenzene, were used as internal standards for each sample to calibrate the system. The dynamic dilution system (Entech 4600A, Entech Instruments, Simi Valley, CA, USA) was used to prepare the gas standard for calibration. The mass responses of the internal standards were monitored during the analysis to ensure their stability. A gas standard (diluting from  $1\mathrm{ppm}$  to  $2\mathrm{ppb}$ )

and a laboratory blank sample were measured each day to check the stability and contamination of the analysis system, respectively. Based on a  $100\mathrm{-mL}$  injected sample volume and a five-fold dilution of the approximately 1 ppbv gas standard, the GC-MS/FID analysis was repeated for seven times. The method detection limits of each species were calculated by  $\mathrm{MDL_i} = C_i\times \sigma_i\times 3.14$  where  $C_i$  is the concentration of species  $i$ ,  $\sigma_{\mathrm{i}}$  is the standard deviation of concentration. The precision and method detection limit of each VOC species were provided in Table S1 in the Supplementary Material (SM). Detailed descriptions for the VOCs analysis system are available elsewhere (Liu et al., 2008; Yuan et al., 2010).

# 2.3. Risk assessment

# 2.3.1. Determination of toxic VOCs

Among the detected VOCs in this study, the species with quantitative assessments of health risk were selected. The non-canner reference concentrations (RfC) for non-cancer assessments and inhalation cancer unit risk (UR) for cancer-risk assessments are provided in Table 2. These values were collected from available open sources, including IRIS (Integrated Risk Information System; IRIS, 2014); PPRTV (Provisional Peer Reviewed Toxicity Values of IRIS; US EPA, 2007); HEAST (Health Effects Assessment Summary Tables; RAIS, 2009); WHO (World Health Organization; WHO, 2000); ATSDR (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry; ATSDR, 2017); OEHHA (Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment; OEHHA, 2017). Priority was giving to IRIS and WHO if duplicate values were available. In addition, according to the carcinogenic effect, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC, 2017) classifies the toxic pollutants into five groups, namely Group 1-Carcinogenic to humans, Group 2A-Probably carcinogenic to humans, Group 2B-Possibly carcinogenic to humans, Group 3-Not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to humans, and Group - Probably not carcinogenic to humans. Carcinogenic classifications of the VOCs in IARC are also listed in Table 2.

$$
\begin{array}{l} E C _ {a} = C _ {m} \times E T \times 1 d a y / 2 4 h o u r s \times E F \times 1 y e a r / 3 6 5 d a y s \times E D / A L \\ \times (1 - \eta) \end{array} \tag {1}

where  $EC_{a}$  is adjusted exposure concentration,  $C_{m}$  is measured concentration,  $ET$  is exposure time (hours/day);  $EF$  is the exposure frequency (days/year);  $ED$  is exposure duration (years);  $AL$  is the average lifetime, given as 70 years (US EPA, 2005). Based on the field survey and interviews of the workers, the exposure time  $(ET)$  was given as the working time of  $8\mathrm{h}$  per day. The exposure frequency  $(EF)$  was given as the working days of 245 days per year, considering 2 days weekly vocation and 7 day vocation for Chinese New Year. The exposure duration was assumed to be 20 years which is a common work lifetime among the occupational coating workers (Durmusoglu et al., 2010). The  $\eta$  is the efficiency of personal protective equipment. The activated carbon-based masks were used by the workers. Compared with N95 mask which could have the filter efficiency of  $95\%$ , the activated carbon-based mask has stronger absorption capacity for toxic organic gas. Here, we employed a maximum value of removal efficiency to be  $99.97\%$  as indicated by the instruction of the mask, which is a conservative value compared with  $51\pm 19\%$  to  $78\pm 22\%$  for VOC removal and  $98\% -99.7\%$  for toxic gas tested in previous studies (Gallego et al., 2013; Khayan et al., 2019).

The non-cancer risk was assessed by hazard quotient (HQ) proposed by US EPA (2005):

$$
H Q _ {i} = \frac {E C _ {a i}}{R f C _ {i}} \tag {2}
$$

where  $HQ_{i}$  is the hazard quotient of VOC species  $i$ ;  $RfC_{i}$  is the reference concentration, as shown in Table 2. Cumulative noncancer risks from multiple VOCs species, using hazard index (HI) as an indicator, were estimated by summing the  $HQ_{i}$  of the VOCs considered in this study.

$$
H I = \sum H Q _ {i} \tag {3}
$$

The inhalation cancer risk in workshops was estimated by multiplying the exposure concentration by the inhalation unit risk as follows:

$$
C R _ {i} = E C _ {a i} \times U R _ {i} \tag {4}
$$

where  $CR_{i}$  is the cancer risk of species  $i$ ;  $UR_{i}$  is the inhalation unit risk  $(\mu \mathrm{g} \, \mathrm{m}^{-3})$ . It should be noted that this study used the inhalation cancer unit risks (UR) multiplying by the  $EC_{a}$  to estimate the cancer risk while the slope factor multiplying by the chronic daily intake (CDI) were deployed by some other studies (Lai et al., 2016; Lerner et al., 2012; Lin et al., 2019). The difference between these two approaches have been discussed in US EPA (2005). It is appropriate for calculating upper-bound risks for air by the UR (US EPA, 1992).

The total cancer risk, as indicated by cancer index  $(CI)$ , was calculated by summing the  $CR_{i}$  of the VOCs with known UR values.

$$
C I = \sum C R _ {i} \tag {5}
$$

According to criteria proposed by US EPA (2005), the  $CI$  lower than  $1 \times 10^{-6}$  is regarded as negligible risk. The  $HI$  lower than 1 is considered to pose no adverse health effect to human beings.

# 2.4. Uncertainty analysis

The uncertainty of the risk assessment was quantified by performing the Monte Carlo simulation and sensitivity analysis using Crystal Ball software (v11.1.2.4). The probabilistic model provides a probability distribution of risk based on the entire range of input data instead of a single point value. This could help characterize the uncertainty of risk assessment (Hu et al., 2007; Du et al., 2014; Sielken and Valdez-Flores, 1999). The input parameter of the model included the exposure concentrations and  $UR$  values. When conducting the modelling, the VOCs concentrations were assumed to be described as lognormal distribution which had been commonly adopted in previous risk assessment studies (Zhou et al., 2011; Dai et al., 2017; Du et al., 2014). The  $UR$  values were described as triangle distribution. US EPA IRIS defines  $UR$  as the upper-bound excess lifetime cancer risk estimated by continuous exposure to an agent at a concentration of  $1\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$  in air (US EPA, 2005; Zhou et al., 2011). As such, the  $UR$  value was used as the maximum and the most likely values, and zero was assigned to minimum (lowest risk) in the triangle distribution. Independent runs with multiple iterations were tested and 10,000 iterations were sufficient to ensure the convergence and stability of the simulation. Sensitivity analysis was performed to determine the contribution of input variables to the uncertainty (Dai et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2011).

# 3.2. Toxic VOCs levels in coating workshops

The concentrations of the toxic VOCs selected in this study among different coating workshops are illustrated in Fig. 3 and Table S3. Here, we look details into the species concentrations in different coating workshop in each sector. For the ship coating sector, the species concentrations were much lower than those in container sector. In the furniture sector, toluene had the highest concentration in primary coating  $(5.3 \times 10^{3} \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{m}^{-3})$ , followed by secondary coating  $(1.83 \times 10^{4} \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{m}^{-3})$  and tertiary coating  $(1.26 \times 10^{4} \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{m}^{-3})$  workshops. From the above observation in different workshops, the concentrations in the same coating sector were generally within the same order of magnitude. However, the difference could be an order of magnitude among different coating sectors. This is mainly because the coating surface areas of container were much larger than the surface areas of ship and furniture components. More solvents were used in the coating processes of container, resulting in larger concentrations of VOCs species.

# 3.4. Uncertainty analysis

The total uncertainties of the risk estimation for ethylbenzene and 1,2-dichloropropane were determined by Monte Carlo simulation. # 4. Conclusions and implications

This study collected field samples in the coating workshops that were being operated under normal conditions. The concentration profiles of VOCs in container coating, ship coating, and furniture painter sector were determined. Toluene, m/p-xylene, and ethylbenzene are most abundant species in coating processes, which was generally in line with the previous studies. However, 1,2-dichloropropane contributed much in ship coating and furniture coating, which was not observed in previous studies. It indicates that halocarbon should be included in the emission profiles. Sector-based and regional-specific emission profiles should be established to better understand the source characteristics of coating processes.

A total of 25 toxic VOCs were selected to assess their non-cancer risk and cancer risk for occupational painters in different coating workshop. The concentration levels varied in different workshops of different sectors. Highest concentration of total VOCs was found in tertiary coating workshop of container sector, followed by secondary coating workshop and primary workshop of container sector. Based on the potency values of non-canner reference concentrations and inhalation cancer unit risk, the non-cancer risk and cancer risk were estimated for the toxic VOCs. Although no noncancer health effect was found, the top five workshops of the relatively high non-cancer risk were identified, including secondary coating workshop of container sector, primary workshop of container, mixing workshop of ship coating sector, tertiary coating workshop of container sector, and primary coating workshop of ship sector. For the cancer risk, the secondary coating workshop of container sector had highest cancer risk, followed by primary coating workshop of container, tertiary coating workshop of container, and mixing workshop of ship sector, which were 1.34-5.53 times of the safe limit of  $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ . The ethylbenzene and 1,2-dichloropropane were the major species that contributed to the high cancer risk in these workshops. This study reveals that the container coating had highest cancer risk exceeding the acceptable level. Ship coating had low probability of cancer risk while there was no cancer risk for the furniture coating. Control of ethylbenzene and 1,2-dichloropropane during the container coating process should be on priority in attempt to reduce adverse health effect on the occupational painters. Because the maximum removing efficiency (99.97%) of the activated carbon-based mask was adopted for the health risk assessment, the non-cancer and cancer risk could be higher if the masks are overused or loss of efficiency of the activated carbon adsorption. Frequent changes of masks are recommended for occupational workers. Last but not the least, it should be noted that the limited number of samples is one of the main limitations in this study. More field samples should be collected in a wider arrays of coating workshops and sectors would help reduce the uncertainty of the risk assessments.

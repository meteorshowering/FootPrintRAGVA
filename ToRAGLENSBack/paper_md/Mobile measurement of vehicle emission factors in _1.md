# ABSTRACT

Tunnels are the preferred experimental environments for estimating vehicle emission factors (EFs) under real-world driving conditions. In this study, online measurements of traffic-related air pollutants (including  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{X}}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{O}_{3}$ , particulate matter [PM], and volatile organic compounds [VOCs]) were conducted using a mobile laboratory in the Sujungsan Tunnel in Busan, Korea. Mobile measurements generated concentration profiles of the target exhaust emissions inside the tunnel. These data were used to produce a zonation of the tunnel, i.e., mixing and accumulation zones. There were differences between the  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ , and  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{X}}$  profiles, and a starting point that was free from ambient air mixing effects could be set at  $600\mathrm{m}$  from the tunnel entrance. The EFs of vehicle exhaust emissions were calculated using pollutant concentration gradients. The average EFs for  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ , NO,  $\mathrm{NO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ , and  $\sum \mathrm{VOCs}$  were 149,000, 380, 55, 29.2, 9.64, 4.33, and  $16.7\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\cdot \mathrm{veh}^{-1}$ , respectively. Among the VOC groups, alkanes contributed more than  $70\%$  of the VOC EF. Mobile measurement-derived EFs were validated using the conventional EFs from stationary measurements. The EF results from the mobile measurements matched those from the stationary measurements, while the absolute concentration differences between them implied complex aerodynamic movements of the target pollutants inside the tunnel. This study demonstrated the usefulness and advantages of applying mobile measurements in a tunnel environment and indicated the potential of the approach for observation-based policymaking.

# 1. Introduction

Vehicle emissions are a major source of air pollution in urban areas (Franco et al., 2013), with nitrogen oxides  $(\mathrm{NO} + \mathrm{NO}_2; \mathrm{NO}_\mathrm{X})$  and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) being key precursors for the production of secondary organic aerosols and  $\mathrm{O}_3$  in tropospheric chemical processes (Huang et al., 2017; Ho et al., 2009). These processes play important roles in the deterioration of regional air quality and global climate change (Kumar et al., 2018; Sahu et al., 2016). In addition, vehicle emissions are associated with an increased prevalence of various adverse health effects, including respiratory illness, DNA damage, cardiovascular disease, and adverse birth outcomes (Choi and Paulson, 2016; Hoek et al., 2010; Knol et al., 2009). In Korea, the number of registered automobiles reached 23.7 million in 2019, meaning that approximately one in two citizens owns a vehicle (Korean Statistical Information Service, 2019). In this environment of increasing vehicle ownership, concern over the health risks of vehicle exhaust emissions has prompted the need for accurate exhaust gas measurements and the estimation of emission factors (EFs).

There are four widely used methods for determining vehicle EFs and emission profiles: (1) the chassis dynamometer method (Triantafyllopoulos et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Pelkmans and Debal, 2006); (2) the portable emission measurement system (Gallus et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2011); (3) a sealed housing for evaporative determination (SHED) (Frosina et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2017); (4) direct measurements in tunnels (Song et al., 2018b; Zhang et al., 2018b; Brimblecombe et al., 2015; Ameur-Bouddabbous et al., 2012; Mancilla et al., 2012; Ho et al., 2009). The chassis dynamometer and SHED methods, which are commonly used for chamber studies, can control the operating conditions and fuel consumption factors, allowing the emission profile to be determined under various driving conditions and fuel configurations. However, these methods are expensive and time-consuming, and the estimated emission profiles do not reflect real traffic conditions (Yang et al., 2018; Jamriska et al., 2004). For these reasons, tunnel studies have been used as an alternative for determining vehicle emissions in recent decades. In addition, tunnel monitoring can encompass a large number of vehicles and measure primary emissions without the effects of photo-oxidation (Franco et al., 2013).

Although tunnel studies are useful for EF determination, there are some limitations. First, external ambient effects are not considered (Deng et al., 2018). Second, the selection of the sampling points is ambiguous and subject to the influence of bore entrances. Finally, conventional measurements for estimating the EF of VOCs are limited. Generally, VOC analysis requires a series of time- and resource-consuming sample collections and pre-treatments before instrumental analysis. Air samples collected in adsorbent tubes or canisters are then analyzed using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) in an adequately established laboratory (Song et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2018b; Stemmler et al., 2005).

Most tunnel studies have been conducted based on fixed-point measurements, and EFs are estimated using the concentration difference between the sampling points (Song et al., 2018b; Huang et al., 2017; Ho et al., 2009; Hwa et al., 2002; Pierson et al., 1996). Selecting an ideal sampling location is difficult because of limitations such as the power supply to the instrument, safety issues, and space constraints inside the tunnel. Sampling may also be affected by external influences near the tunnel entrance and exit. Some studies have estimated EFs through tunnel measurements made using a mobile laboratory (Brimblecombe et al., 2015; Perkins et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2005). For example, Brimblecombe et al. (2015) estimated EFs using the fuel carbon balance principle.

In this study, we measured the concentration changes in traffic-related air pollutants inside an urban roadway tunnel in Busan, Korea, using a mobile laboratory equipped with high-resolution equipment. A new EF estimation method for vehicle exhaust emissions was then proposed using the concentration gradient in the tunnel. The new method

was compared with the conventional fixed two-point method (Pierson et al., 1996) to verify its applicability. The newly developed EF estimation methodology reflects actual vehicle driving conditions.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study site

A field campaign was conducted at the Sujungsan Tunnel (SJT) in the central urban area of Busan  $(35^{\circ}8'22''\mathrm{N}, 129^{\circ}2'31''\mathrm{E})$ . The tunnel has two bores with two lanes in each direction with relatively low slopes  $(+1.65\%)$ . The total length and cross-sectional area of SJT are  $2350\mathrm{m}$  and  $61.7\mathrm{m}^2$ , respectively, and the vehicle speed limit is  $70\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ . Because the tunnel connects the Port of Busan and the Jungang Expressway, heavy flows of heavy-duty diesel trucks (HDDT) occur throughout the day. During the sampling period, in-tunnel meteorological parameters such as temperature, wind speed, and direction were measured using a 3-D sonic anemometer (CSAT3, Campbell Scientific, Logan, UT, USA) installed at a distance of  $1000\mathrm{m}$  from the entrance of the tunnel. The average temperature and humidity during the sampling period were  $14.8 \pm 0.9^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  and  $37.5 \pm 19.7\%$ , respectively, and the average horizontal wind speed was  $3.3\mathrm{ms}^{-1}$  inside the tunnel. The ventilation systems, including ventilation ducts and mixing fans, were not operational during the measurement periods, thereby minimizing the escape of vehicular emissions.

Traffic data were collected from the SJT management office. The SJT is a toll tunnel, with all passing vehicles recorded according to their type and tonnage. The results of the vehicle type classification were as follows: subcompact cars  $(7.3\%)$ , passenger cars  $(84.0\%)$ , large trucks  $(1.1\%)$ , and extra-large trucks  $(7.7\%)$ . Vehicle fuel-type data were calculated based on the number of vehicles counted using vehicle statistical data for Busan in 2019 (Korean Statistical Information Service, 2019). The vehicle fuel-type classification yielded the following results: gasoline-fueled vehicles (GVs;  $55\%$ ), diesel-fueled vehicles (DVs;  $36\%$ ) and liquefied petroleum gas (LPG)-fueled vehicles (LPGVs;  $8\%$ ). There was a higher proportion of DVs than in many previous studies, which reflected the characteristics of the Korean automobile market, in which vehicles with a high fuel efficiency are preferred. Because city buses and motorcycles are prevented from entering the tunnel, the LPGVs in the study were mostly commercial taxis. The average traffic volume was  $1817 \pm 77$  vehicles·h $^{-1}$  (Fig. 1).

# 2.2. Field measurements

The monitoring campaign was conducted over 4 days on December 10, 11, 26, and 27, 2019. Mobile measurements were repeated 12 times between 14:00 and 17:30 for the first three days, and stationary measurements were taken on the 27th at the emergency stopping zones of 550, 1050, and  $1700\mathrm{m}$  from the tunnel entrance. The stationary measurements were conducted for  $1\textrm{h}$  at each point from 14:00 to 15:00, 15:00 to 16:00, and 16:00 to 17:00, respectively, next to the traffic lane (Fig. 1). Vehicle emissions were measured for  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , NO,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ , and VOCs.  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  was measured by cavity ring-down spectroscopy (CRDS; G2201-I, Picarro, Santa Clara, CA, USA).  $\mathrm{NO - NO_2 - NO_X}$  and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  were measured using individual gas analyzers (Model 42C and Model 43C, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA).  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  and  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  were measured using two DustTrakTM devices equipped with different impactor sizes (Model 8530, TSI, Shoreview, MN, USA). The details of the instrumentation, analysis mode, and time resolution of SIFT-MS are provided in the Supporting Information (Text S1) and Table S1. The other instruments used for gaseous and particulate species are well-established and have been widely used in various stationary and mobile air quality monitoring studies (Loh et al., 2023); hence, the details about their instrumentation have not been described here. Forty-nine species of VOC were measured using selected ion flow tube-mass spectrometry (SIFT-MS; Voice 200 Ultra, Syft Technologies,

Christchurch, New Zealand). The performance of the SIFT-MS was validated every day before monitoring using calibration standards. Information about the target VOCs and quality assurance/control (QA/QC) results is provided in Text S2 and Table S2.

It is important to align the time lag between the air sample intake from the inlet and the response time of each instrument during mobile measurements (Perkins et al., 2013). Response time differences can be caused by differences in the length of the sampling tubing and analysis time of each instrument. To calibrate the response time differences, the time lag for each instrument was determined using  $\mathrm{N}_2$  gas at the inlets of each instrument. The lag times for the DustTrak, SIFT-MS, gas analyzers, and CRDS were 0, 30, 60, and  $120\mathrm{s}$ , respectively. All instruments were powered by a generator mounted inside a mobile laboratory. The air sample inlet was installed on the rooftop of the mobile laboratory, approximately  $3\mathrm{m}$  above the ground level. This placement helped to avoid direct exposure to exhaust emissions from vehicles in front. To further minimize any potential impact of high emitters, we carefully reviewed the raw data and dashcam footage. However, we did not observe any transient spikes during the measurements. Additionally, we derived concentration profiles by averaging data from 12 transect profiles. This approach helped to mitigate any direct effects of emissions from high emitters. The inhaled air sample was distributed to each instrument through a manifold system using a 1/4-inch polytetrafluoroethylene tube. The traveling speed of the mobile laboratory during monitoring was maintained at less than  $40\mathrm{km}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ .

# 2.3. Modification of the EF equation

The fleet-average EF for vehicles passing through tunnels is typically calculated using Equation (1) (Pierson et al., 1996; Pierson and Brachaczek, 1982):

$$
E F = \left(\left(C _ {\text {o u t l e t}} - C _ {\text {i n l e t}}\right) \cdot A \cdot v \cdot T\right) / (L \cdot N) \tag {1}
$$

where  $EF$  ( $\mathrm{mg} \cdot \mathrm{km}^{-1} \cdot \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1}$ ) is the average EF;  $C_{outlet}$  and  $C_{inlet}$  represent the mass concentrations of target compounds at the outlet and inlet of the tunnel, respectively;  $A$  ( $\mathrm{m}^2$ ) is the cross-sectional area of the tunnel;  $\nu$  ( $\mathrm{m} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ ) is the horizontal wind speed in the tunnel;  $T$  ( $s$ ) is the sampling time;  $L$  ( $\mathrm{km}$ ) is the distance between the two sampling sites; and  $N$  (vehicles) is the number of vehicles that pass through the

sampling points during the sampling period.

This method of estimating EFs using the concentration difference between two points in the tunnel can underestimate or overestimate the EF depending on the measurement location (e.g.,  $C_{\text{inlet}}$  can be influenced by ambient air) (Lee et al., 2021). In addition, it is difficult to set up instruments in the desired location due to power supply or safety issues in the tunnel. In this study, mobile measurements were conducted to overcome the various shortcomings of stationary measurements at fixed locations. This approach provided time- and space-integrated data, which contributed to improvements in the EF calculation method. Initially, to minimize external influences such as mixing and photochemical reactions, we selected a starting point in the tunnel based on concentration profiles of  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ , and  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{X}}$ . Secondly, we calculated the concentration changes in the tunnel, represented by the slope (concentration gradient), by averaging 12 repeated measurements. Thirdly, we used the calculated slope to modify the equation for calculating EF. In Equation (1),  $(C_{\text{outlet}} - C_{\text{inlet}}) / L$  is the change in concentration with distance,  $\Delta C / \Delta L$ . This term was replaced by the slope "m"  $(\mathrm{mg} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{-3} \mathrm{~km}^{-1})$ . The modified equation was as follows:

$$
E F _ {\text {m o d i f i e d}} = m \cdot (A \cdot v \cdot T / N) \tag {2}
$$

According to previous studies, DVs account for  $87\%$  of the  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions from vehicles, with diesel trucks, diesel passenger cars, and diesel buses accounting for  $57\%$ ,  $22\%$ , and  $8\%$ , respectively (Nurrohim and Sakugawa, 2005). Compared with the statistics from other countries (proportion of DVs:  $1.6 - 5.6\%$ ) (Zhang et al., 2020), the proportion of DVs was relatively high, which contributed to the high mixing ratio and EF for  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  in the tunnel.

The NO and  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  concentrations increased from 82.9 to 1520 ppb and 43.9-178 ppb, respectively. Similar to the  $\mathrm{CO}_2$  profile, there were slow rates of increase in the NO and  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  concentrations in the mixing zone and rapid increases in the accumulation zone (Fig. 2c and d). The rate of increase of NO in the accumulation zone  $(763\mathrm{ppb}\mathrm{km}^{-1})$  was 3.3 times higher than that in the mixing zone  $(230\mathrm{ppb}\mathrm{km}^{-1})$ . This large rate of increase was more pronounced for  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , for which the rate of increase changed from 11.1 to  $72.7\mathrm{ppb}\mathrm{km}^{-1}$ . The  $\mathrm{NO / NO_X}$  ratio in the mixing and accumulation zone was 0.66 and 0.89, respectively. Considering that more than  $95\%$  of the  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  was emitted as NO, which is rapidly oxidized to  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  in the presence of  $\mathrm{O_3}$  (Notario et al., 2012), the drastic changes in the  $\mathrm{NO / NO_X}$  ratio suggested that chemical reactions were occurring together with physical mixing in the mixing zone.

In contrast to  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ , and  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{X}}$ , the  $\mathrm{O}_{3}$  profile indicated a decreasing trend from the entrance to the exit of the tunnel. The  $\mathrm{O}_{3}$  concentration decreased until around  $1200\mathrm{m}$ , and then remained at zero to the exit (Fig. 2e). According to (Nihill et al., 2021), the  $\mathrm{NO} / \mathrm{NO}_{2}$  ratio plays an important role in the atmospheric oxidation process. Inside the tunnel, a high  $\mathrm{NO} / \mathrm{NO}_{2}$  ratio (mixing zone: 1.89, accumulation zone: 8.09) may have caused different chemical reactions to those occurring in ambient air ( $\mathrm{NO} / \mathrm{NO}_{2}$  ratio: 0.1-1).

The  $\sum$  VOCs concentration increased as the distance increased,

fluctuating from 306 to 364 ppb, with the alkane group dominating. Interestingly, the  $\sum \mathrm{VOCs}$  concentration decreased near the entrance and exit of the tunnel (Fig. 2f), where VOCs might be suspected to participate in chemical reactions. Gasoline-fueled vehicles (GVs) mostly emit VOCs, whereas DVs mostly emit intermediate VOCs and semi-VOCs (Lu et al., 2018). The relatively low concentration and EF of VOCs in this study could be attributed to the high proportion of DVs in the tunnel. In addition, VOC emissions are affected by driving conditions (e.g., high emissions under low-speed driving conditions) (Huang et al., 2020). Because the mobile measurements were conducted between 14:00 and 17:30 to avoid rush-hour traffic, the average speed of the vehicles in the tunnel was over  $40\mathrm{kmh}^{-1}$ , resulting in low VOC emissions.

The average  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  and  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentrations were in the ranges of 41.0-87.5 and  $23.5 - 41.3\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$ , respectively. The PM concentrations varied substantially compared with the other pollutants (Fig. 2g and h). The maximum  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  concentration  $(188\mu \mathrm{g}\mathrm{m}^{-3})$  was observed when the mobile laboratory passed next to a HDDV, indicating that PM can be strongly affected by the number of HDDVs on the road, which accounted for a relatively high proportion  $(9\%)$  of the traffic volume in the SJT. Non-combustion PM sources in the road environment include tire wear, brake abrasion, and resuspended dust (Abu-Allaban et al., 2003). Because photochemical reactions are rare in tunnels, the main contributors of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  were primary sources, i.e., elemental carbon and primary organic aerosols from vehicle emissions and small particles from non-combustion emissions. The  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5} / \mathrm{PM}_{10}$  ratio remained relatively constant during the campaign (approximately 0.5), indicative of the mixed effects of vehicle combustion and non-combustion sources. Therefore, the intermittent increases in the  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  concentration due to HDDVs was likely caused by tire-road interactions (such as the resuspension of road dust, tire wear, and other factors). # 3.2. EFs calculated using a concentration gradient

Using the modified EF equation (Equation (2)), the EFs were calculated for the target pollutants. The EF of  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ $(\mathrm{EF\_CO}_2)$  in the SJT was  $149,000 \pm 16,800 \mathrm{mg} \mathrm{km}^{-1} \bullet$  vehicle $^{-1}$  (average  $\pm$  standard deviation).  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  accounted for  $>99\%$  of vehicle emissions. The contributions of pollutants excluding  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  followed the order of (unit:  $\mathrm{mg} \bullet \mathrm{km}^{-1} \bullet$  vehicle $^{-1}$ ) NO  $(380 \pm 92.7; 77\%) > \mathrm{NO}_{2}$ $(55.5 \pm 22.8; 11\%) > \mathrm{SO}_{2}$ $(29.2 \pm 5.31; 6\%) > \sum \mathrm{VOCs}$ $(16.7 \pm 22.1; 3\%) > \mathrm{PM}_{10}$ $(9.64 \pm 8.47; 2\%) > \mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ $(4.33 \pm 4.07; 1\%)$  (Fig. 3a). As expected, the largest emissions after  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  in the SJT were for  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{X}}$ , and the  $\mathrm{NO} / \mathrm{NO}_{2}$  emission ratio was 6.85. Among the  $\sum \mathrm{VOCs}$  (unit:  $\mathrm{mg} \bullet \mathrm{km}^{-1} \bullet$  vehicle $^{-1}$ ), alkanes contributed most of the emissions (11.8;  $70.8\%$ ), followed by oxygenated compounds (2.06;  $12.4\%$ ), alkenes (1.32;  $7.93\%$ ), and halogens (1.11;  $6.68\%$ ) (Fig. 3b).

The concentrations and calculated EFs from the mobile measurements were compared with those from stationary measurements for method validation (Fig. 4, Fig. S2). Stationary measurements were conducted for  $90\mathrm{min}$  at three spatial intervals (Table S4). The average traffic volume and fleet composition during stationary measurements did not change significantly; thus, we used the same value as for mobile measurements. The EFs from the stationary measurements were calculated using Equation (1).

Interestingly, the slopes agreed well with each other, with different y-intercepts between the two methods for all pollutants (Fig. 4). The EFs of pollutants were well matched between the two methods. For the EFs of the major exhaust gases (e.g.,  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  and NO) there were no significant differences of less than  $10\%$  between the two measurement modes.  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$  and VOCs also matched well when long-distance measurements were considered. For PM, the EFs of  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  were similar in the two methods, and the EFs of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  showed acceptable differences between the two methods. The concentrations of target pollutants reflected the aerodynamic characteristics of a tunnel environment. All gaseous components, including  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ , NO, and VOCs, had higher concentrations in stationary rather than mobile measurements, while the PM results revealed the opposite tendency.

Turbulence generated by vehicle movement is called vehicle-induced turbulence (VIT) (Xiang et al., 2019; Kalthoff et al., 2005). In high-concentration-gradient regions, such as street canyons and roadway tunnels, VIT can significantly influence exhaust pollutant accumulation patterns (Shi et al., 2019). Laminar (i.e., streamline) flow is dominant in railway tunnels because the train fills most of the tunnel area and travels at relatively high speeds. In roadway tunnels, turbulent flow seldom occurs because of the large unoccupied area and relatively slow movement of vehicles (Niu et al., 2020). A more detailed discussion of the aerodynamics inside roadway tunnels is beyond the scope of this study and further research is needed.

# 3.3. Comparison with other studies

Fleet composition (by fuel type) had the most significant effects on the EFs of vehicle exhaust pollutants. The average EF of  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ $(149,000\pm$ $16,800\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\bullet \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  in this study was more than two times lower than the value reported in the Shing Mun Tunnels in Hong Kong, China  $(310,000\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\bullet \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  (Ho et al., 2009), and Zhujiang Tunnel in Guangzhou, China  $(390,000\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\bullet \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  (Zhang et al., 2015). Both tunnels were strongly affected by the emissions from LPG-fueled vehicles (LPGVs), which emit higher  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  concentrations than GVs and DVs (Ristovski et al., 2005). The governing factor affecting  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  emissions is the proportion of DVs (Tan et al., 2021). The average EF of  $\mathrm{NO_X}$ $(435\pm 115\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\bullet \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  was relatively high in this study compared to other studies. For instance, in the Wujinglu Tunnel (Tianjin, China), the high proportion of GVs  $(94.2\%)$  that operated under strict regulation (China IV-V emission standards) resulted in the  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  EF being as low as one-fifth of that determined in this study  $(83.7\mathrm{mg}$ $\mathrm{km}^{-1}\cdot \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  (Song et al., 2018a, 2018b). The EF of  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  was also higher in this study than in other studies (Table 1) due to the relatively higher proportion of DVs.

Among studies, VOC EFs display clear decreasing trends over time, except for in the Shing Mun Tunnel. The Loma Larga Tunnel (Monterrey, Mexico) had the highest reported  $\sum$  VOC EF  $(1160\mathrm{mgkm}^{-1}\cdot \mathrm{vehicle}^{-1})$  (Mancilla et al., 2012), followed by the results from other tunnel studies in different years (Table 1). The Loma Larga and Wujinglu Tunnels had similar proportions of GVs, but the latter had an EF that was more than 20 times lower than the former, reflecting the strict regulations for vehicle emissions in recent years (Table 1). Although the present study measured a larger number of VOC species, it produced the lowest  $\sum$  VOC EF among the published values, which was likely due to the high proportion of strictly regulated DVs.

The dimensions of the tunnel and monitoring location also affect the EFs of vehicle exhaust emissions in tunnels. For instance, in the Zhujiang Tunnel, most of the measured pollutants had higher EFs than reported in other studies (Table 1). In particular, the  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  EF in the Zhujiang Tunnel was three times higher than that of this study, which could not be explained by vehicle composition and other factors. The traffic flow in the SJT contained three times more DVs (35%) than the Zhujiang Tunnel. The length of the Zhujiang Tunnel is relatively short (721 m), and the sampling inlet was installed  $50\mathrm{m}$  from the tunnel entrance. Considering that the mixing effects of ambient air extended for over 500 m into the tunnel in our study, the Zhujiang Tunnel was not long enough to avoid such effects, which presumably resulted in an overestimation of exhaust EFs.

# 4. Atmospheric implications

The reconstructed real-world emissions from this study were compared with the 2019 national emission inventory in Korea, the Clean Air Policy Support System (CAPSS) (Table 2). The annual vehicle kilometers traveled and the number of registered vehicles were used to calculate the total on-road vehicle emissions in Busan (Korean Statistical Information Service, 2019). As mentioned in Section 2.1, compressed natural gas buses and motorcycles do not travel through the tunnel; therefore, their emissions were subtracted from the total CAPSS emissions before the comparison. There were significant differences in total vehicle emissions between the two approaches. The CAPSS-based calculation produced  $\mathrm{NO_X}$ ,  $\sum$  VOCs, and PM annual emission rates that were 1.6-3.5 times higher than the values from our tunnel study. In contrast, the  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  annual emission rate was more than 47 times higher in this study than in the national inventory. These results have important

In Korea, vehicle emissions are managed by the Clean Air Conservation Act (CACA). In addition, the Korean emission standards for vehicle exhaust are based on the Euro Emission Standards, with the Euro 6 standard applied to all vehicle models since 2015. The Euro 6 standard is much stricter than Euro 5 (e.g.,  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  emissions are set at one-fifth of the Euro 5 value). This has encouraged the use of low-emission engines and adoption of exhaust gas reduction devices, which has led to significant reductions in  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  emissions. This study demonstrated the effectiveness of stricter emission standards under real driving conditions. The calculated annual emission rate of  $\mathrm{NO_X}$  in Busan  $(13,155\mathrm{ton}\cdot \mathrm{year}^{-1})$  by CAPSS was 1.6 times higher than the estimate based on this study (8196  $\pm 2173$  ton-yr $^{-1}$ ). Moreover, the VOC annual emission rate was about 3.5 times higher based on CAPPS  $(1093$  ton-yr $^{-1}$ ) than this study (314 ton-yr $^{-1}$ ). Finally,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  differed by only 1.1 times, while  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  differed by 2.3 times.

Interestingly, the  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  results showed unexpected deviations from emission reduction policy, indicating the mixed effects of fuel and vehicle types. Since 2015, all refineries in Korea have been supplying ultra-low-sulfur diesel and gasoline oil ( $<10\mathrm{ppm}$ ), but the CACA has relatively loose sulfur content standards for LPG and LNG ( $<40\mathrm{ppm}$ ). Considering the relatively high proportion of HDDVs (9%) and LPGVs (8%) in Busan and their high fuel consumption rate compared with light-duty vehicles, these vehicles might make a significant contribution to the high  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions in Busan. Most of the  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  sources in Korea have been traced to coal power plants and ship emissions. The results of this study provide guidance for updating the source apportionment of  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , especially in Busan where the contribution of ship emissions has been designated a major source (Khan et al., 2017).

# 5. Conclusion

This study determined the usefulness and advantages of making mobile measurements of exhaust pollutants in a tunnel study. The safety issues and logistical difficulties in setting up analytical instrumentation inside a tunnel were overcome using a mobile laboratory. The heavy traffic in the urban tunnel did not enable sufficient room for the instrumentation and researchers to operate; thus, safety issues affected the operation of the research. Multipoint data generated by online instruments in a mobile laboratory can also help to overcome the various uncertainties associated with stationary measurements. This study identified a zonation inside the tunnel, i.e., mixing and accumulation zones, and recommended that the mixing zone be avoided to ensure accurate EF measurements. The concept of a concentration gradient was applied for EF calculations. The EF results from the mobile measurements were well matched with those from stationary measurements, while the absolute concentration differences between them suggested that further studies are required to assess the complex aerodynamic movements of the pollutants inside the tunnel. The proposed mobile measurement approach has potential for use in future tunnel studies. Because vehicle exhaust is recognized as one of the main sources of primary and secondary PM in large cities worldwide, there is an increasing need for more frequent and region-specific measurements of EFs in real environments for timely policymaking. Tunnel studies with mobile measurements can provide the required information.

# ABSTRACT

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are important contributors to atmospheric chemical reactivity rate  $(\mathrm{L_{OH}})$ , ozone formation potential (OFP), and secondary organic aerosol formation potential (SOAP). The purpose of this study was to determine the contributions of different VOC sources to  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP using positive matrix factorization (PMF) model. Hourly observation of ambient VOCs at a megacity of Central China was conducted for one month in winter. The  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP of VOC species were firstly estimated. The PMF model was then used to apportion their sources. Five VOC sources including solvent usage, gasoline evaporation, combustion sources, vehicle emissions, and industrial sources were apportioned with their average contributions of  $11.6\%$ ,  $14.2\%$ ,  $39.0\%$ ,  $22.5\%$ , and  $12.6\%$ , respectively. Industrial sources were the highest contributors to the  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$  and SOAP with average contributions of  $32.2\%$  and  $32.8\%$ , respectively, while vehicle emissions contributed most to the OFP  $(27.8\%)$ . This study highlighted that the VOCs and ozone pollution control should give priority to the reactivity (OFP and SOAP) based strategies rather than concentration contribution-based strategies.

# 1. Introduction

Urban areas in China always suffer from serious haze problems due to high fine particle  $\mathrm{(PM_{2.5})}$  concentration during wintertime (Zhang and Cao, 2015; Yin et al., 2017; Song et al., 2017). Organic matter (OM) is one of the most important chemical components of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  during winter haze episodes. During the long-lasting haze period in 2013, OM accounted for  $30.5 - 48.0\%$  of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  masses and secondary organic-rich sources contributed  $12.2 - 25.8\%$  to  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  at four megacities (Huang et al., 2014). As important precursor gases of secondary organic aerosol (SOA), high concentrations of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) were also observed in haze episodes (Zhang et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2017; Han et al., 2017). SOA is mainly formed by atmospheric oxidation and condensation of low-volatility VOCs from both anthropogenic and biogenic sources (Hallquist et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017). The urban ambient VOCs are from anthropogenic activities such as oil and natural gas operations (Gilman et al., 2013; Zheng et al., 2018), fossil fuel combustion (Rodolfo Sosa et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2017), solvent use (Yuan et al., 2010), and chemical manufacturing (Mo et al.,

2017; Zheng et al., 2019), etc. During winter haze periods, which VOC source plays a more important role in the formation of SOA deserves a clear illustration.

Currently, stringent controls on VOCs are needed to mitigate  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution in winter (Huang et al., 2014). The estimation of ozone formation potential (OFP) and SOA of each VOC source is vital before cost-effective strategies can be carried out. Indeed, many researches have been done on determining the most reactive VOC sources. Generally, there are two methods to estimate the OFP or SOA formation potential (SOAP) of each VOC source. One is the speciated VOC emission inventory (Zheng et al., 2009; Liang et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2017; Wu and Xie, 2017) and the other refers to the field measurement of VOC profiles from source emissions. The emission inventory method is sensitive to emission factors, and most of these speciated inventory studies in China always adopted emission factors from studies conducted in foreign countries or other regions, which had large uncertainty (Zhang et al., 2013; Wang, 2014; Wu and Xie, 2017). The local VOC emission inventory is still insufficient (Li et al., 2017), mainly due to the complexity of obtaining emission factors and accurate source activity data.

Meanwhile, the source-specific measurements and emission inventory establishing works are costly. In practice, the estimation of photochemical characteristics (i.e., OFP) with measured VOC source profiles is continuously being reported. For instance, the OFP of gasoline evaporation (Zhang et al., 2013), solvent usage (Wang, 2014), non-coal emission (He et al., 2015), petroleum refinery emission (Zhang et al., 2017), and thermal power plant (Yan et al., 2017) have been done. However, the lack of synchronous measurements of various sources may obstruct the understanding which source contributing most to the ambient ozone and SOA formation.

To identify the ambient VOC sources, positive matrix factorization (PMF) is widely used (Lyu et al., 2016; Abeleira et al., 2017; Bari and Kindzierski, 2018; Zheng et al., 2018). It doesn't need the VOC source profiles, and only observation data and measurement uncertainty are needed in this model. PMF model can give the contribution of each source. Source profiles of each identified factor are useful for further research. For instance, gasoline emission and evaporated liquid gasoline contributed most to the OFP in Beijing with the PMF model (Yuan et al., 2009). Similarly, fuel evaporation held the highest contribution to the OFP at an oil and gas station (Zheng et al., 2018). For the VOCs reactivity, Abeleira et al. (2017) found that the long-lived oil and natural gas (ONG-long lived) source owned the largest contribution to OH radical loss rate  $(\mathrm{LOH})$  for both spring and summer in northern Colorado. However, which VOC source has the highest contribution to SOAP is still less addressed (Mo et al., 2017).

Therefore, this study attempted to comprehensively estimate the reactivity, OFP, and SOAP of different ambient VOC sources by the PMF model. The hourly ambient VOCs monitoring data during wintertime from a megacity in Central China was adopted. The levels, compositions, and diurnal variations of VOCs were firstly studied.  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP of VOC species were then calculated. The VOC sources were identified with the PMF model. The  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP of each identified VOC source were further quantified. Finally, the differences in source variations between clean and pollution episodes were discussed.

# 2. Methodology

# 2.1. VOCs sampling and analysis

From 22th January to 21th February 2017, 57 Photochemical Assessment Monitor Station (PAMS) VOC species were continuously monitored at the rooftop (about  $18\mathrm{m}$  above the ground level) of Hubei Environmental Monitoring Centre  $(114.35^{\circ}\mathrm{E}, 30.52^{\circ}\mathrm{N})$ . The sampling site is located in an urban commercial/residential region. Ambient VOCs were continuously sampled and analyzed at 1-h resolution by a gas chromatograph (GC) equipped with a flame ionization detector (FID) and a mass spectrometer detector (MSD), developed by Wuhan Tianhong Instrument Inc. (TH-300B). The water vapour and carbon dioxide in the sampled air were removed using a cold trap maintaining at  $-80^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The purified air sample was then concentrated at  $-150^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  with an empty capillary column. After pre-concentration, the temperature was raised to  $100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  rapidly and the desorbed VOCs were introduced into the GC-FID/MS for further analysis. The  $\mathrm{C}_2\sim \mathrm{C}_5$  VOCs were analyzed by FID and  $\mathrm{C}_6\sim \mathrm{C}_{12}$  were measured by MSD. The target compounds were quantified with the calibration curves established from standard samples. The correlation coefficients of calibration curves were generally higher than 0.99. Details about the quality assurance/control and the detection limits can be found elsewhere (Lyu et al., 2016; Zheng et al., 2018). Briefly, the instrument was calibrated using a known standard gas every day at 00:00 local time and the standard curves were calibrated using the standard gases every week. The external gases were used to calibrate  $\mathrm{C}_2\sim \mathrm{C}_5$  species, while the internal gases were employed for the calibration of  $\mathrm{C}_6\sim \mathrm{C}_{12}$  species. The method detection limitations of VOC species are listed in Table S1 (see supplementary materials). Due to the weekly and daily instrument calibration and instrument maintenance, 689 samples were available in this study.

# 2.2. Correction of measured VOCs

A time-resolved approach was used to evaluate the influence of photochemical processing on the measured VOC levels since emissions (Yuan et al., 2013; Ait-Helal et al., 2014; Han et al., 2017; He et al., 2019). The first-order kinetic reaction was used to describe the relationship for VOCs reacting with OH- radical:

$$
- \frac {d V O C _ {i}}{d t} = k _ {V O C i} \times \left[ V O C _ {i} \right] \times [ H O ] \tag {1}
$$

where  $k_{\mathrm{VOCi}}$  is the rate constant of the reaction of  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  VOC with OH radicals,  $\mathrm{cm}^3$  (molecule  $\times \mathrm{s})^{-1}$ ; [HO-] is the concentration of HO- radicals, molecule  $(\mathrm{cm}^3)^{-1}$ . The initial concentration of  $\mathrm{VOC}_i$  is determined by Eq. (2):

$$
\mathrm {V O C} _ {\mathrm {i}, \text {i n i t a l}} = \left[ \mathrm {V O C} _ {\mathrm {i}, \mathrm {t}} \right] \times \exp \left(\mathrm {k} _ {\mathrm {V O C} \mathrm {i}} \times [ \mathrm {H O} \cdot ] \times \Delta \mathrm {t}\right) \tag {2}
$$

where  $\mathrm{VOC}_{\mathrm{i},\mathrm{initial}}$  and  $\mathrm{VOC}_{\mathrm{i},\mathrm{t}}$  are the initial and measured concentrations of  $\mathrm{VOC}_{\mathrm{i}}$ , respectively;  $\Delta t$  is the time elapsed since emission.

The product of  $[\mathrm{HO}\cdot ]$  radicals and  $\Delta t$  are derived from the photochemical age, usually by selecting two VOCs that have very different  $k_{\mathrm{VOCi}}$  values (Ait-Helal et al., 2014). In this study,  $m,p$ -xylenes (X) and ethylbenzene (E) were selected. Hence,  $[\mathrm{HO}\cdot ]\times \Delta t$  was calculated from Eq. (3):

$$
[ H O. ] \times \Delta t = \frac {1}{k _ {V O C , X} - k _ {V O C , E}} \left[ \ln \left(\frac {X}{E}\right) \Bigg | _ {t = 0} - \ln \left(\frac {X}{E}\right) \Bigg | _ {t = t} \right] \tag {3}
$$

where  $k_{\mathrm{VOC},X}$  and  $k_{\mathrm{VOC},E}$  are the reaction rates of  $m$ ,  $p$ -xylenes and ethylbenzene with OH radical, respectively;  $\ln (\mathrm{X / E})$ $|t = 0$  and  $\ln (\mathrm{X / E})$ $t = t$  are the ratios of  $\mathrm{X / E}$  for primary emissions and after emissions, respectively. In this study, the  $(\mathrm{X / E})$ $|t = 0$  is calculated according to the  $95\%$  percentile of  $\mathrm{X / E}$  ratios.

Fig. 1 shows the scatter plot between the measured and initial VOC concentrations. The ratios of the initial to measured levels for alkanes, alkenes, and aromatics were  $1.13 \pm 0.12$  (mean  $\pm$  standard deviation),  $7.90 \pm 10.3$ , and  $2.58 \pm 2.37$ , respectively. Large standard deviations of alkenes and aromatics reflected diversity in the reactivity of some species of them. It also suggested that species with higher react rates with OH radical such as isoprene ( $k_{\mathrm{OH}} = 100 \times 10^{12} \mathrm{~cm}^{-3}$  molecule  $\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ ), trans-2-pentene ( $k_{\mathrm{OH}} = 67 \times 10^{12} \mathrm{~cm}^{-3}$  molecule  $\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ ), and styrene ( $k_{\mathrm{OH}} = 58 \times 10^{12} \mathrm{~cm}^{-3}$  molecule  $\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ ) were more impacted by photochemical processes.

# 2.3. VOC source apportionment

Positive matrix factorization (PMF) model is widely used in ambient VOCs source apportionment (Bari and Kindzierski, 2018; Zheng et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2019). It can produce a better fit for the data and provide non-negative factors, error estimates, and better data treatment including handling missing values and values below the detection limits (Paatero and Tapper, 1994). Briefly, the PMF model decomposes a receptor sample matrix  $(i \times j$  dimension) into contribution matrix  $(i \times k)$  and factor matrix  $(k \times j)$ :

$$
x _ {i j} = \sum_ {k = 1} ^ {p} g _ {i k} \times f _ {k j} + e _ {i j} \tag {4}
$$

where  $x_{\mathrm{ij}}$  is the contribution of  $j^{\mathrm{th}}$  species in  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  sample;  $g_{\mathrm{ik}}$  is the contribution of  $k^{\mathrm{th}}$  source in  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  sample;  $f_{\mathrm{kj}}$  is the concentration of  $j^{\mathrm{th}}$  species in  $k^{\mathrm{th}}$  source;  $e_{\mathrm{ij}}$  is the error fraction. The target of PMF model is to find the global minimum of the following equation:

$$
Q = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {m} \left(\frac {e _ {i j}}{u _ {i j}}\right) \tag {5}
$$

where  $u_{ij}$  is the uncertainty of  $j^{\mathrm{th}}$  species in  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  sample calculated as the following equation:

$$
U n c = \frac {5}{6} \times M D L \tag {6}
$$

$$
\mathrm {U n c} = \sqrt {\left(\text {e r r o r f r a c t i o n} \times \text {c o n c e n t r a t i o n}\right) ^ {2} + \left(0 . 5 \times M D L\right)} \tag {7}
$$

If the concentration is less than corresponding method detection limit (MDL), Eq. (6) is used to calculate the uncertainty; if the concentration is higher than MDL, Eq. (7) is used.

Not all measured species were input into the PMF model in this study. There are some rules to choose the appropriate VOC species including: (1) highly collinear species are included; (2) species indicating VOC sources are retained; and (3) species with a high percentage below the detection limit  $(>50\%)$  are excluded (Guo et al., 2011; Callén et al., 2014; Shao et al., 2016). The input species and their categories are listed in Table S2. Finally, a dataset of  $689\times 33$  was introduced into the model in this study.

Choosing the optimal factor number is challenging. Too many factors would result in meaningless outputs, while few factor numbers would result in mixed sources (Bressi et al., 2014). In this study, several diagnostic parameters were considered to choose the optimal factor number: (1) correlation coefficients  $(r^2)$  between the observed and predicted values; (2) the fitness of error estimation (expressed by  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{theoretical}}$  and  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{robust}}$ ), and (3) percentage of accepted case in bootstrap-displacement (BS-DISP) (Table S3). As the factor number increasing, the  $r^2$  increased from 0.87 to 0.95, suggesting a better fitting for a higher number factor solution. As shown in Table S3, the  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{robust}}$  ratios in all cases approximately equal to one, suggesting the less impact of the high scaled residuals data point on the solution. For  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{theoretical}}$ , if the factor number is estimated well, this ratio approaches one. As the factor number increasing, the  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{theoretical}}$  ratios decreased. 7-factor and 8-factor solution had the  $Q_{\text{true}}/Q_{\text{theoretical}}$  ratios approaching one. The accepted cases in BS-DISP, however, decreased from 100 to  $73\%$  with the increasing number of factors, suggesting the decreasing robustness. Overall, the 5-factor solution was adopted because it was an appropriate factor number and robustness ( $99\%$  in BS-DISP). Bootstrap-displacement results of the 5-factor solution showed no swaps by factor and the decrease in Q value ( $0.06\%$ ) was less than  $1.0\%$ . These suggested that the solution did not have significant rotational ambiguity and the solution was well constrained. Therefore, the f-peak technic was not used in this study. To better understand the impacts of photochemical processes on VOCs source apportionment results, the initial and measured VOC mixing ratios were separately input into the

PMF model. The PMF results calculated from initial VOC levels (Section 2.2) were reported in this study. The comparison of PMF results from two different datasets was provided in Section 3.5.

# 2.4.  $L_{OH}$ , OFP, and SOAP calculation

VOCs reactivity based on OH radical loss rate was calculated as Abeleira et al. (2017):

$$
L _ {O H} = V O C _ {i} \times k _ {V O C i} \tag {8}
$$

where  $VOC_{i}$  is the mixing ratio of the  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  species;  $k_{\mathrm{VOCi}}$  is the reaction rate of the  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  species with OH radical, which was reported elsewhere (Atkinson and Arey, 2003).

The ozone formation potential of VOCs species was estimated using maximum incremental reactivity (MIR) method:

$$
O F P _ {i} = V O C _ {i} \times M I R _ {i} \tag {9}
$$

where  $VOC_{i}$  and  $MIR_{i}$  are the concentrations and reactivity of each VOC (Carter, 2013).

The SOAP method has been widely used to estimate the contributions of different VOC species to SOA formation, which can be calculated by the following equation (Derwent et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2017):

$$
S O A P _ {i} = \frac {\text {I n c r e m e n t i n S O A m a s s c o n c e n t r a t i o n w i t h s p e c i e s} , i}{\text {I n c r e m e n t i n S O A w i t h t o u e n e}} \times 1 0 0 \tag {10}
$$

where  $SOAP_{i}$  is the SOA formation potential parameter for species  $i$  (unitless), which is summarized in Table S1. The potential of SOA formation for species  $i$ $(P_{SOAP_i})$  can be calculated by the following equation (Zhang et al., 2017):

$$
P _ {S O A P _ {i}} = \frac {V O C _ {i} \times S O A P _ {i}}{1 0 0} \times F A C _ {\text {t o l u e n e}} \tag {11}
$$

where  $VOC_{i}$  is the concentration (ppbC) of the  $i^{\text{th}}$  species;  $FAC_{toluene}$  is the fractional aerosol coefficient of toluene (\%). In this study,  $FAC_{toluene} = 5.4\%$  was used according to Zhang et al. (2017).

# 2.5. Determining the  $L_{OH}$ , OFP, and SOAP of each VOC source

Based on PMF results ( $F$  matrix), the  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP of each source were calculated according to the following equations:

$$
L _ {O H, i k} = g _ {i k} \times \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {3 3} f _ {k j} \times L _ {O H, j} \tag {12}
$$

$$
O F P _ {i k} = g _ {i k} \times \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {3 3} f _ {k j} \times M I R _ {j} \tag {13}
$$

$$
P _ {S O A P _ {i k}} = \frac {g _ {i k} \times \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {3 3} f _ {k j} \times S O A P _ {j} \times F A C _ {t o l u e n e}}{1 0 0} \tag {14}
$$

where  $g_{ik}$  is the contribution of  $k^{\mathrm{th}}$  source in  $i^{\mathrm{th}}$  sample;  $f_{kj}$  is the concentration of  $j^{\mathrm{th}}$  species in  $k^{\mathrm{th}}$  source.  $L_{OH,j}$ ,  $MIR_j$  and  $SOAP_j$  are reaction rates with OH radical, maximum incremental reactivity, and  $SOAP$  of  $j^{\mathrm{th}}$  species, respectively.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. General characteristics

# 3.1.1. Levels, compositions, and diurnal variations of measured VOCs

Table S1 summarizes the statistics of VOC concentrations. These 57 PAMS VOCs can be grouped into alkanes, alkenes, acetylene, and aromatics and their time series are shown in Fig. 2a. The total concentrations of VOCs were  $53.1 \pm 27.6$  ppbv. The average concentrations of alkanes, alkenes, acetylene, and aromatics were  $33.6 \pm 17.3$ ,  $6.55 \pm 4.15$ ,  $1.71 \pm 0.90$ , and  $11.4 \pm 6.79$  ppbv, respectively. They accounted for  $63.7\%$ ,  $12.0\%$ ,  $2.96\%$ , and  $21.3\%$  of total detected VOCs, respectively (Fig. 2b). Propane  $(9.89 \pm 7.16$  ppbv), ethene  $(4.27 \pm 2.06$  ppbv), and benzene  $(3.76 \pm 1.53$  ppbv) were found as the most abundant VOC

species in their groups. Their diurnal variations are shown in Fig. 2c. Generally, the minimum levels in the afternoon were due to the better dispersion condition and strong photochemical reaction (Zheng et al., 2018). During a day, higher levels of VOCs were found during nighttime compared to daytime. VOCs in this study were higher than the annual average values (24.3 ppbv) in Wuhan during 2013-2014 (Lyu et al., 2016) as well as those in Beijing (43.4 ppbv) (Song et al., 2007), Shanghai (32.4 ppbv) (Cai et al., 2010), and Hong Kong (18.5 ppbv) (Guo et al., 2007). This is partly due to the different sampling periods, as the winter period always holds the highest ambient pollutant concentrations. However, compared to the winter campaigns in other regions, the VOC levels in this study were still higher than those reported in Guangzhou (47.6-48.1 ppbv) (Zou et al., 2015), Tianjin (26.9-39.4 ppbv) (Liu et al., 2016), and Nanjing  $(40.2\pm 24.0~\mathrm{ppbv})$  (Zhao et al., 2020). It suggested the relative serious VOC pollution in Wuhan.

# 3.2. Source identification

Fig. 4 shows the source profiles derived from the PMF bootstrap results. The diurnal variations of each source are shown in Fig. 5. The Pearson correlations between VOC source contributions, meteorological parameters, and other air pollutants were analyzed to support the source identification results (Table S5).

Factor 1 was interpreted as solvent usage (SU). This factor was heavily loaded on aromatics such as 1,3,5-trimethylbenzene  $(51.6\pm$ $18.0\%)$  ,o-ethyltoluene  $(34.1\pm 9.48\%)$  , 1,2,4-trimethylbenzene  $(34.2\pm$ $8.01\%)$  , and 1,2,3-trimethylbenzene  $(50.2\pm 13.0\%)$  . These species are associate with solvent usage emission (Ou et al., 2015). The source profiles from surface coating and printing sectors also indicated that aromatics accounted for  $79.5 - 81.4\%$  of VOCs (Shen et al., 2018). A significant positive correlation between the contribution of this factor and temperature was found with  $r = 0.39(p <   0.01)$  , suggesting higher contributions under high temperature due to more fugitive emissions. The diurnal variation of its contribution also peaked in the afternoon with the maximum temperature appearing of a day.

Factor 2 was heavily weighted on ethane  $(56.5\pm 4.23\%)$ , ethene  $(61.8\pm 6.60\%)$ , propane  $(48.7\pm 7.08\%)$ , acetylene  $(43.5\pm 5.15\%)$ , propene  $(48.4\pm 12.1\%)$ , and benzene  $(49.1\pm 6.06\%)$ , which are from combustion processes (Liu et al., 2008). Additionally, a positive correlation  $(r = 0.47,p < 0.01)$  between the contribution of this factor and a combustion tracer-CO was found. Therefore, it was reasonable to attribute this factor to combustion sources (CS). The diurnal variation showed two peaks in the morning (about 09:00 of local time) and evening (21:00) and a minimum value in the afternoon (15:00).

Factor 3 was also heavily loaded on aromatics such as  $o$ -xylene  $(54.3 \pm 7.85\%)$ ,  $m$ ,  $p$ -xylenes  $(52.4 \pm 7.77\%)$ , and  $1,2,4$ -trimethylbenzene  $(39.5 \pm 6.29\%)$ , which are from vehicle emissions or solvent usage (Borbon et al., 2002; Shao et al., 2016). High fractions of propane  $(41.6 \pm 10.4\%)$  and  $\mathrm{C}_4 \sim \mathrm{C}_7$ , however, were also found in this factor, which were from vehicle emission (Liu et al., 2008). Similar source profiles were reported elsewhere (Liu et al., 2008; Dumanoglu et al., 2014; Abeleira et al., 2017), which were mainly regarded as gasoline vehicles. Additionally, the source contribution of this factor showed a positive correlation with the vehicle exhaust tracers such as NO  $(r = 0.56, p < 0.01)$  and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$ $(r = 0.53, p < 0.01)$ . Therefore, this factor was identified as vehicle emissions (VE). The diurnal variation of this source showed two peak values at about 09:00 and 19:00, which were corresponding to the traffic rush hours in the morning and evening, respectively.

Factor 4 was heavily loaded on  $i$ -pentane  $(45.3 \pm 6.77\%)$  and  $n$ -pentane  $(50.0 \pm 6.84\%)$ . These two species are typical tracers of gasoline evaporation (Zhang et al., 2013) and vehicle exhaust (Barletta et al., 2005). # 3.3. Reactivity, OFP, and SOAP of each VOC source

As shown in Fig. 6, industrial sources contributed  $32.2\%$  to the total OH loss rate, followed by solvent usage  $(21.4\%)$ , combustion sources  $(20.4\%)$ , vehicle emissions  $(19.4\%)$ , and gasoline evaporation  $(6.61\%)$  respectively. Usually, vehicle emissions or petrochemical industrial emissions dominate man-made VOC reactivity in urban areas. For instance, motor vehicle emissions (dominated by high fractions of aromatics and alkenes) contributed to  $50\%$  of the calculated VOC reactivity in Pasadena (Borbon et al., 2013). Similarly,  $\mathrm{C}_2 \sim \mathrm{C}_6$  alkenes and aromatics from the petrochemical refinery and motor vehicles accounted for  $42\%$  of the total calculated VOC reactivity  $(7.5~\mathrm{s}^{-1})$  in the Houston/ Galveston Bay region (Gilman et al., 2009). The percentages of vehicle emissions and industrial sources to OH loss rate in this study were lower than those in the two above researches. However, in this study, the VOC reactivity  $(2.79~\mathrm{s}^{-1})$  contributed by vehicle emissions was higher than the traffic-related reactivity  $(1.2~\mathrm{s}^{-1})$  in the Northern Front Range Metropolitan Area of Colorado (Abeleira et al., 2017).

For the OFP of different sources (Fig. 6), vehicle emissions had the largest contribution  $(27.8\%)$ , followed by industrial sources  $(25.4\%)$ , solvent usage  $(20.9\%)$ , combustion sources  $(18.9\%)$ , and gasoline evaporation  $(7.04\%)$ , respectively. The OFP of vehicle emissions in this study was comparable to a previous study in Wuhan that vehicle exhausts accounted for  $23.4 \pm 1.3\%$  of  $\mathrm{O}_3$  formation (Lyu et al., 2016). # 3.4. Differences between clean and pollution episodes

To better understand the differences in levels and reactivity of VOC sources under different air quality, the  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  levels were classified into several groups and the source contributions, OH loss rate, OFP, and SOAP of VOCs were summarized in Fig. 7. Generally, with the increase of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  levels from clean to heavy pollution, the VOC levels also increased from  $40.8 \pm 17.6$  ppbv to  $89.6 \pm 26.3$  ppbv. The same phenomenon was also reported elsewhere (Han et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2017; Hui et al., 2019). Fig. 7 clearly shows that the high  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  pollution was characterized by an increase of industrial sources, which accounted for up to  $27.3\%$ ,  $56.2\%$ ,  $48.4\%$ , and  $57.0\%$  of VOCs mixing ratio, OH loss rate, OFP, and SOAP, respectively. The results here were similar to a previous study that the percentage of industrial sources increased significantly with the increase in VOC levels and this source dominated the VOC origins during an entire severe pollution episode (Hui et al.,

2019). On the contrary, the contributions of combustion sources, vehicle emissions, and solvent usage to OH loss rate, OFP, and SOAP decreased with the increase of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  levels. Despite a decreasing trend of combustion sources to the VOC mixing ratio from clean to pollution, it still dominated the VOC origins during the heavy pollution period. Therefore, to alleviate the VOCs pollution, it should prior control combustion sources. From the perspective of VOC reactivity, OFP, and SOAP, reducing VOC emissions from industrial sources would have benefits to both the reduction of VOC levels and SOA during pollution episodes. The results here contributed to better-developing countermeasures of alleviating VOCs, ozone, and particulate matter pollutions.

# 3.5. Limitations and implications

The limitations of this study were from several aspects: one is the limited known chemistry properties of VOC species; the other is the underestimation of the current modelling; and the third is the assumption of the PMF model. For instance, the ozone reactivity scales for 118 VOC species under different  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  conditions were summarized by Carter (2013). Similarly, Atkinson and Arey (2003) summarized the atmospheric degradation of about 190 VOC species (known as the rate constant  $k_{OH}$ ). A list of the SOAPs relative to toluene for 113 VOC species was also reported by Derwent et al. (2010). Despite the great efforts have been made to study the photochemical characteristics of the VOC species, some VOC species' properties are still unknown. They may be important to the atmospheric reactivity, ozone, and secondary aerosol formation potentials. Limited species (i.e., 33 species in this study) introduced into the PMF model suggested the underestimation of  $\mathrm{L_{OH}}$ , OFP, and SOAP. For SOA modelling, the SOA yield method using chamber study or simulation using volatility basis sets (VBS) underestimated the SOA mass (Chen et al., 2017). For the estimation of OFP, despite the MIR method considers the chemical mechanisms and impacts of the  $\mathrm{VOC} / \mathrm{NO}_x$  ratio on ozone production, it may also introduce simulation uncertainties (Ran et al., 2009).

For the PMF model derived uncertainty, it comes from four assumptions: (1) the source profiles do not change significantly over time; (2) the species do not react chemically or undergo physical processes; (3) the collected data are consistent with the conceptual model and represent the studied geographical area; and (4) equivalent/comparable analytical methods are applied to the receptor site throughout the study (Belis et al., 2013). However, the photochemistry of VOCs in the ambient air made it difficult to separate the sources derived from the PMF model and attention should be paid to the interpretation of the sources derived from the PMF model (Yuan et al., 2012).

# 4. Conclusion

This study reported the levels, compositions, and diurnal variations of VOCs in a megacity of Central China during a winter period. Despite

the flaws and uncertainty existed, this study also provided an additional approach to understand the contributions of various VOC sources to the reactivity of OH loss rate, ozone formation potential (OFP), and secondary organic aerosol potential (SOAP) using the PMF model.

In this study, alkanes contributed most (63.7%) to VOC mixing ratio while aromatics contributed most to the OFP and SOAP with contributions of 49.5% and 98.5%, respectively. The main VOCs sources were combustion sources (39.0%), vehicle emissions (22.5%), gasoline evaporation (14.2%), industrial sources (12.6%), and solvent usage (11.6%). Industrial sources had the highest proportions to the VOC reactivity and SOAP, with average contributions of 32.2% and 32.8%, respectively, while vehicle emissions contributed most to the OFP (27.8%). From clean to pollution episodes, the contributions of combustion sources, vehicle emissions, and solvent usage to OH loss rate, OFP, and SOAP decreased. However, the contributions of industrial sources increased.

This study also tried a time-resolved approach to evaluate the influence of photochemical processes on VOC source apportionment by receptor models. The VOC decay should be considered in the receptor modelling. VOCs contributed both to  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and  $\mathrm{O}_3$  formations. To achieve the reduction of them synchronously in China, it is important to identify the VOC sources with high contributions to SOA and OFP. This study suggested that the priority control on VOC emissions from industrials and vehicle emission would be an effective strategy in alleviating  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and  $\mathrm{O}_3$  pollution in winter.

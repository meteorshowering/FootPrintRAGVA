# ABSTRACT

Coal used for heating rural households poses a severe threat to ambient air pollution and human health. In 2017, China implemented the Winter Clean Heating Pilot (WCHP) project, which was designed to convert energy from "coal-to-gas/electricity". However, the impact of the WCHP project and its associated dynamic changes have not yet been well quantified. Based on daily monitored panel data covering 35 pilot cities, we conducted a difference-in-differences (DID) model to identify whether or not the WCHP project is effective in maintaining air quality. Results showed that AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{x}}$ , and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions decreased by  $20.4\%$ ,  $18.59\%$ ,  $34.1\%$ , and  $68.4\%$ , respectively, and, except for  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , these pollutant reductions were greater in Western China than in Eastern China. Furthermore, when certain variables were controlled, such as weather, holidays, GDP, the ratio between secondary industries, income levels, and environmental expenditures, these results were also robust. Parallel trends and placebo tests were additionally used to confirm the reliability of the DID model. The implementation of energy transition could gain the synergy between emission reduction and air quality improvement. Policy recommendations were finally made on rural-scale energy transition, including reforming the cost of electricity/gas through advanced technology and subsidies to account for regional inequality.

# 1. Introduction

Air pollution is one of the greatest environmental problems the world faces today, leading to significant undesirable effects. Being the greatest environmental risk to health, air pollution threatens human health by increasing the morbidity and mortality of respiratory disease, heart disease, lung cancer, diabetes, etc. [1-3], contributing to estimated 7 million premature deaths each year [4]. Although typical air pollutants in China have decreased significantly in recent years, air pollution in China remains inferior to that experienced on average globally. According to the latest 2018 reports released by the IQAir [5] and Greenpeace [6], China ranks third in  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions and twelfth in annual average  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentrations globally.

The governance of air pollutants has gone from pure pollution control to pollution prevention via energy structure transformation and renewable energy development; hence the countries

implementing intervention measures have seen substantial air quality improvements [7,8]. For example, the United States of America has replaced coal with natural gas in generating electric power since 1997 and has achieved  $44\%$  and  $40\%$  reduction in  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{x}}$  emissions from the 1997 levels [9]. A cleaner heating technologies program in New Zealand has led to the significant decline of PM emissions and concentrations [10]. Such policies aiming to diversify energy structures with clean and renewable energy have been successful in certain countries, which also has important implications for China [11]. In Northern China, the annual consumption of coal used for heating is approximately 400 million metric tons, nearly  $50\%$  of which is separately distributed for use in rural areas. Moreover, the combustion of scattered coal heating has caused severe haze to occur in winter [12]. Chen et al. found that the combustion of scattered coal heating has led to an approximately two-thirds increase in air pollutants during periods when heating is employed (i.e., winter) compared to periods when heating is not employed (i.e., summer) [13]. Compared to coal, clean energies such as natural gas and wind power emit less  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{x}}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ , and  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ , and thus reduce human health risks from exposure to air pollutants [9]. Therefore, switching from scattered coal

heating to clean energy in winter has become the current focus of China's efforts to promote winter pollution control from a household perspective.

Impact evaluations can be used to assess the degree to which changes in outcomes are attributed to an intervention rather than to other factors [14], which is useful for determining the validity of policy. In recent years, regression discontinuity (RD), instrument variables (IV), propensity score matching (PSM), and difference-in-differences (DID) have been widely used to evaluate policies in applied economics research. Regression discontinuity design (RDD) is more similar to a random experiment, but it is difficult to identify and specify a good cutoff point using this technique [15]. Although IV is able to make unbiased estimations, this estimation technique requires stringent exogenous factors to be used as proxies for explanatory variables [16]. Compared to the RDD, PSM, and IV techniques, the DID estimation technique attempts to construct a natural experiment, requires fewer sample assumptions and restrictions, and is effective in controlling omitted variable bias [17].

Studies on the effectiveness of energy transition have not produced a consensus among scholars. Lin and Zhu [18] found that fuel switching in "energy saving and emission reduction" (ESER) policy had failed to decrease  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentrations. Li and Chen [19] reported that a city that underwent coal-to-gas transition had no significant impact on reducing  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions, and this was primarily because the city focused on the replacement of industrial coal while ignoring the combustion of scattered coal heating in rural areas. Additionally, although Zhao and Luo [20] reported that a "coal-to-gas" project decreased pollutant emissions from a household sector by substituting civil urban coal consumption, they confirmed that this project had a limited effect on reducing industrial coal consumption. Liu et al. [21] adopted the GAINS model to examine the effect of coal switching strategy in Jing-Jin-Ji region, and estimated that the primary  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  decreased by  $28\%$  and  $11\%$ , respectively. Wu et al. [22] evaluated the household coal-replacement program in North China and concluded that coal-to-electricity policy achieved the goal of a clean winter, while the high-quality coal replacement policy had no improvement in indoor air quality. Since the  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  (smog) crisis in the winter of 2013, environmental governance in China has shifted attention to scattered coal heating in rural areas, after which a series of policies were implemented and subsequently achieved remarkable results [23]. However, official reports may have overestimated the benefits of the clean heating policy by simply attributing the decrease in air pollutant concentrations to the promotion of clean heating policies while ignoring the interference of other exogenous energy-saving and emission-reduction policies [24]. Additionally, policy impacts will vary in different regions; thus, it is necessary to identify the validity of such policy among regions. Few studies to date have focused on the evaluation of energy transition policies in China. As an important part of China's energy transition process, it must be determined that whether or not the "Winter Clean Heating Pilot" (WCHP) project improves air quality. Answering this question can offer some guidance in China's future environmental governance while also providing a quantitative reference for other coal-based, energy-consuming developing countries. It could also be beneficial to achieve the synergy between emission reduction and air quality improvement under the background of climate change.

This study examines whether the WCHP project has reduced the negative impacts caused by winter heating on air quality in northern cities of China. Based on China's urban daily panel database, covering 35 cities from 2017 to 2018, we used the DID model to evaluate such impacts following the implementation of WCHP project, and the robustness of results was further verified by a parallel trend test and a placebo test. According to air quality improvement findings of fuel switching, we further make

recommendations for the future promotion of this policy. The remainder of this study is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews the development of air pollution control policies in China and the basic features of the WCHP project; Section 3 presents our empirical framework and descriptive statistics; Section 4 presents our estimation results; Section 5 provides robustness checks; Sector 6 summarizes our conclusions.

# 2. Policy background

Compared to western countries, the Chinese governmental response to the accumulated environmental issues was more than a decade behind. Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, environment regulation in China was largely ineffective given that concentration-based controls provided no improvements to deteriorating air quality [19]. Since the 2000s, mass-based emissions have replaced concentration-based controls. During 2016-2012, China implemented the "total emission control on  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and "energy-saving" policies, thus a  $10\%$  reduction goal aimed at  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions has been achieved (Fig. 1). After the 2013  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  winter haze crisis, air pollution control policies have transformed air pollution issues significantly. The transition of the energy system has been adopted as a key component of the sustainable energy transformation strategy, where the concept of "coal-to-gas/electricity" was first proposed. To promote this energy transition initiative, the Government of China first designed a pilot project in heavily polluted regions and made efforts to popularize it if the pilot project proved to be effective. China's household winter heating system was coal-based and technically inefficient. Accordingly, China implemented an energy transition pilot project to reform winter heating (Fig. 1), initiated in the United States of America, the United Kingdom, and other developed countries as references [25,26].

Due to the lower temperatures in Northern China, burning coal for heating had become the most common way in which residents kept warm in winter. Since the 1950s, the Government of China provided winter heating via coal-fired boilers in the north Huai River areas. Northern cities received free unlimited heating from November 15 to March 15. Up until now, heating has not only been an important indicator of social wealth but has also become necessary for the survival of northern people in winter. According to statistical data released by the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development (MOHURD) of the People's Republic of China, the annual growth rate of the urban centralized heating area reached  $14.04\%$  during 1991-2017 (Fig. 2). At the same time, coal consumption increased under the rising demand for heating in the two decades prior to 2013. The "coal-to-gas/electricity" policy has generally altered the previous energy consumption structure. However, the rural heating energy consumption structure has largely remained unbalanced, among which coal consumption accounts for approximately  $75\%$ . Moreover, the large scattered coal heating demand has caused a series of environmental and social problems. The combustion of coal in boilers is associated with the release of air pollutants, especially  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and total suspended particles (TSP). Winter heating has also reduced people's life expectancy in northern regions of China [27]. To meet the increasing demand for heating as well as to alleviate air pollutant emissions, the National Development and Reform Commission of the People's Republic of China announced its plan for the WCHP project (covering 2017-2021).

The WCHP project was first implemented in 2017 to promote clean fuel switching from coal to natural gas, electricity, and other renewable energy. The Government of China selected a total of 35 cities to be used as pilot cities for the WCHP project, which was implemented in two separate parcels. The first parcel (July 23,

According to related reports from local governments, the WCHP project worked effectively within these pilot cities, and air pollutants clearly decreased. However, the reduction of pollutants may be

attributable to other factors beyond the WCHP project during winter. Other pollution control policies, such as ceasing operation of highly-polluting industrial plants and reducing the number of vehicles on the roads, can also help mitigate air pollution. Therefore, it is necessary to quantitatively examine the validity of the WCHP project.

# 3. Methodology and data description

# 3.1. Empirical model

In order to examine the effects of heating on air quality during heating periods, we constructed a generalized baseline dynamic panel data model based on Chen et al. [27] and Beck et al. [17] to measure differences in air quality during heating periods and nonheating periods:

$$
\ln Y _ {i t} = \sum_ {s = 1} ^ {p} \rho \ln Y _ {i, t - s} + \alpha \text {H e a t p e r i o d} _ {i t} + \beta X _ {i t} + \lambda t + \mu_ {i} + \eta_ {t} + \varepsilon_ {i t}
$$

where  $i$  is a regional city, and  $t$  refers to the date.  $\ln Y_{it}$  represents the logarithmic form of the indicators used to measure air pollution ( $Y_{it}$  can be set to AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , or CO). AQI is considered as a comprehensive indicator reflecting ambient air quality [28]. Both  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  and  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  represent the atmospheric particulate pollution characteristics. CO is a common component of air pollution.  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and  $\mathrm{NO}_x$  are two important precursors of secondary  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  [29]. Because air quality variables may be influenced by the lag value, this model contains a lag value of  $\ln Y_{it}$ , and the lagging order is determined by a sequential  $t$ -rule. The critical explanatory variable Heatperiod $_{it}$  is used as a dummy variable, which equals 1 when a city is in its heating period.  $X_{it}$  is a vector of the control variables (including wind level, wind direction, and weather type).  $\lambda t$  denotes the time trend term controlling the possible trend.  $\mu_i$  is the fixed effects of a city;  $\eta_t$  is the fixed effects of time; and  $\varepsilon_{it}$  is the error term.

To minimize estimation bias of the factors affecting air quality, we determined weather conditions and holidays as control variables based on Luo and Li [30] and Lin and Zhu [18]. Weather conditions included the highest and lowest temperatures; weather types included sunny, rainy, snowy, and cloudy. Rainfall lowers concentrations of airborne particles by transporting such particles

to the ground surface. Wind strength and direction can also influence air pollutant concentrations. Weekends and holidays are set as dummy variables, which equal 1 if the  $t$ -date is a weekend or a holiday.

After estimating the negative effects of heating on air quality, we attempted to verify whether the implementation of the WCHP project alleviated air pollution caused by winter heating. This study employed a DID framework by comparing differences in outcomes among pilot cities and non-pilot cities by means of a before-and-after comparison of the clean heating policy. Adopting the procedures of Chen et al. [27], Beck et al. [17], and Moser and Voena [31], we used the following standard DID estimation model in our study:

$$
Y = \alpha_ {0} + \gamma W C H P _ {i t} + \beta X _ {i t} + \mu_ {i} + \eta_ {t} + \varepsilon_ {i t}
$$

where  $WCHP_{it}$  indicates the WCHP policy status of a city. Specifically,  $WCHP_{it} = treated_i \times period_{it}$ , where  $treated_i$  equals 1 if a given city belongs to pilot cities and 0 otherwise.  $period_{it}$  equals 1 only after a city adopts the WCHP project during the heating period.

We focused on  $\gamma$  in this study. The consistent estimation of  $\gamma$  requires that the WCHP project is a natural experiment; namely, random selection is employed among pilot and non-pilot cities. In order to ensure that the DID model is robust, it is necessary to add some control variables that affect dependent variables. Since the selection is influenced by other key determinants of cities, deviation from the natural experiment will lead to bias of an estimator [18,30,32]. Associated covariates that were endogenous in the selection of pilot cities were identified and included in the regression to control bias. Accordingly, weather conditions, holidays, GDP, the ratio between secondary industries, income levels, and environmental expenditures were included as covariates in our DID model.

# 3.2. Data sources and description

Dependent variables in this study were used as indicators measuring air pollution, particularly AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , CO, and  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ . Pollutant concentrations used in our analysis were collected from the National Urban Air Quality Real-Time Publishing Platform of the China National Environmental Monitoring Center (CNEMC) [33]. Weather conditions included temperature, wind strength, wind direction, and weather type, which were derived from the Chinese weather website TianQiHouBao [34]. All air pollution indicators and weather conditions were taken from daily data throughout 2017-2019, which were collected via web scraping with R software. Macroeconomic data (i.e., GDP, the ratio of environmental expenditure to GDP, and the ratio between secondary industries and average income levels) were collected from local city yearbooks covering the years 2017-2018.

The experimental group consisted of 35 demonstration cities, and these cities were selected for inclusion into the WCHP project. We focused on the selection of the control group containing non-pilot cities during the winter heating period. Referring to Tan et al. [35], Lin and Zhu [18] as the precedents of environmental policy evaluation, we selected control cities that are geographically adjacent to the pilot cities but not been selected in the project. If a city is adjacent to multiple cities, all the adjacent cities will be selected into the control group. # 4. Empirical analysis

# 4.1. Negative impacts of winter heating

Table 3 shows the effects of the winter heating period on air quality. The dependent variables are the logarithmic transformations of the different air pollutants; therefore, the estimated coefficient of Heatperiod represents the percentage change of air pollutants. The estimated coefficient of Heatperiod is statistically significant at a  $1\%$  significance level. Specifically, the winter heating policy resulted in increases in AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , and CO by  $26.3\%$ ,  $41.7\%$ ,  $26.1\%$ ,  $19.2\%$ ,  $8.8\%$ , and  $24.4\%$ , respectively, during the winter heating period. Obviously,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  emissions in northern cities during heating periods were most severe. Our empirical findings therefore indicated that winter heating worsened the air quality of northern cities, which was consistent with existing research. However, our estimation results were significantly higher than those of previous studies. For instance, Li and Cao [36] found that  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ , CO,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  concentrations increased by  $14.73\%$ ,  $26.79\%$ ,  $20.85\%$ ,  $30.45\%$ , and  $74.10\%$ , respectively, during heating periods based on data from 2013 to 2015. Wang et al. [37] employed RDD to conclude that heating led to increases in AQI by  $10.4\%$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$  by  $9.77\%$ ,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  by  $17.25\%$ , CO by  $9.84\%$ ,  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  by  $5.23\%$ , and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  by  $17.1\%$ , respectively. Obviously, many studies have shown that increases in  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  and  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  are always significantly higher compared to CO and  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  [38,39]. It is noteworthy that these different estimation results can be attributed to the sample data and research design utilized in our study. First, most sample cities were from "TTS" cities, which suffered severe air pollution. Second, the timespan used in our study was less than that in other studies, which could affect calculation results.

With respect to weather factors, the highest temperature increased air pollutant concentrations, while the lowest temperature had the opposite effect. Compared to sunny days, rainy and snowy days improved air quality, while cloudy days facilitated increases in air pollutants. Pollutant concentrations increased during holidays compared to non-holidays (working days), which could be caused by increases in the number of motor vehicles on roads used by people taking private journeys. The coefficients of AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{10}$ , and  $\mathrm{NO}_2$  were significant, but  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , and CO were less impacted during holidays. Additionally, the coefficient of the trend term was significantly negative at a  $1\%$  level, which may indicate that measures controlling air pollution have made some progress and air quality in winter has gradually improved.

# 4.2. Reduction effects derived from the WCHP project

Table 4 provides the pollutant reduction effects under the WCHP project using the DID model. As a weighted-average indicator of primary air pollutants, AQI can objectively represent the degree of air pollution [40].  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  had the highest percentage increment derived from scattered coal combustion during winter heating periods.  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and  $\mathrm{NO_x}$  were the main components of acid rain. Hence, we selected AQI,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$ ,  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , and  $\mathrm{NO_x}$  as explanatory variables. Results showed that AQI significantly increased (42.79) during periods when heating was employed, while the WCHP project decreased AQI by 8.72, offsetting approximately  $20.38\%$  of the negative impacts caused by winter heating. Likewise,  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentrations decreased by  $18.59\%$  due to the WCHP project. This finding indicated that the WCHP project has had a significant reduction effect on air pollution during winter heating periods, particularly for  $\mathrm{SO}_2$ , since  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  emissions decreased significantly by  $68.36\%$ . # 5. Robustness checks

# 5.1. Parallel trend test

One important precondition of the DID method is that in the absence of an external shock (i.e., the WCHP project in our DID models), the time trend in pollutant concentrations in pilot cities should parallel that of non-pilot cities prior to treatment. Specifically, if similar time trends existed for long periods prior to treatment and diverged after treatment, this could potentially indicate that differential trends may be caused by the treatment itself. Therefore, this study further tested whether the validity of a hypothetical common trend between the control group and the

treated groups was satisfied during periods prior to winter heating. To determine this, we applied an event study approach similar to that applied by Jacobson et al. [45] and Chen et al. [46], when taking into consideration the following model:

$$
\begin{array}{l} A Q I = \alpha + \sum_ {n = 0} ^ {1 5} L _ {n} \cdot \text {H e a t p e r i o d} _ {i, n + 1} + \sum_ {n = 0} ^ {1 5} E _ {n} \cdot \text {H e a t p e r i o d} _ {i, n - 1} + \eta_ {t} \\ + \varepsilon_ {i t} \\ \end{array}
$$

where  $Heatperiod_{i,n-1}$  is the set of dummy variables, which represents whether the  $i$ -th city is in a heating period at  $n-1$ -day.  $L_n$  represents a series of estimated coefficients 15 days prior to the start time of heating, and  $E_n$  represents a series of estimated coefficients 15 days following the start time of heating. With the omitted time category being the start time of heating,  $L_n$  captures the differences in pollutant indicators between pilot and non-pilot cities relative to differences just prior to the launch of the policy. Here we selected AQI as the air pollutant proxy. Under such specifications, if the signs of the estimated  $Heatperiod_{i,n-1}$  coefficients differ, it can be assumed that changes in pollutant concentrations are not significant.

As Fig. 4 shows, the 15 day estimator prior to and following the start of heating varied around a value of zero (0) devoid of a clear trend, and most coefficients were not statistically significant. This result indicated a similarity between pilot cities and non-pilot cities regarding AQI. Despite an abnormal increase 4-6 days following the start time of heating, such differences decreased to a negative value (as seen 2 days prior to the start time of heating), and this trend maintained for the ensuing two weeks. These results were consistent with the hypothesis that the WCHP project generated differential trends in pollutant concentrations between pilot and non-pilot cities. It is noteworthy that the period prior to the start time of heating was implemented and continued for 3-4 days in some cities prior to the formal start time of heating, which may have resulted in the early decrease. Additionally, we also estimated the Heatperiod coefficient 30 days following the start time of heating, which also indicated that effects of the WCHP project existed following the start time of heating.

# 5.2. Placebo test

As a second robustness check, we constructed a counterfactual test to check whether our results could be driven by other unobserved factors. In doing so, we selected a different date during the non-heating period (May, June, July, August, September, and October) in lieu of the real start date of heating, specifying the interaction term between the treatment variable and the pseudoheating start time variable as Pseudo-HCP. The WCHP project can only take effect during the heating period, while the pseudo-HCP coefficient can reflect the impact of the WCHP project during the non-heating period. If estimated coefficients of pseudo-WCHP are significant and have the same sign as of the true WCHP shown in Table 4, we can conclude that air quality improvements in pilot cities could not be mainly attributed to the WCHP project, and the robustness of the DID model would be inadequate. In this case, improvements may be related to other pollution control policies, such as production restrictions and limits to the number of private vehicle allowed on roads.

According to the regressions shown in Table 5, only when the start time of heating was specified in October, coefficients of pseudo-WCHP were significantly negative at a  $1\%$  level, while other estimation results were not significant or had a positive sign. Compared to Table 4, these conflicting results failed to reject the validity of the "WHCD" policy. That is to say, the effect of the WCHP project was evident. In fact, we specified the heating start time on the 15th of November in consideration of the "official" start time of central heating while ignoring differences caused by scattered coal combustion. In October, some rural areas in the pilot and non-pilot cities began to burn scattered coal for heating, which could cause an evident increase in pollution emissions [47,48].

Moreover, the WCHP project led to significant improvements in the air quality of the pilot cities. Based on our findings on air quality improvements derived from the energy transition initiative, we proposed several policy recommendations.

On the one hand, even though the WCHP project has proven to be an effective mean to control winter-derived heating emissions, the cost of this project should not be ignored. The conversion cost of "coal-to-gas/electricity" is higher and includes a subsequent increase in the cost of gas. Replacing unprocessed coal with clean coal can be used as a transitional method in the ultimate goal of energy transition for regions that experience considerable financial governmental pressure and low residential income. The government should focus on reforming the cost of electricity/gas by updating to more advanced heating technology and devices. On the other hand, since the reduction effect of the WCHP project on regional air quality varies under different development levels and resource distributions, project control should be delegated to regional governments based on local features rather than following the outdated pattern of "one-size-fits-all". In order to minimize the negative impact of clean heating policies on energy poverty and energy consumption inequality, the government should provide subsidies to clean energy more affordable to low-income groups.

There are two limitations in this study. First, lack of mid- and long-term monitoring data will hinder the assessment of the longterm effects of this policy. Second, although pollutant concentrations are characterized by daily scale data, macroeconomic variables are characterized by annual scale data. Estimation bias may thus occur due to such unmatched scale data.

# ABSTRACT

Oxygenated volatile organic compounds (OVOCs) are important precursors and intermediate products of atmospheric photochemical reactions, which can promote the formation of secondary pollutants such as ozone  $(\mathrm{O}_3)$  and secondary organic aerosol (SOA). However, there have been few studies on the sources of and long-term variation in ambient OVOCs. This study combined sensitive, near real-time measurements of VOCs by proton transfer reaction-mass spectrometry (PTR-MS) with an improved photochemical age parameterization method to quantify daytime sources of OVOCs in an urban atmosphere in China from 2014 to 2019, permitting the observation of the impacts of emission control strategies that were implemented during this period. Temporal variation in six key OVOCs (methanol, acetaldehyde, acetone, methyl ethyl ketone (MEK), formic acid, and acetic acid) were observed. The sum of concentrations of OVOCs was averagely  $13\%$  higher during the dry season (November to April), when winds transported polluted air masses to Shenzhen from the continent, than during the wet season, and peak diurnal levels occurred during the daytime year-round due to photochemical production and higher daytime anthropogenic emissions. The average dry season concentration of OVOCs declined from a peak of 30.3 ppb in 2015 to 18.7 ppb in 2019. The results of source apportionment showed that primary anthropogenic sources contributed the most to methanol, MEK, and acetic acid  $(32 - 51\%)$ ; the dominant sources of acetaldehyde and formic acid were both primary and secondary anthropogenic sources; and biomass burning contributed a small fraction  $(5 - 11\%)$  to the six OVOCs. From 2014 to 2019, contributions from primary anthropogenic sources of OVOCs decreased significantly by  $50 - 60\%$  due to intensive pollution control measures in Shenzhen, whereas pollution control measures had no observable impact on secondary OVOCs, indicating their formation was not limited by availability of their primary VOC precursors.

# 1. Introduction

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are important precursors that can undergo atmospheric photochemical reactions with nitrogen oxides  $\left(\mathrm{NO}_{\mathrm{x}}\right)$  under ultraviolet radiation to produce secondary products such as ozone  $\left(\mathrm{O}_{3}\right)$  and peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN) and form photochemical smog (Derwent et al., 2003; Seinfeld and Pandis, 2016). Oxygenated volatile organic compounds (OVOCs) are a class of VOCs with oxygen-containing functional groups, mainly composed of aldehydes, ketones, and alcohols. The concentrations of atmospheric OVOCs vary greatly in different regions. Studies in clean marine areas showed that although phytoplankton in the marine may release OVOCs, such as methanol, acetaldehyde and acetone, their ambient air mixing ratios were usually only a few

to a few hundred pptv (Galbally et al., 2007; Colomb et al., 2009; Wohl et al., 2020), while the concentrations of OVOCs in polluted areas can be as high as dozens of ppbv (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2016; Sheng et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2019; Maji et al., 2020). The variation in OVOC concentrations is due to the wide variety and complexity of sources of OVOCs. OVOCs can be directly emitted by anthropogenic sources, e.g., solvent volatilization, vehicle emissions (Legreid et al., 2007; Ou et al., 2015), and biogenic sources (Jacob et al., 2002; Mellouki et al., 2015). In addition, OVOCs can also be formed as secondary products by photochemical oxidation of nonmethane hydrocarbons (NMHCs) from anthropogenic and biogenic sources (Yuan et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2015).

Previous studies have proven that some low-carbon OVOCs (formaldehyde and acetaldehyde) can generate OH radicals and hydroperoxyl radicals  $\mathrm{(HO_2)}$  through photolysis to affect the oxidizing capacity of the atmosphere (Volkamer et al., 2010; Shao et al., 2011; Xue et al., 2016). OVOCs can also participate in photochemical reactions and contribute to the generation of  $\mathrm{O_3}$  and SOA (Louie et al., 2013; Ehn et al., 2014). In addition, as most OVOCs are highly water soluble, they can be absorbed into the human body, irritating the eyes, skin and respiratory tract, thus harming human health. OVOCs such as formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, acetone and acrolein have been shown to pose serious health risks (WHO, 2000, Delfino et al., 2003; Bashkin, 2009; Du et al., 2014). Given the important role of OVOCs in photochemical reactions and the complexity of their sources, OVOCs have been the subject of extensive research recently (Sahu et al., 2017; Maji et al., 2020), especially in China, where air pollution is severe (Zhang et al., 2012, 2015; Zhou et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019). Zhang et al. (2012) studied the pollution characteristics of BTEX (benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, m,p-xylene and o-xylene) and carbonyl compounds during summer in Beijing and found that the  $\mathrm{O_3}$  formation potential of carbonyl compounds  $(166.1~\mu \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{m}^3)$  was approximately 2.5 times that of BTEX  $(65.4~\mu \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{m}^3)$ . Zhou et al. (2019) used proton transfer reaction mass spectrometry (PTR-MS) to measure VOCs in Xi'an and used the positive matrix factorization (PMF) model to analyze over 30 types of VOCs and identify eight primary sources and found that local emissions, such as industrial processes, solvent usage, residential biofuel use and vehicular exhaust, contributed about  $70\%$  of the VOCs measured. Zhu et al. (2019) used PTR-MS to monitor OVOCs during different seasons and applied photochemical age parameter analysis to evaluate the sources of OVOCs in Shenzhen. They found that primary anthropogenic sources were the most important source of OVOCs in urban areas of China.

Shenzhen is located in China's Pearl River Delta (PRD) urban agglomeration, a region where rapid urbanization and industrialization have led to serious, complex atmospheric pollution (Sin et al., 2001; He et al., 2011). With the implementation of strict environmental protection measures in recent years, the  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  concentration in Shenzhen has decreased significantly. From 2013 to 2019, the average value of  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  in Shenzhen was reduced from 40 to  $24~\mu \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{m}^3$  (Ecological Environment Bureau of Shenzhen Municipality, 2019). However, due to the high temperatures and high solar radiation characteristics of this region, atmospheric photochemical reactions are active, and the concentration of  $\mathrm{O_3}$  was increasing at an average annual rate of 0.7 ppb from 2014 to 2018 (Wang et al., 2020), such that  $\mathrm{O_3}$  is replacing  $\mathrm{PM}_{2.5}$  as the primary atmospheric pollutant in the region (Huang et al., 2019). Several previous short-term studies have observed the diurnal and seasonal patterns of OVOCs and identified their sources in this region (Liu et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2012; Louie et al., 2013; Han et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019); however, this is the first long-term study of OVOCs that permits the observation of interannual variability and long-term changes, in particular those related to emission reduction measures.

# 2.1. Site description and sampling period

As presented in Fig. 1, Shenzhen is located in the PRD of Guangdong Province, China. The sampling site for this study was located on the campus of the Shenzhen Graduate School of Peking University (PKUSZ,  $22.60^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$ ,  $113.97^{\circ}\mathrm{E}$ ) in western Shenzhen, which is predominantly surrounded by green areas, teaching buildings and apartments. No evident anthropogenic emission sources of air pollutants surrounded the sampling site, except a highway approximately  $100\mathrm{m}$  away. Shenzhen belongs to the subtropical marine monsoon climate zone. # 2.2. Measurement of VOCs with PTR-MS

During the sampling period, VOC data were obtained using a high-sensitivity proton transfer reaction quadrupole mass spectrometer (PTR-Quadrupole-MS) produced by Ionicon Analytik, Austria. PTR-MS utilizes proton transfer from  $\mathrm{H}_3\mathrm{O}^+$  to VOC species (R) and then uses mass spectrometry to achieve rapid quantification of protonated VOC ions  $(\mathrm{RH}^{+})$  (Lindinger et al., 1998). The instrument can measure a large group of OVOC species, including alcohols, aldehydes, ketones and organic acids; it helps monitor the rapid change processes of the active OVOC components in the atmosphere and plays an important role in promoting the understanding of the emissions, transport, chemical transformation and source contribution of atmospheric OVOCs (de Gouw and Warneke, 2007., Yuan et al., 2017). It should be pointed out that PTR-MS cannot distinguish species with the same mass-to-charge ratio, which will lead to measurement errors (Yuan et al., 2017). We conducted a brief assessment of this error and showed that formic acid, MEK and C8 aromatics measured by PTR-MS were overestimated by approximately  $22\%$ $32\%$  and  $23\%$  respectively, as detailed in the supporting information and Figure S3.

The instrument was installed on the roof of a four-story teaching building at Peking University's Shenzhen Graduate School, and the sampling head was located approximately  $20\mathrm{m}$  above the ground. The sampling line uses a Teflon tube with an outer diameter of  $1/4$  inch during sampling. A particulate filter was added to the front end of the inlet, the flow rate of which was maintained at approximately  $200\mathrm{ml/min}$ . An assessment of the inlet loss of VOC species was detailed in our previous paper, and the results show that the inlet loss of most VOC species was less than  $5\%$ , except for the species with larger stickiness, such as formic acid and acetic acid

$(\sim 10\%)$  (Zhu et al., 2018). Considering that the inlet loss of each VOC species was not large enough, the data of VOCs have not been corrected in this study. During online monitoring, background signals were detected for 30 out of every 300 scan cycles. The background values were measured by passing ambient air through a platinum-coated wool trap at  $360^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  to remove VOCs from the ambient air without changing the water content before entering the PTR-MS measurement system. The real VOC response signals were obtained by subtracting the background signals from the atmospheric signals of the samples. PTR-MS was performed in multiple ion detection (MID) mode during the observation period, and each cycle scanned multiple selected ions, taking approximately 30 s (Han et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019), and 1-h averaged data were used for data analysis. The set values for the main instrument parameters during the observation period are shown in Table S2. In addition, the concentration of ambient  $\mathrm{O}_3$  was measured by a  $49\mathrm{iO}_3$  analyzer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).

# 2.3. PTR-MS calibration and MDLs

In this study, the PTR-MS was calibrated every 5–7 days, and the measured VOC species were calibrated using two different standard gases. The first standard gas was cylinder standard gas (Air Environmental Inc., Denver, Colorado, USA), which contained 63 VOC gases, including olefins, aromatics, halogenated gases, and OVOCs. The standard gas was generally prepared according to the target compound of the EPA TO15 method, hereinafter referred to as TO15 standard gas. In this study, VOC species demarcated by the TO15 standard gas included methanol, acetonitrile, acetaldehyde, acetone, isoprene, methyl vinyl ketone (MVK)+methacrolein (MACR), butanone, benzene, toluene, styrene, and C8 and C9 aromatic hydrocarbons. The second standard gas comprised four types of permeation tubes, including formic acid, acetic acid, monoterpenes and dimethyl sulfide (DMS), produced by the company VICI (United States). Using a Dynacalibrator230 dynamic standard gas generator to set different concentration gradients, sensitivity calibration of the above five VOC species was achieved. Table S3 shows the changes in calibration factors of each species during the observation period. The linear correlations coefficient  $(R^2)$  of the multipoint calibrations were above 0.99. The scatter  $(\pm 1\sigma)$  of the approximately weekly calibration over the six years of sampling was about  $86\%$  (ranging from 76 to  $98\%$ ). The instrument response over the six years in this study was generally stable and did not differ systemically during the measurement periods (Figure S4), with a relative standard deviation in the instrumental sensitivity ranging from 16 to  $32\%$  for the species measured (see Table S3). The method detection limit (MDL) of PTR-MS was defined as the volume mixing ratio (VMR) that can be detected with a signal-to-noise ratio of 3, with the detailed calculation process described in previous studies (de Gouw et al., 2003a). The average MDL of the instrument ranged from 0.009 to 0.358 pptv and is given for each species in Table S4. In addition, more assessment methods for the stability, accuracy and uncertainty of PTR-MS during the sampling period can be seen in previous studies (Taipale et al., 2008; Kajos et al., 2015; Dunne et al., 2018).

# 2.4. OVOC source apportionment methodology

In previous studies, the source apportionment methods for OVOCs primarily included the multilinear regression method (Garcia et al., 2006), the chemical mass balance (CMB) model (Liu et al., 2015), the photochemical aging parameterization method (de Gouw et al., 2005; Huang et al., 2019) and the PMF model (Lau et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2019). Compared with other methods, the parameterization method based on

photochemical aging developed by de Gouw et al. (2005) has the advantage of not requiring emission inventories and considers the photochemical reactions and consumption processes of OVOCs (Han et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019, 2020). OVOC concentrations are attributed to primary anthropogenic sources, secondary anthropogenic sources, biogenic sources and the regional-scale background concentrations as described in equation (1), originally proposed by de Gouw et al. (2005), modified here with the addition of terms  $m$  and  $ER_{bb} * [Tracer_{bb}]$  as used in previous studies in this region (Wang et al., 2017, Han et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019; 2020):

$$
\begin{array}{l} [ O V O C ] = E R _ {O V O C} * \left[ T r a c e r _ {a p} \right] * \exp \left(- \left(m * k _ {O V O C} - k _ {T r a c e r _ {a p}}\right) \right. \\ [ O H ] \Delta t) + E R _ {\text {p r e c u s o r}} * [ T r a c e r _ {a p} ] \\ * \frac {k _ {\text {p r e c u s o r}}}{m ^ {*} k _ {\text {O V O C}} - k _ {\text {p r e c u s o r}}} \\ * \frac {\exp \left(- k _ {\text {p r e c u s t o r}} [ O H ] \Delta t\right) - \exp \left(- m * k _ {O V O C} [ O H ] \Delta t\right)}{\exp \left(- k _ {\text {T r a c e r} _ {a p}} [ O H ] \Delta t\right)} \\ + E R _ {\text {b i o g e n i c}} * \left(\text {i s o p r e n e} _ {\text {s o u r c e}}\right) + [ \text {b a c k g r o u n d} ] \\ + E R _ {b b} * [ \text {T r a c e r} _ {b b} ] \tag {1} \\ \end{array}
$$

In the equation, [OVOC] and  $\mathrm{[Tracer_{ap}]}$  represent the concentration of an individual OVOC and the tracer concentration in primary anthropogenic sources, respectively. In this study, they were directly measured by PTR-MS. [background] represents the regional-scale background concentration of an individual OVOC. A background concentration should be well mixed and distributed evenly in the atmosphere at a regional scale rather than an urban scale, reflecting the overall regional pollution from various sources (de Gouw et al., 2005).  $k_{\mathrm{Tracerap}}$  and  $k_{\mathrm{OVOC}}$  are the reaction rates of the tracer and an individual OVOC with OH (Atkinson and Arey., 2003).  $k_{\mathrm{precursor}}$  is the OH radical reaction rate constant of the precursor of OVOCs, which is obtained by fitting the equation.  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{OVOC}}$  is the emission factor of an OVOC relative to the tracer, and  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{precursor}}$  is the emission factor of the precursor of OVOCs relative to the tracer.  $[\mathrm{OH}]\Delta t$  represents the exposure of OH radicals, which can be calculated from the concentration of ambient isoprene and its photochemical products MVK + MACR (de Gouw et al., 2005).  $[\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{biogenic}}]$  is the emission ratio of an OVOC relative to the isoprene emitted from biogenic sources (isoprene $_{\mathrm{source}}$ ), and isoprene $_{\mathrm{source}}$  can be calculated by ambient isoprene and  $[\mathrm{OH}]\Delta t$  (de Gouw et al., 2005). The method for calculating  $[\mathrm{OH}]\Delta t$  and isoprene $_{\mathrm{source}}$  is further described in the supporting information. The parameters [background],  $k_{\mathrm{precursor}}$ ,  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{OVOC}}$ ,  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{precursor}}$ , and  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{biogenic}}$  are calculated by least-squares fit of Equation (1), and the fitted values are listed in Table S5. In part 3.3 of this paper,  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{OVOC}}$  obtained by fitting is compared with the results of the relevant emission inventory in the PRD to verify the rationality of the parameter results obtained by fitting.

When using photochemical-age-based parametric methods to analyze the sources of OVOCs, de Gouw et al. (2005) proposed the following assumptions: (1) anthropogenic emissions of OVOCs and their precursors are in direct proportion to the selected tracers of primary emissions; (2) OVOCs are mainly scavenged by reactions with OH radicals during the daytime; and (3) the biogenic source of OVOCs is proportional to the emission of isoprene. Therefore, the uncertainty of the method mainly depends on the effectiveness of the selected tracer, the assumed stable emission ratio and the simplified variability of VOC properties (de Gouw et al., 2005). In this study, some modifications were made to the equation based on the above assumptions to make the model more suitable for the

PRD and to improve the accuracy of the source apportionment. First, we chose benzene as the tracer of primary anthropogenic sources of OVOCs. Generally, CO (de Gouw et al., 2005, 2018), acetylene (Yuan et al., 2012) and benzene (Ma et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019) are commonly used tracers representing anthropogenic sources of VOCs. Anthropogenic emissions of VOCs in China are rather complicated, with industrial solvents and motor vehicle exhaust contributing significantly to VOCs. However, CO and acetylene mainly come from combustion emissions, which cannot represent well the anthropogenic emissions of VOCs in China. Compared with CO and acetylene, benzene can better reflect the anthropogenic emissions of VOCs in the PRD. For example, Zhang et al. (2013) used the PMF model to analyze the source of VOCs in the PRD and found that combustion processes and industrial emissions were the predominant sources of benzene. Second, considering the photolysis of aldehydes and ketones during the daytime, which obviously conflicts with assumption (2), we need to consider the effect of the photodegradation of aldehydes and ketones on their removal. Referring to the studies of Wang et al. (2017) and Huang et al. (2020), we introduced a coefficient  $(\mathrm{m})$  before the OH reaction rate constant  $\left(\mathrm{k}_{\mathrm{ovoc}}\right)$  to compensate for the daytime loss of aldehydes and ketones caused by photolysis. A detailed description of the calculation method of the m value is given in the supporting information. For methanol, formic acid and acetic acid, with almost no photolysis (Saunders et al., 2003), this study ignored the effect of photolysis on daytime removal (set m as 1), while the calculated m values for acetaldehyde, acetone and methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) were 1.02, 1.26 and 1.25, respectively, during the sampling period. Third, previous studies have shown that biomass burning emissions are an inevitable source of OVOCs (Yuan et al., 2010; Warneke et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2016). For example, Yuan et al. (2010) reported that biomass burning emissions in the PRD contributed  $9.5 - 17.7\%$  to nine kinds of VOCs (including methanol, acetone, and MEK), suggesting that the contribution of biomass burning to OVOCs cannot be ignored. Therefore, the last parameter  $(ER_{bb} * [\text{Tracer}_{bb}])$  in Equation (1) was added in this study to estimate the contribution of biomass burning to OVOCs.  $[\text{Tracer}_{bb}]$  represents the concentration of ambient acetonitrile, which is an excellent indicator for biomass burning and can be accurately measured by PTR-MS (de Gouw et al., 2003b). In addition, previous studies have shown that benzene mainly comes from transportation and solvent use in the PRD, and biomass burning accounts for less than  $10\%$  (Lyu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2013), so the influence of using benzene as a tracer of anthropogenic sources on biomass burning emissions can be ignored.  $\mathrm{ER}_{bb}$  is the emission factor of OVOCs relative to acetonitrile, which was calculated by least-squares fit along with  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{OVOC}}$ ,  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{precursor}}$ ,  $\mathrm{ER}_{\mathrm{biogen}}$ ,  $\mathrm{k}_{\mathrm{precursor}}$ , and [background].

It should be emphasized that because the sampling data of this study span six years, in addition to the above assumptions, one more assumption should be added: (4) during the sampling period, there was no significant change in the ratio of OVOC anthropogenic emissions to tracers (benzene) in Shenzhen. The emission reduction strategy of Shenzhen is to reduce emission sources of pollutants as a whole, such as replacing gasoline vehicles with electric vehicles and closing down or moving VOC-emitting enterprises to other areas. These measures have reduced emissions of VOCs, but the emission ratio among specific VOC species is relatively stable. Toluene/benzene (T/B) was considered a good indicator to judge the contribution of motor vehicle sources and solvent use to VOC emissions (Nelson and Quigley, 1984; Wang et al., 2002; Araizaga et al., 2013). Figure S5 shows the changes in the concentrations of benzene and toluene and T/B from 2014 to 2019. The concentrations of benzene and toluene in the six years showed a downward trend, while T/B remained at approximately 5, which also indicated

that assumption (4) was reasonable in this study. Therefore, it is reasonable for this study to fit the six-year data as a whole.

# 3. Results and discussion

A total of 17 species of VOCs were monitored in this study by PTR-MS from 2014 to 2019, including 6 species classified as OVOCs (methanol, acetaldehyde, acetone, formic acid, acetic acid, and MEK); 5 aromatic hydrocarbons (benzene, toluene, styrene, C8 and C9 aromatics); 3 classified as biogenic species (isoprene, MVK + MACR, and monoterpenes); and 2 species that, in addition to benzene, were measured as tracers (acetonitrile and DMS). The compounds, the respective  $\mathrm{m} / \mathrm{z}$  of their parent ion signal, and their detection limits are provided in Table S3. As shown in Fig. 2, the average of the sum of the VOC concentrations in the dry season over the six years ranged from 25.3 to 41.0 ppbv, with an overall 6-year average of 33.1 ppbv; for the wet season, the average value ranged from 23.0 to 39.0 ppbv, with an overall 6-year average of 29.4 ppbv. In terms of the proportion of each component, in the six years of dry and wet seasons, OVOCs (70–78%) accounted for the largest proportion, followed by aromatic hydrocarbons (16–26%), whereas the concentrations of biogenic VOCs (BVOCs) and tracers were relatively low (less than 10%). # 3.1. Seasonality of VOCs in shenzhen

In this study, the six-year average of VOCs in the dry season was approximately  $13\%$  higher than that in the wet season. As shown in Figure S7, the concentrations of OVCs, aromatic hydrocarbons and tracers in the wet season were lower than those in the dry season, while the BVOC concentration was higher in the wet season. As a highly developed urban area, Shenzhen was active in anthropogenic activities year-round, and there was no obvious seasonal difference in anthropogenic emissions. Therefore, it is more likely that seasonal meteorological differences lead to variations in VOC concentrations between wet and dry seasons. For example, the air masses passing through Shenzhen during the dry season were predominantly from northern inland regions, and the concentration of pollutants carried by inland air masses was higher than that of marine air masses (He et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2020). In addition, the dry season had a lower boundary layer and less precipitation, which was also conducive to the accumulation of pollutants. However, the concentration of BVOCs during the wet season was significantly higher than during the dry season, as shown in Figure S7 (g), (h) and (i), primarily because the higher light levels and solar radiation during the wet season led to accelerated release rates of VOCs by plants (Loreto et al., 2010). It should be pointed out that the data collection frequency in the dry season  $(\sim 73\%)$  was much higher than that in the wet season  $(\sim 35\%)$ , which may lead to relatively higher uncertainty of the conclusion for the wet season.

Then, the air mass trajectories were clustered according to the similarity of their spatial distribution to study the difference in the composition and concentration of VOCs under different air mass sources. The average diurnal variations in mixing ratios of  $\mathrm{O_3}$  and selected VOCs during the dry and wet seasons are shown in Fig. 4. # 3.3.  $O_3$  formation potential of VOCs

As VOCs are important precursors of  $\mathrm{O_3}$  in the atmosphere, studying their  $\mathrm{O_3}$  formation potential (OFP) is conducive to further exploring the key active species. For a VOC species i, its OFP can be calculated as follows:

# 3.5. Six-year trends in OVOCs in shenzhen 2014-2019

Fig. 6 shows the trends in the relative contributions to OVOCs from 2014 to 2019 during the dry and wet seasons. The OVOC source compositions during the wet season, when clean marine air masses from the south dominated the sampling site, did not exhibit a clear year-by-year trend. Previous studies have shown that atmospheric oxidizing capacity was strong in the PRD region, and the concentration of  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , one of important atmospheric oxidizing substances, was on the rise in this region in recent years, which may strongly transform VOC to OVOCs and SOA (Wang et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2018; Xue et al., 2014). # 4. Conclusions

PTR-MS was used for continuous online monitoring of atmospheric VOCs in Shenzhen from 2014 to 2019. The results revealed that OVOCs were the most abundant of 17 species of VOCs measured, comprising  $70 - 78\%$ . The six-year mean concentration for the dry season was  $33.1~\mathrm{ppb}$ , approximately  $13\%$  higher than that for the wet season of  $22.9~\mathrm{ppb}$ . There were diurnal variations in OVOC concentrations, with higher concentrations during the daytime. The diurnal variation in acetaldehyde was most similar to that of  $\mathrm{O}_3$ , and it had the largest OFP of the OVOC species, indicating that acetaldehyde was an important intermediate of photochemical reactions. The results from source apportionment based on photochemical aging parameterization revealed that contributions of OVOCs from primary anthropogenic sources exhibited an evident declining trend of  $50 - 60\%$  from 2014 to 2019 during the dry season, indicating that the intensive VOC control measures implemented in the northern inland areas of the sampling site had a significant effect on the reduction of primary emissions. However, to reduce the impact of OVOCs on air quality in Shenzhen, more efforts are needed to control OVOCs from anthropogenic secondary sources by reducing not only their primary VOC precursors but also their complex atmospheric oxidizing capacity, which will finally restrict the production of  $\mathrm{O}_3$  and SOA.

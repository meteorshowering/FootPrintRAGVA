# ABSTRACT

In China, energy use from passenger vehicles is increasing rapidly due to urbanization and income growth, but the contributions of transport service structure shift and energy efficiency improvement, especially future energy-saving directions are controversial and unclear because of the differences in the choices of traffic modes, which may greatly affect the analysis results and their interpretations. This study investigates the growth sources of energy use in the passenger transport sector during 1979-2017 and future energy-saving potentials by 2030 by re-examining statistical data. Results show that the transport service increase and transport structure shift led to the increase in energy use by  $665.25\%$  and  $337.22\%$ , but approximately  $14\%$  of the increased energy use was offset by energy efficiency improvements, and its effect is limited in public transportation sector, compared with private passenger vehicle sector. In public passenger transport sector, adjusting the service or volume structure may be a desirable option while in private passenger vehicle sector the policy focus is more on improving fuel efficiency and reducing average annual kilometers traveled per capita. Overall, there is still at least  $28.3\%$  of energy reduction potential by energy efficiency improvement before 2030 compared to 2017 baseline level. Potential evaluation showed that the current potential of transport service structure adjustment based on industrial planning is limited, which highlights the necessity of reform, involving promoting the sharing transportation, railway, subway and buses.

# 1. Introduction

In the last two decades, the transport sector has become a major source of energy consumption worldwide. In China, transport energy use increased from 58.63 Mtce (million tons of coal equivalent) in 1995 to 421.91 Mtce in 2017, with an annual average growth rate of nearly  $10\%$ . Energy use from passenger transport dominates total transport energy use, accounting for nearly  $60\%$  in 2017. By 2030, more than 300 million new rural residents are expected to enter the cities in China, which will have a great impact on the passenger transport energy consumption. Passengers are relatively easily guided by policy measures to change their energy-using behaviors in their daily travel, thus leading to low-carbon

transportation [1,2]. In recently years, the sharing of transportation has already developed quickly in China, including car-sharing, bicycle-sharing and electric bicycle-sharing. In 2017, there were 6300 car rental enterprises operating and 0.2 million sharing cars in China, growing at about  $20\%$  every year. In this context, it is critical to analyze the sources of energy growth and future energy conservation potentials, which will help to improve the understanding of energy growth trend and provide policy implications of energy conservation.

However, one of the key challenges is that the current energy statistics of passenger transportation are fragmented in China. The lack of comprehensive statistic specification causes that the data is scattered into different sectors in energy statistical system. The data does not properly cover metrics of interest for energy consumption analysis of passenger transportation. Part of the data is covered in the Transport, Storage and Post sector and Residential Consumption sector in China Energy Statistical Yearbook [3]. # 2. Passenger transport energy consumption

With accelerating urbanization in China, passenger transport energy use has rapidly increased, from 28.78 Mtce in 1997 to 252.47 Mtce in 2017, with an annual average growth rate of  $11.47\%$  (Fig. 1). Energy use could thus be divided into three development stages. From 1978 to 1990, the intercity public passenger transport was the main traveling mode of passengers, as the urban and the private vehicle subsectors were undeveloped. From 1990 to 2002, energy consumption of the private passenger vehicles gradually caught up with that of the intercity subsector. From 2003 to 2017, the private subsector became the major source of the passenger transport energy use. The energy intensity of passenger transport decreased continually due to technological advances, and the energy intensity of the private passenger vehicles decreased rapidly from 143.68 Tce/Mp-km (ton coal equivalent/million passenger-kilometers) in 1997 to 51.38 Tce/Mp-km in 2017, due to the policy of fuel consumption limits for passenger cars [48]. This corresponds to a huge reduction in fuel consumption. Without this reduction in energy intensity, the fuel consumption in the private passenger transport sector would have increased nearly three times greater than it is today.

# 2.1. Intercity public passenger transport subsector

The intercity public passenger transport consists of four modes: railway, highway, waterway, and civil aviation. The railway dominated passenger transport prior 1995, accounting for over  $50\%$  of

the total energy use. After 1995, highway and civil aviation became the main sources of energy consumption for intercity passenger transport, because the Chinese government gave priority to the highways over the railways [50]. The modal energy intensities of the other three transport modes had decreased since 1978 except the highway, whose modal energy intensity increased from 66.92 Tce/Mt-km (million tons-kilometers) to 147.00 Tce/Mt-km from 1978 to 2016, due to the improvements of vehicle accessories, comfortability, and performance.

# 2.2. Urban public passenger transport subsector

The urban public passenger transport consists of three modes: urban bus, taxi and rail transit (ferry is excluded due to its tiny proportion in total volume). The transport activity of the urban public passenger transport is measured in passenger volume in China's statistics. If an individual takes the urban bus twice, the passenger volume is measured as two passenger-trips. The unit contains no information about travel distance. The energy consumption of this subsector increased from 2.07 Mtce in 1990 to 24.20 Mtce in 2017, with an annual average growth rate of  $9.53\%$ . The passenger volume of the urban subsector increased from 12.23 Bp-t in 1978 to 127.13 Bp-t in 2017 [4]. In the 1980s, the urban bus was the main mode of the subsector and accounted for over  $95\%$  of the total passenger volume. After 1990, the taxi developed rapidly and its volume share in the total increased from  $2.5\%$  in 1990 to  $28.74\%$  in 2017. Prior to 2008, the use of rail transit was slow due to imperfect development mechanisms [51,52], but then it developed rapidly after 2008 due to the significant effects of national macroeconomic policies.

# 2.3. Private passenger vehicle subsector

Private passenger vehicles consist of four vehicle types, namely large vehicles, medium vehicles, small vehicles and mini vehicles according to the definition of the length of passenger vehicle and its seating capacity from Ref. [53]. The small and mini vehicles used in families are the main sources of increased energy consumption (Fig. 2a). With economic development in China, the private passenger vehicle population and per capita ownership has grown rapidly. From 2000 to 2017, the private passenger vehicle population increased from 3.65 million to 186.95 million (Fig. 2b), and ownership per thousand people increased from 2.88 to 134.49, both with an annual average growth rate of about  $26\%$ , but the average annual mileage per passenger vehicle has fallen by half since 1997. The energy intensities of the four types of private passenger vehicles decreased by  $37.56\%$ ,  $67.48\%$ ,  $65.62\%$ , and  $56.81\%$  for large, medium, small and mini vehicles, respectively in 2017, compared to that in 1997, due to the improvement in fuel consumption standards (Fig. 2c).

# 3. Methodology and data

Passenger transport involves complex traffic modes, including intercity public passenger transport, urban public passenger transport, and private passenger transport sectors, for which there are great differences in the impetus of energy consumption. These could be reduced to activity indicators, vehicle structure, and energy efficiency indicators; therefore, a hybrid decomposition

analysis framework is proposed to consider different characteristics of traffic modes, as shown in Fig. 3. The effects of main driving factors on total passenger transport energy use are analyzed briefly. Furthermore, a specific investigation into three subsectors of passenger transport is performed in turn.

# 3.1. Decomposition of energy consumption growth

# 3.1.1. Total passenger transport sector

The total passenger transport energy consumption is the sum of the results in the three subsectors. The total energy consumption  $(E_{\mathrm{tot}})$  can be divided into activity  $(A)$ , structure  $(S)$ , and intensity  $(I)$ .

$$
E _ {\text {t o t}} = \sum_ {i} E _ {i} = \sum_ {i} S e \times \frac {S e _ {i}}{S e} \times \frac {E _ {i}}{S e _ {i}} = \sum_ {i} A \times S _ {i} \times I _ {i} \tag {1}
$$

where.

$i$ $(i = 1,2,3)$  is the type of subsector;

$A$  ( $A = Se$ ) is the total passenger service of the passenger transport sector, which is the passenger turnover here with the unit of passenger-kilometers. It is defined as the activity of transporting a certain number of passengers for a certain distance.

$S_{i}$ $(S_{i} = \frac{Se_{i}}{Se})$  is the transport service share of subsector  $i$  to the total;

$I_{i}$ $(I_{i} = \frac{E_{i}}{Se_{i}})$  is the energy consumption per transport service of subsector  $i$ .

The energy consumption change  $(\Delta E_{tot})$  from year  $t - 1$  to year  $t$

\Delta E _ {t o t} = E _ {t o t} ^ {t} - E _ {t o t} ^ {t - 1} = \Delta E _ {a c t} + \Delta E _ {s t r} + \Delta E _ {i n t} \tag {2}

where.

# 3.1.2. Intercity public passenger transport subsector

Energy consumption  $(E_{1})$  from intercity public passenger vehicles can be divided into population  $(P)$ , GDP per capita  $\left(\frac{GDP}{P}\right)$ , passenger service per GDP  $\left(\frac{Se}{GDP}\right)$ , the transport service structure of different modes  $\left(\frac{Se_{j}}{Se}\right)$ , and the energy consumption per service  $\left(\frac{E_{j}}{Se_{j}}\right)$ .

$$
E _ {1} = \sum_ {j} P \times \frac {G D P}{P} \times \frac {S e}{G D P} \times \frac {S e _ {j}}{S e} \times \frac {E _ {j}}{S e _ {j}} \tag {4}
$$

where.

$j(j = 1,2,3,4)$  is the mode type in the intercity public passenger

transport;

$Se_{j}$  is the passenger service of mode  $j$ ;

$E_{j}$  is the energy consumption of mode  $j$

The energy consumption change  $(\varDelta E_{1})$  from year  $t - 1$  to year  $t$  is the sum of the population effect  $(\varDelta E_{pop})$ , the GDP per capita effect  $(\varDelta E_{GP})$ , the passenger service intensity effect  $(\varDelta E_{TI})$ , the passenger service structure effect  $(\varDelta E_{str})$ , and the service energy intensity effect  $(\varDelta E_{int})$ .

$$
\Delta E _ {1} = E _ {1} ^ {t} - E _ {1} ^ {t - 1} = \Delta E _ {\text {p o p}} + \Delta E _ {G P} + \Delta E _ {T I} + \Delta E _ {\text {s t r}} + \Delta E _ {\text {i n t}} \tag {5}
$$

The calculation of energy consumption changes due to factor  $x$  ( $\Delta E_{x}$ ) is similar to Eq. (3).

# 3.1.4. Private passenger vehicle subsector

For the private passenger vehicle, vehicle population is used as the main explanatory. The energy consumption  $(E_{3})$  can be divided into the number of households  $(H)$ , population per household  $(\frac{P}{H})$ , vehicle stock per capita  $(\frac{S}{P})$ , vehicle structure of different types  $\left(\frac{S_{l}}{S}\right)$ , the average annual kilometers traveled  $\left(\frac{D_{l}}{S_{l}}\right)$ , and the vehicle fuel efficiency  $\left(\frac{E_{l}}{D_{l}}\right)$ .

E _ {3} = \sum_ {l} H \times \frac {P}{H} \times \frac {S}{P} \times \frac {S _ {l}}{S} \times \frac {D _ {l}}{S _ {l}} \times \frac {E _ {l}}{D _ {l}} \tag {8}
$$

where.

$l(l = 1,2,3,4)$  is the vehicle type;

$S_{1}$  is the vehicle population of type  $l$ ;

$D_{l}$  is the total annual kilometers traveled of type  $l$ ;

$E_{l}$  is the energy consumption of type  $l$ .

# 3.2. Scenario design

In this section, we attempt to investigate underlying energy conservation path in 2020 and 2030 for one baseline scenario and four energy efficiency improvement scenarios. Scenario analysis is performed based on a bottom-up energy accounting system for calculating the energy consumption of passenger transport used in

this paper.

Scenario 1 ( $S1$ ) is the baseline scenario. In  $S1$ , the transport structure and energy intensity in all three subsectors are kept in the level of 2017. Population growth is from the No change scenario of the World Population Prospects (The 2012 Revision) [55]. The Long-term baseline projection of the OECD Economic Outlook No.95 is used for the GDP per capita [56]. The household number is projected by trend extrapolation. The vehicle stock (or per capita) in 2020 and 2030 are from the Department of Information Resource Development, State Information Center and the Tsinghua Automotive Strategy Research Institute, respectively. The future average annual mileage of private vehicles is from the FEEI model [49]. The growth rate of the service or volume in the public transport sector are both set to be  $5\%$ , according to the 13th Five-Year Plan for Integrated Transportation Service [57].

Scenario 2 (S2) is the structure adjustment scenario of the 13th Five Year Plan (2015-2020), and it is to evaluate the energy-saving potential of the structure adjustment during 2015-2020, in which railway, civil aviation, and urban rail transit are largely promoted according to policy guidelines [58,59], as shown in Table 1.

Scenario 3 (S3) is the energy intensity reduction scenario. Energy intensities decline due to technological progress until 2020 and 2030 (Table 2). Specifically, for the intercity subsector, the energy intensities of railway, highway, waterway, and civil aviation are planned to decline by  $5\%$ ,  $2.1\%$ ,  $6\%$ , and  $4\%$  during the  $13^{\text{th}}$  Five Year Plan [59-62]. For the urban subsector, the energy intensities of urban bus and rail transits are both planned to decline by  $10\%$  during the period 2015-2020 [61]. The fuel efficiency improvement of tax and private passenger vehicles are set according to the Stage IV Fuel Consumption Standard [48].

Scenario 4 (S4) is the combination scenario based on Tables 1 and 2, in which the overall energy-saving potential of shifts in traffic modes and fuel efficiency improvements are evaluated.

Scenario 5 (S5) is the enhanced scenario used to investigate further energy-saving potential beyond the 13th Five Year Plan. The service share of civil aviation is expected to decline through further promotion of the use of highway and railway transport, since passenger service structure effect played an increasing role in energy consumption rises after 1990. In addition, the miniaturization of passenger vehicles is promoted by encouraging the public to adopt mini vehicles.

# 3.3.Data

The data includes socio-economic activity, transport structure and energy intensity. The socio-economic activity data includes the population, the number of households and the GDP, which are from the China Statistical Yearbook [4]; the transport-related activity data includes the passenger service of the intercity public passenger transport, the passenger volume of the urban public passenger transport and the vehicle population of the private passenger vehicles, which are from 1) Passenger Kilometers of the Transport, Postal and Telecommunication Services, and Software Industry, 2) Basic Statistics on Public Transportation in Cities by Region of the Urban, Rural and Regional Development, 3) Possession of Private Vehicles of the Transport, Postal and Telecommunication Services, and Software Industry in the China Statistical Yearbook [4]. The structure data consists of the service structure of the intercity public modes, the volume structure of the urban public modes and the vehicle type structure of the private subsector, which are calculated based on the above transport-related activity data. The intensity data includes the energy intensity of the three subsectors and the average annual mileage of the private passenger vehicles, which are from the Yearbook of China Integrated transport [63], the Statistics bulletin of transportation sector development [64], the Railway Statistics [65], and the Fuel consumption limits for passenger cars [48]. The average annual mileage of the private passenger vehicles is from the survey data in the FEEI model [49].

# 4. Results and discussion

# 4.1. Passenger transport sector

The decomposition results are shown in Fig. 4. The energy consumption of passenger transport increased from 28.78 Mtce in 1997 to 252.47 Mtce in 2017 (Fig. 4a). It is found that the passenger service increase (passenger activity effect) and the service structure adjustment (transport structure effect) are the main drivers of energy use increase, which contribute 191.47 Mtce (665.25% of the original energy consumption) and 97.06 Mtce (337.22%), respectively, to the increase in total energy consumption, while the improved energy efficiency resulted in energy conservation amounting to 40.29 Mtce (225.3% of the 1997 total energy consumption). Thus, the transport structure effect still plays an important role in increasing passenger transport energy use, which warrants more attention. Since the activity is hard to adjusted via policy instruments, transport structure adjustment should be the focus in the next-step policy formulation. An in-depth investigation is carried out below.

# 4.2. Intercity public passenger transport subsector

As shown in Fig. 5, there are positive effects for the factors of GDP per capita, the service structure change and the population growth. These factors contribute to increases in energy consumption by 138 Mtce, 14 Mtce, and 6 Mtce, respectively. A decline of service intensity and modal energy intensity obviously reduces energy consumption by 94 Mtce and 9 Mtce respectively.

The GDP per capita effect was the dominant contributor to energy consumption growth and its contribution increased over time, but the contribution decreased during 1996-2000 (Figs. 5b) and 2008-2010 (Fig. 5c) due to the Asian financial crisis and the global financial crisis respectively. The shift of various modes of passenger transport (structure effect) contributed to the energy consumption decrease in the years leading up to 1990 because the passenger service share of civil aviation decreased (Fig. 5c). Since 1990, the structure effect turned from a negative value to a positive value. After 1990, the highway and civil aviation services were promoted vigorously by the State Council and Civil Aviation Administration of China [50,66]. Therefore, adjusting the transport structure in the intercity subsector, such as promoting the transfer form highway and civil aviation to railway, could be an effective way to save energy. The passenger service intensity effect provides an increasingly negative contribution to the increase in energy consumption during 1978-2017. The energy intensity of the railway, waterway, and civil aviation services decreased rapidly due to technological advancements and improved management.

# 4.3. Urban public passenger transport subsector

From 1990 to 2017, the GDP growth per capita, the change in the modal energy intensity, the volume structure change and the population growth all contributed to energy consumption growth in the urban public passenger transport subsector, with an energy increase of  $2623\%$ ,  $219\%$ ,  $203\%$  and  $84\%$ , respectively, but approximately  $66\%$  of increased energy use was offset by the improvement of passenger volume efficiency per GDP ( $\Delta E_{TI}$ , passenger volume intensity effect). It causes that the real increase of energy consumption is far below the theoretical value.

The GDP per capita effect was the dominant contributor to energy consumption growth due to rapid economic development in China. The most prominent contribution appeared during 1991-1995 and 2006-2010, with an energy consumption increase of  $275\%$  and  $195\%$ , respectively (Fig. 6b). The passenger volume structure effect was another main driver of the energy consumption growth due to the shift in the dominant mode of transport. From the late 1980s to the 1990s, the taxi industry grew rapidly, owing to the policy of open business implemented by the State Council

[67,68]. This transition in the volume structure from the mode of lower intensity bus to the higher intensity taxi caused energy consumption to increase. The passenger volume structure effect gradually begins to produce a negative contribution to the increase of energy consumption in recent years due to the increase of the volume share of the low-intensity rail transit (Fig. 7b). Therefore, promoting rail transit construction and guiding residents to use rail transit rather than taxes could be effective ways to reduce energy use in the urban transport subsector especially for metropolises.

# 4.4. Private passenger transport subsector

As shown in Fig. 7, the energy consumption of this subsector increased quickly from 7.48 Mtce to 169.07 Mtce during 1997-2017, with an annual average growth rate of  $17\%$ . The motorization level, changes in vehicle type structure and the number of households all contributed to energy consumption growth, with an increase of  $2725\%$ ,  $273\%$ , and  $182\%$ , respectively. The fuel efficiency improvement, the decline of average annual mileage and family size all provide the negative contribution to the increase of energy consumption decrease.

The motorization level effect was the dominant contributor to energy consumption growth from 1998 to 2017 due to the rapid popularization of private cars (Fig. 8a). With increases in people's income and growing travel demand, the private passenger vehicle ownership per thousand people in China increased from 1.55 in 1997 to 134.49 in 2017. The fuel efficiency effect provides the dominantly negative contribution to the increase of energy consumption during 1998-2017 (Fig. 8c). Due to the increasingly stringent fuel consumption limits for passenger cars, the fuel efficiency consistently increased, which explains the negative effect of the fuel efficiency effect on increases to energy consumption. However, it can still be seen that the fuel efficiency effect only offset a rather small portion of the motorization level effect to bring energy conservation. Some of the major metropolises in China have carried out restriction policies such as vehicle license rotate which comes from traffic congestion control. This kind of policies did have an obvious energy-saving effect, but also have negative effect on the automobile sales market. The household effect and the population per household effect both had small influences on energy consumption change because the growth rate of the household and family size were both less than  $2\%$ .

# 5. Comparison and outlook on energy consumption

# 5.1. Comparative analysis

The multiplication decomposition results in the three subsectors are shown in Fig. 9. Five influential factors on energy consumption can be observed and are quite similar in their effects in the intercity and urban passenger transport subsectors. The GDP per capita increase was always the dominant contributor to rises in energy consumption, but this effect was larger in the urban subsector (2.713). The passenger volume/service per GDP played a critical role in reducing energy consumption as opposed to energy intensity in the public transportation sector. In contrast, however, the private transport subsector showed that fuel efficiency improvement, decline of family size and average annual kilometers per capita offset the effect of vehicle stock growth per capita together.

# 5.2. Outlook on energy conservation potentials

According to the scenario design in Section 3.2, the energy conservation potentials in 2020 and 2030 is carried out in the

passenger transport sector. The energy consumption and energy-saving potentials under different scenarios are shown in Table 3.

Results show that up until 2020 there is only  $0.7\%$  and  $1.9\%$  of energy-saving potential from structure adjustment measures (S2) and fuel efficiency measures (S3) respectively, compared with the base year (2017). But, the potential of fuel efficiency measures increases quickly before 2030, leading to  $28.3\%$  in energy conservation compared with the base year, which is far higher than that of structure adjustment measures  $(1.7\%)$ . The main reason is that the target of energy efficiency improvement and structure adjustment in the 13th Five Year Plan (2015-2020) are not very tight and easy to accomplish. The actual level in the base year of 2017 is close to the target value, so further potential of energy conservation will be released during the period of 2020-2030.

Overall, the energy-saving potential until 2020 is very limited, reaching about  $2\%$  in total. An enhanced scenario (S5) shows that the feasible energy-saving potential could reach  $3.7\%$  in 2020 and  $31.6\%$  in 2030. Specifically, there is great energy-saving potential for urban public passenger vehicles and private passenger vehicles with  $54.3\%$  and  $34.3\%$  of energy conservation from combining shifts in traffic modes and fuel efficiency improvement.

The above results suggest that the policies of energy intensity reduction deserve to be strictly enforced, especially for the fuel efficiency standard for passenger vehicles. For urban public passenger vehicles, this is worthy especially focusing on shifts in traffic modes and fuel efficiency improvement. For private passenger vehicles,  $32.7\%$  of energy-saving potential lies in the energy efficiency improvement and vehicle structure adjustment.

# 6. Conclusions and future directions

This study contributes to understanding the underlying driving factors of energy growth and exploring energy efficiency improvement opportunities in passenger transport sector in China. First, a comprehensive picture of passenger transport energy consumption trends during 1979-2017 in China is given. Main drivers of energy growth are briefly identified through re-classifying the fragmented data statistics, which, to our knowledge, are unique and original. Second, the effects of the main driving forces of increased energy consumption are investigated firstly by using a hybrid decomposition method. Third, the future energy-saving potentials of different policy pathways until 2020 and 2030 are estimated and compared involving sharing transportation, structure adjustment, energy efficiency improvement, and urban car ownership control.

The results show that there are diversified contributors to energy consumption depending on the traffic modes utilized. On the whole, GDP growth per capita, motorization level and shifts in service structure mainly contribute to energy consumption increase in passenger transport sector during the period 1979-2017, and they cause more than 10 times increase in energy consumption, but the actual energy consumption increased just by 8.77 times because of the effect of energy efficiency improvement. For the intercity and urban public passenger transport, the GDP growth per capita, the service or volume structure change, and population growth strongly dominate the changes in energy consumption, and the effect of energy efficiency improvement is not very significant on a relative basis. In the private passenger vehicle sector, however, the situation is significantly different. Fuel efficiency improvement, decline of family size and average annual kilometers traveled per capita together offset the positive impacts from motorization level rise and vehicle type structure change. Especially, the fuel efficiency improvement has enormously contributed to energy conservation since 2010 in China.

The above results indicated that there are totally different policy implications and directions of energy conservation for public and

private passenger vehicle sectors. In public transport sector, macro-economic factors, such as GDP and population growth rates, dominate the trend of energy growth, and they are considered as exogenous variables and generally beyond the scope of industrial policy makers. In this case, adjusting the service or volume structure by policy design may be a desirable option in public passenger transport sector. In contrast, in private passenger vehicle sector, the effect of vehicle structure adjustment on energy conservation is very limited, and the policy orientation of improving fuel efficiency or reducing average annual kilometers traveled per capita deserve more attention. The results of outlook on energy-saving potentials in Section 5.2 also verify this point, where for public passenger transport sector, the main potential of energy conservation by 2030 lies in vehicle structure adjustment, and for private passenger transport sector fuel efficiency improvement owns the biggest energy-saving potential.

Outlook analysis shows that by 2030 energy conservation potential in intercity public passenger transport sector is limited (16.6%), but there are still huge energy-saving potentials in urban public passenger transport sector (54.3%) and private passenger transport sector (34.3%). They could totally lead to 31.6% of energy consumption reduction in passenger transport sector by 2030.

The transition from low energy-intensity transportation to high energy-intensity modes contributed to the rapid growth of the energy consumption. This was a result of economic development and rapid urbanization in China, especially after 1990, when the railway was eventually replaced by highways and civil aviation, and the bus was replaced by the taxi in the city.

In order to reduce energy use in passenger transport sector, multiple policy directions deserve attention. In China, the motorization level could be regulated by some mandatory policies, such as the vehicle license lottery in major cities, such as Beijing and Tianjin. In future, it is essential to give priority to public transport and sharing transport, such as the subway, bus, car-sharing, and bike-sharing. Particularly, first, sharing transportation deserves more attention in China because Chinese people are more likely to accept sharing and collective ideas, and there are less regulatory restrictions in China. Second, the energy intensity reduction targets up until 2020 need to be strictly enforced, especially for fuel efficiency standards for private passenger vehicles. Third, low-intensity transport modes, such as railway, waterway, rail transit, and mini vehicles, are strongly recommended to promote the low-carbon transition.

# Development of a database for chemical mechanism assignments for volatile organic emissions

William P.L. Carter*

College of Engineering Center for Environmental Research and Technology, University of California, Riverside, CA, USA  
*Please address correspondence to: William P.L. Carter, College of Engineering Center for Environmental Research and Technology, University of California, Riverside, CA 90521, USA; e-mail: carter@cert.ucr.edu

The development of a database for making model species assignments when preparing total organic gas (TOG) emissions input for atmospheric models is described. This database currently has assignments of model species for 12 different gas-phase chemical mechanisms for over 1700 chemical compounds and covers over 3000 chemical categories used in five different anthropogenic TOG profile databases or output by two different biogenic emissions models. This involved developing a unified chemical classification system, assigning compounds to mixtures, assigning model species for the mechanisms to the compounds, and making assignments for unknown, unassigned, and nonvolatile mass. The comprehensiveness of the assignments, the contributions of various types of speciation categories to current profile and total emissions data, inconsistencies with existing undocumented model species assignments, and remaining speciation issues and areas of needed work are also discussed. The use of the system to prepare input for SMOKE, the Speciation Tool, and for biogenic models is described in the supplementary materials. The database, associated programs and files, and a users manual are available online at http://www.cert.ucr.edu/~carter/emitdb.

Implications: Assigning air quality model species to the hundreds of emitted chemicals is a necessary link between emissions data and modeling effects of emissions on air quality. This is not easy and makes it difficult to implement new and more chemically detailed mechanisms in models. If done incorrectly, it is similar to errors in emissions speciation or the chemical mechanism used. Nevertheless, making such assignments is often an afterthought in chemical mechanism development and emissions processing, and existing assignments are usually undocumented and have errors and inconsistencies. This work is designed to address some of these problems.

# Introduction

Air quality models need to take differences in how volatile organic compounds (VOCs) react in the atmosphere when predicting effects of their emissions on secondary pollutants such as ozone or secondary organic aerosol. The chemical mechanism is the component of the models that represents how emitted compounds react and form secondary pollutants. Different mechanisms use different model species to represent how organic compounds react, with the number of species in different mechanisms varying by more than an order of magnitude. An important but often overlooked part of the air quality modeling process is determining which model species to use when representing the hundreds of organic compounds that are emitted.

Anthropogenic emissions inventories generally give organic emissions as total mass either of total organic gas (TOG) or of nonexempt (U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, 2014) volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from various sources as a function of time and space, along with codes indicating the emissions sources. These data do not, in themselves, indicate which of the

hundreds of possible types of compounds are emitted from the sources. The chemical composition information is contained in separate "speciation profiles" that are assigned to the sources, and these profiles are contained in separate speciation databases that are maintained for this purpose. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) maintains the SPECIATE database for this purpose (EPA, 2014a), the states of California and Texas maintain separate profile databases that use different categorization systems, and other profile data are available from various studies.

The data in the inventories and profiles are not sufficient for preparing model-ready emissions inputs because they do not give assignments of model species to the compounds in the profiles. In the past, these model species assignments have been in an ad hoc manner for each mechanism and profile database, and the procedures used were not always consistent and were rarely documented. Mechanism developers may give general recommendations concerning which model species should be used for which types of compounds, but such assignments are not obvious for many chemical categories in current profile databases.

Model inputs for biogenic VOC emissions are generally prepared separately from outputs from models such as Biogenic Emission Inventory System (BEIS) (EPA, 2014b; SMOKE, 2014) and Model of Emissions of Gases and Aerosols from Nature (MEGAN) (Guenther et al., 2012; Guenther, 2014). In the case of BEIS version 3, the model species assignments are made in files available from the EPA (EPA, 2014c), whereas in the case of MEGAN version 2.1 the assignments for several mechanisms are built into the code. However, these assignments and procedures for adding new mechanisms are not documented.

Assignments of model species to chemical categories in a consistent manner among models and databases and chemical mechanisms is complicated by differences in chemical categorizations in different models and databases and by the fact that many of the chemical categories they use refer to mixtures of varying degrees of specificity and complexity. Those implementing new mechanisms in air quality models can make different assumptions on how to treat these categories, yielding inconsistent treatment of mixtures with different models. Often the problem of how to represent the many hundreds of types of emitted compounds using the limited number of model species has been treated almost as an afterthought at the last stage of mechanism development, and the results are not always consistent with the intentions of the mechanism developers.

To address this problem, we developed a database of model species assignments for a number of chemical mechanisms for use with various emissions speciation databases on a consistent basis. This database and associated software was first developed and described in 2005 (Adelman et al., 2005) but has undergone a number of modifications since then, and has not been fully documented. This paper describes the current status of the database and associated uncertainties and issues, to make air quality modelers aware of this database and its utility and limitations.

Note that at present the currently supported mechanisms and profile databases are those developed for regional tropospheric modeling in the United States, although there is no reason it cannot be extended to incorporate mechanisms for other modeling applications and other emissions profile data sources as part of future updates.

This speciation assignment database is currently implemented in two Excel files, one containing the data needed to assign model species to the various chemical categories, and the other containing information about the model species used in the various chemical mechanisms. The assignment spreadsheet also includes macros to output the assignments in files that can be used by other programs for processing emissions for modeling, such as SMOKE (CMAS, 2013), the Speciation Tool (Jiminez et al., 2013), or the BEIS model (EPA, 2014a; SMOKE, 2014). Details of the files and macros and the procedures and programs for processing its output for modeling are discussed in the supplementary materials (SM) and in a users manual that is available for download with the database files and programs (Carter, 2015).

The first step in developing this model species assignment database was to compile a master list of all the chemical categories used in the all the supported databases to specify the chemical compositions of the various profiles. This master list is given in a table in the database along with separate tables giving mappings of chemical category codes used in the various speciation profile databases to the categories in the master list. Although most of the categories in the unified list refer to single organic chemical compounds, a significant number refers to mixtures of varying degrees of complexity and specificity, including many where the compositions could not be determined. The types of categories used and the numbers of categories of each type are listed in Table 3. The approach used in

this work was to directly assign model species only to the chemical categories referring to single compounds, and to estimate compositions for the other categories for the purpose of deriving the model species based on their compositions and the assignments for the single compounds. The assignments and procedures used for the different types of categories are discussed in the following sections.

# Individual chemical compound categories

The model species assignment database currently includes over 1850 chemical categories that refer either to individual organic compounds or (in about 70 cases) to mixtures of higher-molecular-weight (primarily  $C_{\geq 12}$ ) isomers that are represented in the same way in all the profile categories and the currently supported mechanisms. For simplicity, all of these are referred to as compounds in the subsequent discussion, to distinguish them from known or unknown mixture categories that are discussed separately below. The numbers of compounds with various chemical structures are shown on Figure 1, which also gives their mole fractions in a total U.S. emissions mixture, discussed below. Approximately half the compounds are hydrocarbons, approximately  $1/3$  are various oxygenates, and approximately  $8\%$  of the compounds contain nitrogen. Note that there are large numbers of types of compounds besides just alkanes, alkenes, aromatic hydrocarbons, and simple oxygenates.

For each such individual compound category, the database includes the numbers of each type of atom, the molecular weights based on these atom numbers, and the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC; 2003) atomic masses, a flag indicating whether the compound is on the EPA exempt list and therefore not reported as a VOC ozone

precursor (U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, 2014), flags indicating whether the compound is judged to be volatile and whether it is assigned model species, and the model species assignments for the various mechanisms. To aid in making model species assignments, structural information is also given for many of the compounds, using the designations shown on Figure 1.

Compounds assigned model species. Most of the compounds that were judged to be volatile and reactive were assigned model species for all the lumped mechanisms listed on Table 1. These are listed in Table S-14 in the SM, along with model species assignments for representative mechanisms used in the Community Multiscale Air Quality Model (CMAQ; CMAS, 2014). Note the distinction between "lumped" and "explicit" mechanisms in Table 1, where "explicit" in this context refers only to how emitted VOCs are represented by model species in the mechanisms, not to other aspects of the mechanism such as how reactions are represented. Lumped mechanisms are intended to represent all emitted VOCs, in most cases using lumped model species. Compounds assigned for any of these lumped mechanisms were assigned for all, to avoid differences among lumped mechanisms in how

comprehensively the emissions are assigned. Mechanisms designated as "explicit" have model species designed to represent selected individual compounds and use minimal or no lumping of emitted VOCs, so no assignment is made for a compound if the mechanism does not have a model species specifically designed to represent it. The methods used for the different types of mechanisms are discussed below.

SAPRC mechanisms. First, assignments were made for compounds represented in the detailed SAPRC mechanism for the purpose of calculating ozone reactivity scales (Carter, 1994, 2000, 2010a, 2010b), and from these the assignments for the more lumped SAPRC mechanisms were derived. Other compounds were assigned to model species to SAPRC lumped mechanisms on a case-by-case basis using chemical judgment. All of these assignments were made on a molar basis, with most being represented by 1 mole of explicit or lumped model species. Note that the author is also the developer of these mechanisms, so no consultation with the mechanism developer was required.

Carbon Bond mechanisms. For most compounds the CB4 and CB05 assignments were made based on a mechanism assignment file provided by Yarwood (2006) that was used in previous modeling work with these mechanisms, with additional assignments based on personal communications with Yarwood. New compounds were assigned by analogy to these assignments whenever possible. Note that the Carbon Bond mechanisms represent compounds on a per-carbon or per-structural group basis rather than on a per-mole basis like most other mechanisms. Representing compounds this way conserves number of carbon atoms in assignments from compounds to model species. The carbon balances were checked for all assignments for these Carbon Bond mechanisms.

RADM2 and RACM2 mechanisms. The RADM and RACM mechanisms are similar to SAPRC in that they represent compounds on a molar basis, although reactivity weighting is used for many compounds (Middleton et al., 1990; Stockwell et al., 1990; Goliff et al., 2013). In most cases, assignments could be made based on assignments of SAPRC model species, using methods that were reviewed and approved by Stockwell for RADM2 (Stockwell et al., 1990) and Goliff and Stockwell for RACM2 (Goliff et al., 2013). Where applicable, OH radical rate constants used for SAPRC detailed model species relative to those for RADM2 or RACM2 model species were used to determine reactivity weighting factors based on the approach of Middleton et al. (1990).

Master chemical mechanism (MCM). Assignments for MCM version 3.2 (MCM, 2015) were based on the list of MCM model species used by Ying and Li (2011) in their implementation of MCM in the CMAQ model and were provided to us by Qi Ying in 2013. MCM is designed to represent major emitted compounds explicitly and has no provision for representing

other compounds using lumped model species. Therefore, there are many compounds assigned model species in the lumped mechanisms that are not represented by MCM. Because of this, additional work is needed before this database can be used for processing profiles for modeling using MCM, but the assignments made thus far provide a useful starting point.

Compounds treated as unreactive. A number of compounds react in the lower atmosphere so slowly that mechanisms do not represent them using reactive model species. The Carbon Bond mechanisms use the model species "UNR" to represent unreactive carbons, but the other mechanisms do not have model species for unreactive compounds. In order to provide modelers an option to represent or track emissions of unrepresented unreactive compounds, the special model species "NROG" was added to all lumped molecule mechanisms for this purpose. This was not needed for the Carbon Bond mechanisms.

Compounds were judged to be unreactive in SAPRC, RADM2, and RACM2 lumped mechanisms if they are not represented explicitly (e.g., methane) and their OH radical rate constant is less than  $1 \times 10^{-13} \mathrm{~cm}^3 \mathrm{molec}^{-1} \mathrm{sec}^{-1}$  and they do not react significantly otherwise. Note that this may not be appropriate for global models or models representing multi-day transport, but these three mechanisms are not designed for these applications. Different criteria for "unreactive" may be more appropriate for other mechanisms and model applications.

There are certain compounds, such as siloxanes (Carter et al., 1992), aromatic isocyanates (Carter et al., 1997, 1999), and amines without  $\alpha$ -hydrogens (Carter, 2008), that are strong radical and ozone inhibitors and are not appropriately represented by any reactive model species in the currently supported lumped mechanisms. These were assigned NROG or UNR if the mechanism gives no better option.

Unreactive compounds have widely varying molecular weights, so moles of lumped unreactive compounds is not particularly meaningful. Therefore, NROG is output in mass rather than molar units. If modelers believe that it is useful to track unreactive moles, moles carbon, or individual unreactive compounds, they can add separate model species to the mechanisms for this purpose if there is not one already. These can be used in place of NROG in the assignments for these compounds and mechanisms.

Compounds treated as nonvolatile. Some compounds in the profiles were judged to be nonvolatile under atmospheric conditions and therefore not appropriately represented by gas-phase model species. These are listed on Table S-15 in the SM. Ideally, nonvolatility assignments should be determined based on knowledge or estimates of the vapor pressures of the compounds, but estimation of vapor pressures for all compounds was beyond the scope of this project. Instead, the following criteria were used to determine nonvolatility for this work: 20 or more atoms other than hydrogen or halogens;

8 or more carbon atoms and two or more acid groups; 4 or more OH or amino groups; or the compound is designated as a salt or contains Na, K, or Mn atoms.

Nonvolatile compounds are represented the special species "NVOL" that is added to all mechanisms. Assignments of NVOL emissions are given in mass rather than mole units, since nonvolatile emissions should actually be included with particle matter (PM) emissions, which are generally given as mass. For most emissions inventories, the nonvolatile species in the ROG or VOC profiles are probably not significant (see below), so output of the NVOL mass can probably be ignored. But this may not be the case in all modeling scenarios.

Unassigned compounds. Although model species assignments for lumped mechanisms were made for most of the compounds judged to be volatile, there are still a number for which such assignments were not made. This is because contributions of these compounds to the total mass of any of the existing profiles are minor, and also model species assignments for these compounds are highly uncertain or unknown. These are listed on Table S-16 in the SM. Although this unassigned mass is not significant in current profiles (see below), this may not always be the case when future profiles are developed.

The system provides two options for treatment of compounds not assigned to model species. One is simply to output molar emissions of the special species "NOASN" to account for the moles of these unassigned compounds. In order to give modelers a better option than ignoring unassigned compounds entirely, the system also allows for the option of using a standard "unassigned mixture" profile to determine a distribution of model species to represent the moles of these unassigned compounds. This is discussed in the SM.

# Mixture and other categories

As shown on Table 3, a large number of categories in current profile databases refer to mixtures rather than individual compounds, and some are not well defined. These include categories such as "C7 olefins," "Mineral Spirits," and "Unidentified." These are assigned model species based on the constituents assigned to them whenever possible, but this was not always the case. The procedures depended on the type of category, as discussed below.

Known mixtures. We could estimate compositions of most of the mixture categories in terms of individual compounds based on the descriptions of the categories or based on analyses of comparable mixtures. The master list of categories in the database indicates which of these are known mixtures whose compositions are given, and a separate table in the database gives the compositions of each of these categories in terms of the single-compound categories. Ideally, such composition estimates should be made based on knowledge of the emissions sources that were being speciated, or the analytical methods that were used, but this level of detailed analysis was beyond the scope of this project. Instead, many of the composition estimates made were quite uncertain and in many cases guesses

or arbitrary assumptions. For example, for “isomers of xylene” we assumed equal fractions of each isomer, and for “toluene; 2,2,3-trimethylpentane” we assumed equal amounts of each. In some cases, we assigned compositions using available information from profile databases or the literature, such as use of the composite petroleum distillate profile from Censullo et al. (2001) for the “petroleum distillates” category. These assignments have not been independently reviewed and must be considered to be uncertain, but they at least provide a mechanism-independent means to make consistent model species assignments for mixture categories.

The model species assignments for these known mixture categories were derived from the mass fractions of the compounds in the mixtures times the moles or mass of the model species per unit mass of the constituents. No unassigned compounds were used in compositions of any of the known mixture categories.

Unknown nonvolatile mixtures. Some mixture categories whose compositions were not estimated were judged to be nonvolatile based on their description. Examples include "C20 compounds (diesel exhaust)," "witch hazel," and "castor oil." These are represented by the same NVOL model species as used for nonvolatile compounds. Knowledge of molecular weights is not required because NVOL represents compounds on a mass basis.

Unknown volatile mixtures. A number of categories refer to mixtures that were not speciated but are judged to be volatile. These cannot be ignored because collectively they represent a non-negligible portion of the mass in many current profiles (see below). Some are unspeciated because they had descriptions such as "unidentified," whereas for others, e.g., "pine oil" and "glycols," it might be possible to estimate a composition, but this was not done because we did not have sufficient information or because of the relatively small contributions of such categories to the profiles. All of these are treated the same way for the purpose of model species assignments.

The system provides two options for treatment of unknown volatile mass. One is simply to output mass emissions of the special species "UNKN" to account for the unknown mass. This has to be output as mass fractions because molecular weights are unknown. The models can ignore this unknown mass, or track its transport, or represent it by reactive model species as determined by the modeler, or represent it by normalizing known emissions. Unknown mass is not negligible in current profiles, so ignoring it is not an acceptable option. Normalizing is not a particularly good approximation because known emissions contain large fractions of low-molecular-weight compounds such as ethene that are not representative of the (generally higher molecular weight) compounds likely to be in unspecified mixtures.

In order to give modelers a better option for treating unknown mass, the system allows for the option of using a standard "unknown mixture" mixture to determine model species to represent this unknown mass. The modeler can change

the composition of this mechanism-independent mixture if desired. This is discussed in the SM.

Poorly defined categories. Compositions could not be estimated for a number of chemical speciation categories used in profiles because they are poorly defined and should not be used in TOG or VOC profiles. Fortunately, they collectively represent only small fractions of the mass in current profiles as discussed below. These categories are treated as unknown mixtures for the purpose of making model species assignments as indicated in Table 3. For example, the category "cb4 paraffin bond" is represented in the same way as "unidentified."

Unclassified categories. # VOC-to-TOG factors

Some emissions inventories report mass of reactive organic gases as emissions of VOCs rather than total organic gases (TOG), where "VOC" is defined as any organic compound that is not listed on the EPA's VOC exempt list (U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, 2014). Examples of exempt compounds include methane, ethane, acetone, methyl acetate, a number of fluorinated compounds, and others. In order to speciate emissions reported as VOCs using profiles that may contain methane or other non-VOCs, the emissions processing systems such as SMOKE use VOC-to-TOG factors in order to convert the emissions to TOG so that TOG profiles can be used. For example, if a profile containing  $50\%$  methane and ethane on a mass basis is used to represent emissions reported as VOCs, it is assumed that the reported mass emissions do not include the methane or ethane content, so the reported emissions is only  $50\%$  of the actual emissions, so they should be multiplied by a VOC-to-TOG factor of 2 before using the assignments to derive the moles of model species based on profiles containing these non-VOCs.

The list of individual chemical compound categories in the database includes a flag indicating whether the compound is treated as a non-VOC for reporting purposes in the United States. Note that  $t$ -butyl acetate is not flagged because the regulation indicates that this compound should be treated as a VOC for reporting purposes even though it is exempt from emissions limitation regulations (it is unique in this regard). The summary information about the profiles output by the system includes mass fractions of these non-VOCs that can be used to compute their VOC-to-TOG factors, assuming that unknown mixtures do not contain exempt compounds.

However, the VOC-to-TOG factors produced by this system must be used with caution because the list of compounds treated as non-VOCs when the inventory was developed may not be the same as the list of compounds treated as non-VOCs today. A good example of this is SPECIATE 4.4 profile 3101, "Consumer Products: Underarm Antiperspirants—Non-Aerosols," which contains  $94\%$  volatile methyl siloxanes and  $0.1\%$  each of acetone and 1,1-difluoroethane, all of which are on the current VOC exempt list. However, the SPECIATE 4.4 database (EPA, 2014b) gives a VOC-to-TOG factor of 1.002 for this profile, which clearly does not reflect the contribution of its major non-VOC constituent of volatile methyl siloxanes. If this were included, the VOC-to-TOG factor would increase to 17.

# Discussion

This model species assignment database represents an improvement over the ad hoc and generally undocumented methods used previously. It greatly simplifies the process of preparing emissions for new or modified mechanisms and allows emissions to be modeled in a consistent manner using different mechanisms and profile databases. This has been used by the California Air Resources Board to process anthropogenic emissions for regulatory modeling using the SAPRC and other mechanisms, and earlier versions of this system have been used to prepare model species assignment tables for the current version of the Speciation Tool developed by the EPA for processing emissions speciation data (Jimenez et al., 2013). It has also been used in various studies of effects of modified mechanisms on model simulations (e.g., Czader et al., 2008; Heo et al., 2014).

This model species assignment system has not been used previously for assigning model species to biogenic model outputs, where existing assignments are undocumented and are inconsistent with each other and our assignments as discussed above. Although the MEGAN model in principle outputs concentrations of 150 compounds, the model species assignments are built into the code and cannot be updated using this system (Guenther, 2014). If the MEGAN code were revised to take model species assignments from a file, then the output of this system could in principle be used for all these 150 compounds, although the inaccuracy of using the estimated mixture compositions is probably negligible compared with biogenic model uncertainty.

This database, or something like it, should be used for emissions processing whenever profiles or mechanisms are updated or added. It also should be enhanced to incorporate mechanisms used in global and other types of models and emissions speciation profiles developed for other countries besides the United States and updated as mechanisms or emissions profiles are updated. Probably the most significant problem with the speciation database system discussed in this paper is that there is presently no provision for its ongoing development and support. The use of Excel spreadsheets and macros is probably not the optimum approach to maintain complex databases such as this (e.g., see Panko, 1998), but completely changing the implementation would require more support than is currently available. Ideally, maintaining such a database should be part of the larger efforts to improve emissions inventories or to develop and maintain improved modules for models, but at present this is not considered a part of either effort.

However, even if a speciation database such as this is consistently used and supported, there will remain uncertainties and incompletely resolved issues regarding speciation of organic compound emissions for modeling. These are discussed below.

# General emissions speciation issues

Probably the greatest uncertainty in making model species assignments for organic emissions concerns the accuracy and quality of the profiles themselves in representing the compounds actually emitted from various sources. This work does not address uncertainties in analyzing compositions of emissions sources, although it does address problems introduced by the use of poorly defined mixture categories to define the compositions of these sources. Since organic compounds differ greatly in impacts on air quality, it is important that the profiles be as accurate as possible, since inaccurate compositions of profiles may cause as much model error as inaccurate mass emissions.

Use of chemical categories that do not refer to individual compounds should be minimized whenever possible when chemical compositions of emissions are reported. If they must be used, ideally the profile developers should estimate their compositions, since they are in a better position to make these

estimates. If not, the mixture category descriptions should be as specific as possible, to narrow down and reduce uncertainties in compositions that need to be estimated for them. In most cases, the mixture category compositions developed in this work were made without knowledge of the nature of individual emissions sources or the analytical issues, and probably could be improved.

We recommend that mixtures be used to represent unknown mass or unassigned compounds in emissions inventories, rather than ignoring them entirely or assuming that they are the same as known or assigned emissions. The SM and Adelman et al. (2005) discuss mixtures and methods developed for this purpose. Although the appropriateness of these mixtures is uncertain, their use is better than the alternatives.

Some of the profiles in current databases contain non-negligible mass fractions of compounds or mixtures judged to be nonvolatile. The volatility judgments in the current model species assignment are uncertain, but many categories are clearly nonvolatile by any reasonable estimate. These probably should not be included in profiles intended for gas-phase emissions.

Although this database can be used to derive VOC-to-TOG factors consistent with the current U.S. list of non-VOCs (U.S., 2014), these should not be used unless it is known that the exact same list of exempt compounds were used when the VOC mass emissions were reported, because the list changes with time. It would be far better if all emitted compounds were reported so the need for uncertain VOC-to-TOG corrections can be eliminated. Most exempt compounds persist in the atmosphere and may have environmental impacts beyond ozone formation (e.g., climate change), so they should be reported in emissions inventories in any case.

# Model species assignment issues

Ideally, this database should have model species assignments for all compounds in the database and mixture assignments should be made for as many mixture categories as possible, but for practical reasons the assignments had to be made on a prioritized basis based on the importance of the categories in the profiles. The assignments are reasonably comprehensive for at least the SPECIATE 4.4 and California profile databases, but this is not the case for the Texas stationary source database and may not be the case for future profiles that may be developed or added. Additional compounds would need to be added and assigned if the MEGAN model were revised to accept input of model species assignments for all 150 compounds it calculates.

Although the documentation of most existing mechanisms includes descriptions of model species used to represent TOG emissions, there are many compounds where the best model species assignment is highly uncertain or no good options exist. We attempted to assign model species in a manner consistent with the objectives of the mechanism developer and in a consistent manner for similar compounds, but many assignments were highly uncertain. Our classifications of compounds as volatile or nonvolatile are largely arbitrary and may not reflect available volatility data or the state of the science concerning estimating vapor pressures of compounds. Even if it were possible to easily estimate vapor pressures with low uncertainty, the actual vapor pressure limit for emissions processing is not obvious and may depend on other properties of the compound such as surface affinity or hygroscopicity, or on how the compound is exposed to the atmosphere. Additional work in this area is needed.
